Uses for Benzocaine (EENT)

Oral Pain

Self-medication (alone or in fixed combination with various other drugs) for temporary relief of minor sore throat pain.<html><head></head><body><sup>114</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>124</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>126</sup></body></html>

Self-medication (alone or in fixed combination with various other drugs) for temporary relief of pain associated with various oral or dental conditions (e.g., cold sores, ulcerations, canker sores, minor injury/irritation, toothache, sore gums, orthodontic appliances, denture irritation).<html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>114</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>117</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>118</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>119</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>121</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>122</sup></body></html>

Local Anesthesia

Has been used topically for local anesthesia† of oral or other accessible mucous membranes (except eyes).<html><head></head><body><sup>125</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>127</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>128</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>129</sup></body></html>

Fixed-combination preparation containing benzocaine with butamben and tetracaine also has been used for local anesthesia prior to surgical, endoscopic, or other medical procedures, and also for suppressing the gag reflex†.<html><head></head><body><sup>127</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>128</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>129</sup></body></html>

However, methemoglobinemia has occurred and FDA states that benzocaine products are not approved for such uses.<html><head></head><body><sup>108</sup></body></html> (See Methemoglobinemia under Cautions.)

Otic Pain

Has been used topically in fixed combination with antipyrine for temporary relief of ear pain† associated with acute otitis media of various causes.<html><head></head><body><sup>123</sup></body></html> However, efficacy and safety not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>123</sup></body></html>

Cerumen Removal

Has been used in fixed combination with antipyrine to facilitate removal of excessive or impacted cerumen (earwax)†.<html><head></head><body><sup>123</sup></body></html> However, efficacy and safety not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>123</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Serious adverse effects (e.g., seizures, coma, irregular heartbeat, respiratory depression) reported following topical application of local anesthetics to skin; reported after application of extemporaneously compounded topical preparations containing high concentrations of anesthetics for cosmetic procedures and following use for FDA-approved indications.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> Use lowest concentration likely to be effective, and use in small amounts for shortest period possible; use of FDA-approved preparations recommended.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Methemoglobinemia, a rare but serious blood disorder, reported after topical benzocaine application; reported mostly with use of aerosol preparations during medical procedures (e.g., intubation, endoscopic, or bronchoscopic procedures), but also with topical application of OTC preparations to oral mucosa.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>108</sup></body></html> Fatalities have occurred.<html><head></head><body><sup>108</sup></body></html>

Most reported cases with OTC gel and liquid preparations involved children <2 years of age who received benzocaine gel for teething; however, cases also reported in adults using a gel or liquid preparation for toothache relief.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> Do not use for teething.<html><head></head><body><sup>108</sup></body></html> (See Advice to Patients.)

Manifestations of methemoglobinemia (e.g., pale, gray, or blue-colored skin, lips, or nail beds; headache, lightheadedness, anxiety, confusion, or fatigue; tachycardia; dyspnea, increased respiratory rate, or decreased blood oxygen saturation) may occur within minutes to 1–2 hours following application.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>108</sup></body></html>

Observe patients receiving aerosol benzocaine preparations for signs of methemoglobinemia during the medical procedure and for ≥2 hours after benzocaine administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html>

Do not use benzocaine preparations in children <2 years of age, and use sparingly, only when needed, and not more than 4 times daily in adults and children ≥2 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> (See Advice to Patients.)

Advice to Patients

  • Before recommending use of benzocaine preparations, inform patients or caregivers of the signs and symptoms of methemoglobinemia and advise them to seek immediate medical attention if such manifestations occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>108</sup></body></html>
