Rotigotine Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer percutaneously by topical application of a transdermal system (Neupro).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Apply transdermal system (or “patch”) once daily at a convenient time (day or night) and at approximately the same time each day.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May apply without regard to meals.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Instruct patients in the proper use and disposal of the transdermal system and advise patients to read the patient information, including the instructions for use, before beginning therapy and each time drug is refilled.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Apply transdermal system immediately after removal from its pouch and removal of the protective liner.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Apply to clean, dry, intact healthy skin on the front of the abdomen, thigh, hip, flank, shoulder, or upper arm by firmly pressing the system with the palm of the hand for 30 seconds with the adhesive side touching the skin to ensure good contact, particularly around the edges.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Avoid application sites that are oily, irritated, or damaged; also avoid sites where the patch may be rubbed by tight clothing, under a waistband, and skin folds.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If application to a hairy area is necessary, shave hair at the application site at least 3 days prior to application;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> do not use creams, lotions, oils, ointments, or powders on skin where patch will be placed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Move application site on a daily basis (i.e., from the right side to the left side, from the upper body to the lower body).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not apply transdermal system to the same application site more than once every 14 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Application Site Reactions under Cautions.)

Wear each transdermal system continuously for 24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If a system becomes dislodged during period of use or if a patient forgets to change the system, apply a new system for the remainder of the day; on the following day, change the patch according to regular time schedule.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May tape edges of the transdermal system in place with bandage tape if patient experiences difficulty with patch adhesion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

After handling a transdermal system, wash hands to remove any drug; avoid touching eyes or other objects prior to handwashing.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Because the backing layer of the rotigotine transdermal system contains aluminum, remove patch prior to magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or cardioversion to avoid skin burns.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

The rotigotine transdermal system (Neupro) is labeled in terms of the approximate rate of drug delivery per 24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

May use either single or multiple patches of rotigotine to achieve prescribed dosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Early parkinsonian syndrome: Initially, 2 mg/24 hours;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> may increase dosage weekly by 2 mg/24 hours if tolerated and additional therapeutic effect is needed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Lowest effective dosage in clinical studies was 4 mg/24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Maximum recommended dosage is 6 mg/24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advanced parkinsonian syndrome: Initially 4 mg/24 hours;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> may increase dosage weekly by 2 mg/24 hours if tolerated and additional therapeutic effect needed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Recommended dosage is 8 mg/24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

When discontinuing treatment in patients with parkinsonian syndrome, reduce daily dosage by a maximum of 2 mg/24 hours with a dosage reduction preferably every other day, until complete withdrawal is achieved.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Withdrawal-emergent Hyperpyrexia and Confusion under Cautions.)

When discontinuing treatment, reduce daily dosage by 1 mg/24 hours preferably every other day, until complete withdrawal is achieved.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Augmentation and Rebound in Restless Legs Syndrome and also see Withdrawal-emergent Hyperpyrexia and Confusion under Cautions.)

Warnings/Precautions

Monitor patients for possible adverse reactions described throughout the Cautions: Warnings/Precautions section.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If any of these adverse reactions develops, dosage reduction or discontinuance of therapy may be beneficial.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Episodes of falling asleep while engaged in activities of daily living (e.g., driving), which sometimes resulted in accidents, have been reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> Some cases occurred as late as 1 year after initiation of transdermal rotigotine therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Some patients perceived no warning signs (e.g., excessive drowsiness) and believed that they were alert immediately prior to the event; however, many experts believe that falling asleep while engaged in such activities always occurs in a setting of preexisting somnolence, although patients may not give such a history.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Somnolence commonly occurs in patients receiving transdermal rotigotine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Concurrent use of alcohol or other CNS depressants may cause additive sedative effects.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs under Interactions.)

Other new or worsening mental status and behavioral changes (e.g., paranoid ideation, delusions, hallucinations, confusion, psychotic-like behavior, disorientation, aggressive behavior, agitation, delirium), which may be severe and include psychotic-like behavior, reported during therapy or after initiating therapy or increasing the dosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Geriatric patients with parkinsonian syndrome are at increased risk for such changes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Other antiparkinsonian agents can produce similar effects.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Do not use in patients with a major psychotic disorder.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Concomitant use with antipsychotic agents may exacerbate parkinsonian symptoms and decrease its effectiveness.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Interactions for Rotigotine

In vitro studies indicate that multiple cytochrome P-450 (CYP) isoenzymes are capable of catalyzing the metabolism of rotigotine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If an individual CYP isoenzyme is inhibited, other isoenzymes can catalyze rotigotine metabolism.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Clinically important pharmacokinetic interactions mediated by CYP isoenzymes are therefore unlikely.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Metabolized by multiple sulfotransferases and 2 uridine diphosphate-glucuronosyltransferases (UGT1A9 and UGT2B15).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

In vitro data indicate no induction of CYP isoenzymes 1A2, 2B6, 2C9, 2C19, and 3A4.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Drugs Affecting or Metabolized by Hepatic Microsomal Enzymes

CYP-450 inhibitors: No risk for inhibition of CYP1A2-, 2C9-, and 3A4-catalyzed metabolism of other drugs; low risk of inhibition of CYP2C19- and CYP2D6-catalyzed metabolism of other drugs at therapeutic rotigotine concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Clinically important pharmacokinetic interactions unlikely.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Rotigotine Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Following topical application, approximately 45% of rotigotine is released from the transdermal system within 24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Following topical application of single 8 mg/24 hours doses to the trunk, average lag time of approximately 3 hours (range: 1–8 hours) before detected in plasma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Peak concentrations typically occur 15–18 hours post-dose, but can occur from 4–27 hours post-dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> No characteristic peak concentration observed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Steady-state plasma concentrations achieved within 2 to 3 days of once-daily dosing in healthy individuals.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Differences in relative bioavailability at steady state by application site ranged from less than 1% (abdomen versus hip) to 46% (shoulder versus thigh).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html>

Because administered transdermally, food should not affect absorption.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Mild to severe renal impairment (up to end-stage renal disease requiring hemodialysis): No relevant changes in plasma concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html> Exposure to conjugated metabolites was doubled in individuals with severe renal impairment (Clcr 15 to <30 mL/min) not receiving dialysis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html>

Moderate hepatic impairment: No relevant changes in plasma concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Severe hepatic impairment: Not studied.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Geriatric patients: Plasma concentrations in healthy individuals and patients 65–80 years of age similar to those in younger adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html> Exposure in geriatric patients >80 years of age not studied, but may be higher due to skin changes with aging.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Distributes into milk in rats; not known whether the drug and/or its metabolites distribute into human milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Approximately 92% to human plasma proteins in vitro; 89.5% in vivo.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Extensively metabolized by conjugation and N-dealkylation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html>

Major metabolites in urine include rotigotine sulfate, rotigotine glucuronide, N-despropyl-rotigotine sulfate, and N-desthienylethyl-rotigotine sulfate.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html>

Approximately 71 and 23% of a single IV dose excreted in urine and feces, respectively, as metabolites; less than 1% excreted as unchanged drug in urine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html>

Biphasic (after removal of patch); initial half-life of 3 hours and terminal half-life of 5–7 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of reading the manufacturer's patient information before beginning transdermal rotigotine therapy and each time the drug is dispensed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of using the rotigotine transdermal system (patch) as prescribed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of instructing patients to wear system continuously for 24 hours, then to remove the patch and immediately apply a new one.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If a patient forgets to change a patch, they should be advised to apply a new patch as soon as possible and then replace it at the usual time the following day.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of severe allergic reactions, including allergic-type reactions in patients with sulfite sensitivity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of advising patients to immediately remove the patch and seek immediate medical attention if they experience swelling of the lips or tongue, chest pain, and/or difficulty breathing or swallowing.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of advising and alerting patients of the potential for sedating effects, including somnolence and particularly the possibility of falling asleep without feeling drowsy or without warning while engaged in activities of daily living.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Patients should avoid driving or engaging in other potentially dangerous activities until effects on the individual are known.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients that hallucinations or other psychotic-like behavior can occur during therapy, particularly in geriatric patients with parkinsonian syndrome.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of symptomatic (e.g., dizziness, nausea, sweating) or asymptomatic orthostatic hypotension and syncope during therapy, particularly during initial therapy and following an increase in dosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients to avoid rising rapidly after sitting or lying down, especially if they have been in a seated or recumbent position for prolonged periods and/or if they are just beginning transdermal rotigotine therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients to contact their clinician if they experience any signs or symptoms of lowered BP during therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of increased BP or exacerbation of hypertension and increased heart rate.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of asking patients whether they have developed any new or increased urges or compulsive behaviors (e.g., gambling urges, sexual urges, uncontrolled spending, binge eating) while receiving transdermal rotigotine and of advising them of the importance of reporting such urges.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>21</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of weight gain and fluid retention, which may manifest as peripheral edema.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Patients should contact their clinician if they experience swelling or fluid retention, especially in the ankles or legs, or have an unusually fast increase in weight.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of new onset or exacerbation of dyskinesia.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients that application site reactions can occur with transdermal rotigotine and that the application site should be rotated on a daily basis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> The patch should not be applied to the same application site more often than once every 14 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Patients should report persistent application site reactions (lasting more than a few days), increases in severity, or skin reactions that spread outside the application site.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If there is a skin rash or irritation from the transdermal system, direct sunlight on the area should be avoided until the skin heals; such exposure may lead to changes in skin color.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of advising patients with parkinsonian syndrome that they are at higher risk of developing melanoma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients to monitor for melanoma frequently and on a regular basis when using transdermal rotigotine for any indication.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients that transdermal rotigotine may cause restless legs symptoms to have an earlier onset during the day or become worse.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients to remove the rotigotine patch before undergoing MRI or cardioversion to avoid possible skin burns.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of advising patients about the potential for heat application to increase rotigotine absorption from the patch.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Instruct patients to avoid applying external heat sources to the transdermal system and to avoid direct sun exposure of the transdermal system.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients that transdermal rotigotine may cause nausea, vomiting, and general GI distress (i.e., dyspepsia, abdominal discomfort).

  • Importance of following recommended procedure for administration, removal, and disposal of rotigotine transdermal systems and of reviewing the instruction sheet provided by the manufacturer.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of patient (or individual assisting the patient) thoroughly washing hands with soap and water after handling the rotigotine transdermal system (e.g., initial application, removal) and to wash the application site thoroughly after removing the system.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of proper transdermal system disposal, including keeping it out of the reach of children or pets.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription (e.g., CNS depressants including sedatives, antidepressants, and antipsychotics) and OTC drugs and herbal supplements, as well as any concomitant illnesses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients of other important precautionary information.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Cautions.)
