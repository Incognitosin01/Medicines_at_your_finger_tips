Administration

Administer by nasal inhalation using a metered-dose pump spray.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Shake inhaler gently immediately prior to use.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html>

Tilt the head slightly forward, insert the spray tip into one nostril, and point the tip toward the back of the nose.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Pump the drug into one nostril while holding the other nostril closed and concurrently inspire through the nose.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Repeat this procedure for the other nostril.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Budesonide (EENT)

Contraindications

  • Known hypersensitivity to budesonide or any ingredient of the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Known hypersensitivity to budesonide or any ingredient of the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Possible corticosteroid withdrawal symptoms (e.g., joint pain, muscular pain, lassitude, depression), acute adrenal insufficiency, or severe symptomatic exacerbation of asthma or other clinical conditions if prolonged systemic corticosteroid therapy is replaced with topical corticosteroid therapy; careful monitoring recommended.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use particular caution in patients with associated asthma or other conditions that may be exacerbated by too rapid a reduction in systemic corticosteroid dosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Increased susceptibility to infections in patients who are taking immunosuppressant drugs.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Certain infections (e.g., varicella [chickenpox], measles) can be serious or even fatal in such patients, particularly in children.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Exposure to varicella and measles should be avoided in previously unexposed patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If exposure to varicella (chickenpox) or measles occurs in susceptible patients, consider administering varicella zoster immune globulin (VZIG) or immune globulin (IG) respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html> Consider treatment with an antiviral agent if varicella develops.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Rarely, immediate or delayed hypersensitivity reactions may occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html> Wheezing reported very rarely.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Possible growth suppression in children and adolescents.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html> (See Pediatric Use under Cautions.)

Excessive intranasal dosages or use in patients who are particularly sensitive to corticosteroid effects may increase risk of systemic corticosteroid effects (e.g., hypercorticism and adrenal suppression).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

To minimize the systemic effects, titrate dosage to the lowest possible effective level;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> avoid use of higher than recommended dosages.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If systemic effects occur, slowly reduce dosage and discontinue drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Rarely, localized candidal infections of the nose and/or pharynx have been reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Local treatment of such infections and/or discontinuance of intranasal therapy may be required.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Rarely, nasal septum perforation and increased intraocular pressure (IOP) have been reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Periodically examine nasal passages for mucosal changes during long-term therapy (several months or longer).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use not recommended in patients with recent nasal septal ulcers, nasal surgery, or nasal trauma until healing has occurred.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use with caution, if at all, in patients with clinical or asymptomatic Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection of the respiratory tract; in untreated fungal, bacterial, or systemic viral infections; or ocular herpes simplex infections.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Category B.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Use during pregnancy may result in hypoadrenalism in infants;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> monitor these infants carefully.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Intranasal corticosteroids may reduce growth velocity in pediatric patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Routine monitoring of growth (e.g., via stadiometry) recommended.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Titrate dosage to the lowest possible effective level.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Budesonide (EENT) Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Relatively well absorbed following intranasal inhalation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Following intranasal administration, approximately 34% of a dose reaches systemic circulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Mean peak plasma budesonide concentrations are attained in about 0.7 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Nasal symptoms improve within 8–12 hours following initiation of therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> About 50–66% of the total symptomatic relief is evident within 1–2 days after treatment initiation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May require about 2 weeks of therapy for optimum effectiveness.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Children have plasma drug concentrations approximately twice those observed in adults due to differences in weight.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Distributes into red blood cells.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Not known whether the drug crosses the placenta or distributes into breast milk; however, other corticosteroids are distributed into breast milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

85-90%.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Little to no binding to glucocorticosteroid binding globulin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Rapidly metabolized to metabolites of low corticosteroid potency by CYP3A4.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Excreted in urine (66%) and feces (33%) as metabolites.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

2–3 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Hepatic impairment may affect elimination of corticosteroids by increasing the systemic availability of oral budesonide.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Intranasally applied budesonide not studied in such patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
