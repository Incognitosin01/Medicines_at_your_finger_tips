Uses for Ivermectin (Topical)

Pediculosis

Topical treatment of pediculosis capitis (head lice infestation) in adults and children ≥6 months of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html>

AAP and others usually recommend topical treatment with OTC preparation of permethrin 1% or pyrethrins with piperonyl butoxide for initial treatment; other topical pediculicides (e.g., malathion 0.5%, benzyl alcohol 5%, spinosad 0.9%) recommended if OTC preparations ineffective or permethrin or pyrethrin resistance suspected.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html> Oral ivermectin recommended as an alternative for infestations not responding to or resistant to topical agents.<html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html>

Ivermectin (Topical) Dosage and Administration

General

  • To avoid reinfestation or transmission of lice, most experts recommend that clothing, hats, bed linen, and towels worn or used by infested individual during the 2 days prior to treatment be decontaminated (machine-washed in hot water and dried in a hot dryer).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

  • Items that cannot be laundered can be dry-cleaned or sealed in a plastic bag for 2 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

  • Decontaminate combs, brushes, and hair clips used by infested individual by soaking in hot water (>54°C) for 5–10 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

  • Thoroughly vacuum car seats, upholstered furniture, and floors of rooms inhabited by infested individual.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> Fumigation of living areas not necessary.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

  • Evaluate other family members and close contacts of infested individual and treat if lice infestation present.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> Some clinicians suggest treating family members who share a bed with infested individual, even if no live lice found on this family member.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> Ideally, treat all infested household members and close contacts at same time.<html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

  • A fine-toothed or nit comb may be used to remove any remaining nits (eggs) or nit shells from hair.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> Some clinicians do not consider nit removal necessary since only live lice can be transmitted, but recommend it for aesthetic reasons and to decrease diagnostic confusion and unnecessary retreatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> Other clinicians recommend removal of nits (especially those within 1 cm of scalp) to decrease risk of reinfestation since no pediculicide is 100% ovicidal and potentially viable nits may remain on hair after treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> Although many schools will not allow children with nits to attend, AAP and other experts consider these no-nit policies excessive.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html>

Ivermectin (Topical) Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

In 20 pediatric patients (6 months to 3 years of age) with head lice infestation, mean peak plasma concentration following single topical application of ivermectin 0.5% lotion was 0.24 ng/mL (lower limit of quantitation 0.05 ng/mL).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Following topical application, not known whether distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Systemically absorbed ivermectin metabolized in liver, principally by CYP3A4, and eliminated almost exclusively in feces.<html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

20–25°C (may be exposed to 15–30°C); do not freeze.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of avoiding contact with eyes; if contact occurs, gently flush eyes with water.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
