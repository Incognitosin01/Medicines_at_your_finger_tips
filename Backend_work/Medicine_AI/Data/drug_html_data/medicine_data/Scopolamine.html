Introduction

Antimuscarinic; naturally occurring tertiary amine.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Uses for Scopolamine

Motion Sickness

Prevention of motion-induced nausea and vomiting; considered most effective drug for this use.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Oral or IM administration usually reserved for patients exposed to short periods of intense motion or those highly susceptible to motion because these routes have short duration of effect and high incidence of adverse effects.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Transdermal administration is effective and has fewer adverse effects and increased duration compared with oral administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Postoperative Nausea and Vomiting

Used transdermally for prevention of nausea and vomiting associated with recovery from anesthesia and surgery, but efficacy is equivocal.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>114</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>117</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>118</sup></body></html>

Surgery

Has been used preoperatively to inhibit salivation and excessive respiratory tract secretions, but use of general anesthetics (e.g., thiopental [no longer commercially available in the US], halothane) that do not stimulate secretions has reduced the need for this use.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Used preoperatively and in obstetrics in conjunction with analgesics or sedatives to produce tranquilization and amnesia; however, benzodiazepines may be preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Has greater sedative, antisecretory (e.g., on respiratory secretions), and antiemetic effects than atropine;<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> however, less effective than atropine in preventing intraoperative cholinergic effects (e.g., cardiac arrhythmias, hypotension, bradycardia).

Parkinsonian Syndrome

Has been used for symptomatic treatment of parkinsonian syndrome; however, antimuscarinincs generally have been replaced with dopaminergic drugs.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Scopolamine Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer orally or by IM, direct IV, or sub-Q injection; also may administer percutaneously by topical application of a transdermal system (Transderm Scop).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

When administered orally or IM for the prevention of motion sickness, generally administer 1 hour (range: 0.5–1.5 hours) before anticipated exposure to motion.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

When used preoperatively, administer 30–60 minutes prior to the anticipated time of induction of anesthesia or at the same time other preanesthetic medications (e.g., opiates, sedatives) are administered, since scopolamine may cause behavioral changes in patients with pain or anxiety.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> (See CNS Effects under Cautions.)

To prevent motion sickness, apply at least 4 hours (e.g., 4–24 hours) before anticipated exposure to motion.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

To prevent postoperative nausea and vomiting, apply the evening before scheduled surgery.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

For use in cesarean section, apply 1 hour prior to surgery to minimize exposure of the fetus to the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Transdermal system should not be cut; only one transdermal system should be worn at any time.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Prior to administration, wipe area behind the ear with a clean, dry tissue to ensure it is dry.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

To expose adhesive surface, peel and discard the clear plastic protective strip prior to administration; avoid finger contact with exposed adhesive layer to prevent contamination of the fingers with scopolamine.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Apply to dry, hairless area of skin behind the ear (postauricular) by firmly pressing the system with the adhesive side touching the skin.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

If the system becomes dislodged during the intended period of use (up to 72 hours), remove it and replace with another system at a different postauricular site.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Generally not affected by limited exposure to water (e.g., during bathing or swimming).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Possible adverse CNS effects, including CNS depression, manifested as drowsiness, euphoria, amnesia, fatigue, and dreamless sleep; disorientation; confusion; memory disturbances; and dizziness.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Excitement, restlessness, hallucinations, or delirium may paradoxically occur, especially when scopolamine is used in the presence of severe pain.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> May result in impairment of performance of activities requiring mental alertness, physical coordination, or visual acuity (e.g., operating machinery, driving a motor vehicle).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Use caution with underwater sports participation; warn patients about possible disorientation.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Use with caution in patients with autonomic neuropathy.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Possible increased intraocular pressure; monitor open-angle glaucoma therapy and adjust as necessary.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Excessive susceptibility to the effects of scopolamine occurs rarely.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> Toxic symptoms may occur with therapeutic doses.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Most serious idiosyncratic reaction is acute toxic psychosis (e.g., confusion, agitation, rambling speech, hallucinations, paranoid behavior, delusions).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> Other manifestations may include marked CNS disturbances (e.g., complete disorientation, active delirium), somnolence, dilated pupils, accelerated pulse rate, and dryness of the mouth with a husky quality of the voice.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Idiosyncratic reaction usually is reversed by physostigmine.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Possible drug withdrawal symptoms (e.g., nausea, vomiting, headache, dizziness, disturbances of equilibrium) following discontinuance of the transdermal system; usually do not appear until ≥24 hours after system removal.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Withdrawal symptoms must be distingushed from overdosage signs and symptoms.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Mental confusion and dizziness may be observed with both withdrawal and acute toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Scopolamine withdrawal is suggested by bradycardia, headache, nausea, abdominal cramps, and sweating, while tachyarrhythmias, dry skin, and decreased bowel sounds occur with acute toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Possible tachycardia; use with caution in patients with tachyarrhythmias, CHF, CAD, or hyperthyroidism.<html><head></head><body><sup>119</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Possible decreased GI motility.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> Use with caution if pyloric or intestinal obstruction is suspected.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Possible increased sensitivity to antimuscarinic effects (e.g., mydriasis, positive chronotropic effect).<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Systemically administered antimuscarinics may reduce bronchial secretions and may lead to inspissation and formation of bronchial plugs in debilitated patients with chronic pulmonary disease; use with caution in such patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Antimuscarinics decrease the tone and amplitude of contractions of the ureters and bladder.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> In patients with uninhibited or reflex neurogenic bladder, the amplitude and frequency of uninhibited contractions are reduced and bladder capacity is increased.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Possible urinary retention in patients with urinary obstruction.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Use with caution in patients with partial obstructive uropathy.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Use contraindicated in patients with obstructive uropathy (urinary bladder neck obstruction caused by prostatic hypertrophy).<html><head></head><body><sup>119</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Scopolamine may aggravate seizures or psychosis; use with caution in patients with a history of these conditions.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Scopolamine Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Well absorbed from the GI tract, principally from the upper small intestine.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Rapidly absorbed following IM or sub-Q injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Transdermal system is designed to provide antiemetic effect in about 4 hours after application.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Inhibition of salivation occurs within 30 minutes to 1 hour after oral administration, peaks within 1–2 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Competitively inhibits acetylcholine or other cholinergic stimuli at autonomic effectors innervated by postganglionic cholinergic nerves and, to a lesser extent, on smooth muscles that lack cholinergic innervation.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • At usual doses, principally antagonizes cholinergic stimuli at muscarinic receptors and has little or no effect on cholinergic stimuli at nicotinic receptors.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Generally more potent than atropine in its antimuscarinic action on the iris, ciliary body, and certain secretory (salivary, bronchial, sweat) glands, and less potent than atropine in its antimuscarinic action on the heart and on bronchial and intestinal smooth muscle.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Apparently corrects some central imbalance of acetylcholine and norepinephrine that may occur in patients with motion sickness.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Antimuscarinics may block the transmission of cholinergic impulses from the vestibular nuclei to higher centers in the CNS and from the reticular formation to the vomiting center; these effects result in prevention of motion-induced nausea and vomiting.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Antimuscarinics also have been referred to as anticholinergics (cholinergic blocking agents), but this term is appropriate only when it describes the antagonism of cholinergic stimuli at any cholinergic receptor, whether muscarinic or nicotinic.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Also have been referred to as parasympatholytics since the antagonized functions principally are under the parasympathetic division of the nervous system.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Receptors at various sites are not equally sensitive to inhibitory effects of antimuscarinics, and degree of inhibition at each site is dose dependent.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Relative sensitivity of physiologic functions (proceeding from the most sensitive) is as follows: secretions of the salivary, bronchial, and sweat glands; pupillary dilation, ocular accommodation, and heart rate; contraction of the detrusor muscle of the bladder and smooth muscle of the GI tract; and gastric secretion and motility.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Doses used to decrease gastric secretions are likely to cause dryness of the mouth (xerostomia) and interfere with visual accommodation, and possibly cause difficulty in urinating.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of patients reporting serious adverse reactions promptly.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of drowsiness, dizziness, blurred vision; avoid activities requiring mental alertness and/or visual acuity (e.g., driving, operating machinery, hazardous work) until effects on individual are known.<html><head></head><body><sup>119</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of using caution with underwater sports participation because of possible disorientation.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

  • For transdermal therapy, carefully instruct patient about use of transdermal scopolamine; importance of providing patient a copy of manufacturer's patient information.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of patient (or individual assisting the patient) thoroughly washing hands with soap and water after handling transdermal system (e.g., initial application, removal) and to wash the application site thoroughly after removing the system, since contamination of the fingers and subsequent contact with the eyes may result in cycloplegia (i.e., mydriasis and blurred vision).<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of proper transdermal system disposal, including keeping it out of the reach of children or pets.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of removing the transdermal system and contacting clinician if pain and reddening of eyes accompanied by dilated pupils occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of removing the transdermal system if difficulty in urinating occurs.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> , and any concomitant illnesses.

  • Importance of women informing clinician if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>119</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients of other important precautionary information.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> (See Cautions.)
