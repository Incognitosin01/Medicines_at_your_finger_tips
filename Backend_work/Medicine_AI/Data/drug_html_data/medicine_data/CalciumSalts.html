Introduction

Calcium salts are used as a source of calcium, an essential nutrient cation.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Uses for Calcium Salts

Dietary Requirements (oral therapy)

For maintaining an adequate intake of calcium to support the development and preservation of bone mass at a level sufficient to prevent fractures associated with osteopenia or osteoporosis in later life and of other calcified tissues (e.g., teeth).<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>126</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>129</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>147</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>153</sup></body></html>

Life long intake of adequate calcium is necessary for good bone health at any age; calcium requirements may increase among older individuals.<html><head></head><body><sup>147</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>148</sup></body></html>

Adequate intakes of calcium can be accomplished through changes in food consumption behaviors, consumption of nutrient-fortified foodstuffs, use of dietary supplements, or a combination of these.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>147</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>153</sup></body></html>

In the US and Canada, calcium principally is obtained from dairy products.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> Other principal sources include fruits, vegetables, and grain products.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> In addition, many healthy individuals take dietary supplements containing calcium.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

For specific information on currently recommended Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs) of calcium for various life-stage and gender groups, see Dosage under Dosage and Administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Hypocalcemia (parenteral and oral therapy)

Calcium salts are used as a source of calcium cation for the treatment or prevention of calcium depletion when dietary measures are inadequate.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> Conditions associated with calcium deficiency include hypoparathyroidism, achlorhydria, chronic diarrhea, vitamin D deficiency, steatorrhea, sprue, pregnancy and lactation, menopause, pancreatitis, renal failure, alkalosis, and hyperphosphatemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Some clinicians consider IV calcium chloride the calcium salt of choice to prevent hypocalcemia during transfusions with citrated blood.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> In addition to being irritating, however, the chloride salt is acidifying and generally should not be used when acidosis coincides with hypocalcemia (e.g., renal failure).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

The calcium glycerophosphate and calcium lactate fixed-combination injection is used IM to increase serum calcium concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>142</sup></body></html>

Administration of certain drugs (e.g., some diuretics, anticonvulsants) may sometimes result in hypocalcemia, which may warrant calcium replacement therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium is administered in long-term electrolyte replacement regimens.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Use of calcium salts should not preclude the use of other measures intended to correct the underlying cause of calcium depletion.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Vitamin D analogs may be administered concomitantly with oral calcium salts for the treatment of chronic hypocalcemia, especially when caused by vitamin D deficiency.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium salts may be used orally for the treatment of hypocalcemia secondary to the administration of anticonvulsant drugs.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Hypocalcemic Tetany (IV therapy)

IV calcium gluconate is considered the salt of choice for the treatment of acute hypocalcemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium salts are used to treat acute hypocalcemic tetany secondary to renal failure, hypoparathyroidism, premature delivery, and/or maternal diabetes mellitus in infants, and poisoning with magnesium, oxalic acid, radiophosphorus, carbon tetrachloride, fluoride, phosphate, strontium, or radium.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Hypoparathyroidism, Chronic (oral therapy)

Calcium salts may be used for treatment of chronic hypoparathyroidism.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Latent Tetany (oral therapy)

Calcium salts may be used for the treatment of latent tetany.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Osteoporosis (oral therapy)

Calcium salts (e.g., calcium carbonate, calcium citrate) are used as supplements in the prevention and treatment of osteoporosis when dietary intake of calcium is insufficient.<html><head></head><body><sup>147</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>148</sup></body></html>

Adequate intake of calcium and vitamin D (which increases absorption of calcium) is universally recommended for all individuals to diminish age-related bone loss and prevent osteoporosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>147</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>148</sup></body></html>

In addition to lifestyle modifications (e.g., regular weight-bearing exercise, avoidance of excessive alcohol and tobacco use), the National Osteoporosis Foundation recommends a daily calcium intake of 1 g in men 50–70 years of age, and 1.2 g in women ≥51 years of age and men ≥71 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>147</sup></body></html>

The combination of calcium and vitamin D has been shown to reduce risk of fractures.<html><head></head><body><sup>147</sup></body></html>

Glucocorticoid-induced Osteoporosis (oral therapy)

Calcium salts are used for prevention of glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>622</sup></body></html>

The American College of Rheumatology (ACR) recommends optimizing dietary intake of calcium (1–1.2 g daily) and vitamin D (600–800 units daily) in all patients receiving long-term glucocorticoid therapy (defined as a daily dosage equivalent to 2.5 mg of prednisone or greater for ≥3 months).<html><head></head><body><sup>622</sup></body></html>

Because of concerns about potential harms (e.g., cardiovascular risks), ACR states that additional study is needed to determine potential benefits versus risks of calcium and vitamin D supplementation in patients receiving glucocorticoids.<html><head></head><body><sup>622</sup></body></html>

Osteomalacia (oral therapy)

Calcium salts may be used for treatment of osteomalacia.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Anticonvulsant-induced Hypocalcemia (oral therapy)

Calcium salts may be used for treatment of rickets, latent tetany, and hypocalcemia secondary to the administration of anticonvulsant drugs.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Rickets (oral therapy)

Calcium salts may be used for treatment of rickets.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Hyperphosphatemia in Chronic Renal Failure (oral therapy)

Calcium acetate and calcium carbonate are considered the salts of choice for chronic renal failure.<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>126</sup></body></html>

In addition to providing a source of calcium, calcium acetate or carbonate sequesters phosphate in the intestine by forming insoluble phosphates that are excreted fecally, thus reducing serum phosphate concentrations and secondary hyperparathyroidism.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium carbonate partially corrects metabolic acidosis which may occur in chronic renal failure.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Because of the risk of aluminum accumulation and resultant neurotoxic and osteomalacic effects, most clinicians no longer use aluminum hydroxide to inhibit phosphorus absorption; instead calcium acetate or carbonate and/or non-calcium-, non-aluminum-, non-magnesium-containing phosphate binders (e.g., lanthanum carbonate, sevelamer hydrochloride) currently are used.<html><head></head><body><sup>127</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>130</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>131</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>132</sup></body></html>

When taken with meals, calcium acetate or carbonate can contribute to controlling hyperphosphatemia in chronic renal failure by binding to and inhibiting absorption of phosphates in the GI tract.<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>127</sup></body></html>

Exercise caution in patients undergoing chronic hemodialysis to prevent hypophosphatemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Patients with end-stage renal failure may develop hypercalcemia when calcium is administered with meals; do not give calcium supplementation concomitantly when calcium salts are used to control hyperphosphatemia in such patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

Progressive hypercalcemia secondary to overdose of calcium salts can occur and may require emergency treatment measures.<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

Chronic hypercalcemia also may lead to vascular and other soft-tissue calcification;<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>126</sup></body></html> periodic (e.g., twice weekly) monitoring of calcium concentrations is recommended during the initial dose adjustment.<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> One manufacturer recommends that the serum calcium times phosphate (Ca × P) product should not exceed 66.<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> Radiographic evaluation of a suspected anatomical region for early soft-tissue calcification may be useful.<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support (IV therapy)

Because of the lack of demonstrated benefit and potential for detrimental effects, calcium should not be used routinely during cardiac arrest unless there is documented hypocalcemia, calcium-channel blocker toxicity, hypermagnesemia, or hyperkalemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>196</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>400</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>401</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>403</sup></body></html> When used in this setting, either calcium chloride or calcium gluconate may be administered.<html><head></head><body><sup>403</sup></body></html>

Aminoglycoside Neuromuscular Blockade (IV therapy)

Calcium salts are used to antagonize neuromuscular blockade† resulting from the use of aminoglycoside antibiotics (e.g., gentamicin, kanamycin, neomycin) with or without agents possessing neuromuscular blocking properties (e.g., gallamine triethiodide).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Magnesium Intoxication (IV therapy)

Calcium gluconate may be used in the treatment of magnesium sulfate overdosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>196</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Myasthenia Gravis (oral therapy)

Calcium salts are used as adjunctive treatment of myasthenia gravis.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

In general, any oral calcium salt may be used for chronic replacement therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (IV therapy)

Calcium infusions (“calcium challenge”) are used in medullary thyroid carcinoma†.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Acid Indigestion (oral therapy)

Calcium carbonate or phosphate may be used for self-medication for the relief of acid indigestion, heartburn, and sour stomach.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Colic, Renal, Biliary, Intestinal, or Lead (IV and IM therapy)

Calcium salts have been used IM or IV as adjunctive therapy to reduce spasms in renal, biliary, intestinal, or lead colic.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome, Diagnosis (IV therapy)

Calcium infusions (“calcium challenge”) are used to diagnose the Zollinger-Ellison syndrome†.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Eaton-Lambert Syndrome (oral therapy)

Calcium salts are used as adjunctive treatment of the Eaton-Lambert syndrome.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

In general, any oral calcium salt may be used for chronic replacement therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Insect Bites and Other Sensitivity Reactions (IV therapy)

Calcium salts have been used IV as adjuncts to relieve muscle cramps in the treatment of insect bites or stings (e.g., black widow spider) or to decrease capillary permeability in sensitivity reactions characterized by urticaria or angioedema and in allergic conditions, including nonthrombocytopenic purpura, dermatitis herpetiformis, drug-induced pruritus, hay fever, and asthma.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Preeclampsia (oral therapy)

Although some evidence suggested a beneficial effect of calcium supplementation on preeclampsia,<html><head></head><body><sup>117</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>118</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>125</sup></body></html> a large, well-designed study did not confirm a beneficial effect of calcium supplementation in preventing preeclampsia during pregnancy.<html><head></head><body><sup>117</sup></body></html> However, these findings do not obviate adequate dietary calcium intake during pregnancy nor do they address whether adequate or increased calcium intake can affect blood pressure favorably in pregnant women.<html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>118</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>125</sup></body></html>

β-Adrenergic or Calcium-channel Blocking Agent Overdosage (IV therapy)

Some experts state that calcium salts may be considered in the treatment of calcium-channel blocking agent-induced toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>403</sup></body></html>

Also may be useful in the treatment of β-adrenergic blocking agent toxicity in patients with shock refractory to other treatment measures.<html><head></head><body><sup>196</sup></body></html>

Diuresis (oral therapy)

Calcium chloride, an acid-forming salt, has been used to promote diuresis, however, because it is irritating and loses effectiveness after a few days, it is rarely used for this effect.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium Salts Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer calcium orally (as acetate, carbonate, citrate, gluconate, lactate, or phosphate salt) or IV (as chloride or gluconate salt).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>j</sup></body></html>

The fixed combination of calcium glycerophosphate and calcium lactate is injected IM.<html><head></head><body><sup>142</sup></body></html>

Calcium chloride also may be administered by intraosseous (IO) injection† during pediatric resuscitation; onset of action and systemic concentrations are comparable to those achieved with venous administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>403</sup></body></html>

Administer acetate, carbonate, citrate, gluconate, lactate, and phosphate salts of calcium orally.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Administer most oral calcium supplements 1–1.5 hours after meals or with a demulcent (e.g., milk).<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> However, calcium carbonate powder generally should be administered with meals, since mixing the powder with food for administration is recommended.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium salts used to bind dietary phosphate in patients with end-stage renal disease should be administered with meals (e.g., 10–15 minutes before, or during, the meal).<html><head></head><body><sup>130</sup></body></html>

For solution and drug compatibility information, see Compatibility under Stability.

Calcium chloride or gluconate may be administered IV.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>j</sup></body></html>

Calcium gluconate usually is administered IV as a 10% solution and calcium chloride as a 2–10% solution.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>j</sup></body></html>

When injected IV, administer calcium salts slowly through a small needle into a large vein to avoid too rapid an increase in serum calcium and extravasation of calcium solution into the surrounding tissue with resultant necrosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Following IV injections, the patient should remain recumbent for a short time.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Close monitoring of serum calcium concentrations is essential during IV administration of calcium.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Children: Calcium salts should not be administered through scalp veins; oral administration of calcium supplements or calcium-rich foods should replace IV calcium therapy as soon as possible.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Usually, administer IV undiluted.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Parenteral calcium salts also may be administered in a compatible large-volume IV infusion fluid.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> (See Solution Compatibility under Compatibility.)

Pharmacy bulk packages are for preparing IV admixtures only.<html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Administer IV calcium injections slowly at a rate not exceeding 0.7–1.8 mEq/minute.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Stop the injection if the patient complains of discomfort.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Pharmacy bulk packages must not be infused IV directly.<html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Administer pediatric dose for CPR by slow IV injection over 10–20 seconds.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium chloride should not be injected IM or into subcutaneous or perivascular tissue, since severe necrosis and sloughing may occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Although other calcium salts may cause mild to severe local reactions, they generally are less irritating than calcium chloride.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> (See Cautions.)

Although some manufacturers previously stated that calcium gluconate could be injected IM when IV administration was not possible,<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> manufacturers of calcium gluconate currently state that the drug should not be injected IM or into subcutaneous tissue because of the potential for severe local reactions.<html><head></head><body><sup>140</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>141</sup></body></html>

Calcium Salts Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Oral bioavailability of calcium from nonfood sources and supplements depends on intestinal pH, the presence or absence of a meal, and the dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Calcium is actively absorbed in the duodenum and proximal jejunum and, to a lesser extent, in the more distal segments of the small intestine.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Degree of absorption depends on a number of factors; absorption from the intestine is never complete.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Absorption requires a soluble, ionized form.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

An acidic intestinal pH is necessary for ionization of calcium; thus an alkaline pH impedes absorption.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Vitamin D, in its activated forms, is required for calcium absorption and increases the capability of the absorptive mechanisms.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Decreased absorption with hypocalcemia secondary to deficiency of either parathyroid hormone or vitamin D.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

IM or IV administered calcium salts are absorbed directly into the blood stream.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Glucocorticoids and low serum concentrations of calcitonin may depress calcium absorption.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

When a 250-mg dose of calcium is administered with a standardized breakfast, average oral bioavailability in adults ranges from 25–35% with various salts; under the same conditions, absorption from milk is about 29%.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Extent of calcium absorption from supplements is greatest when taken in doses ≤500 mg.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Calcium absorption is decreased in the absence of a meal.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Calcium absorption is retarded by certain anions (e.g., oxalates, phytates, sulfates) and by fatty acids which precipitate or complex calcium ions; however, an intestinal pH of 5–7 facilitates maximal dissolution and dissociation of these complexes.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Calcium may be poorly absorbed from foods rich in oxalic acid (e.g., spinach, sweet potatoes, rhubarb, beans) or phytic acid (e.g., unleavened bread, raw beans, seeds, nuts, grains, soy isolates).<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Although soybeans contain high concentrations of phytic acid, calcium absorption is relatively high from this food.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Following IV injection of calcium salts, serum calcium concentrations increase almost immediately and may return to previous values in 30 minutes to 2 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Normal total serum calcium concentrations range from 9–10.4 mg/dL (4.5–5.2 mEq/L), but only ionized calcium is physiologically active.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Serum calcium concentrations are not necessarily accurate indications of total body calcium; total body calcium may be decreased in the presence of hypercalcemia, and hypocalcemia can occur even though total body calcium is increased.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Of total serum calcium concentration, 50% is in the ionic form and 5% is complexed by phosphates, citrates, and other anions.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Hyperproteinemia is associated with increased total serum calcium concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Hypoproteinemia is associated with decreased total serum calcium concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Acidosis results in increased concentrations of ionic calcium, while alkalosis promotes a decrease in the ionic serum calcium concentration.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Infancy: about 60%.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>
