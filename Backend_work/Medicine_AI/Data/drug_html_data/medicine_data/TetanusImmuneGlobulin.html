Introduction

Specific immune globulin (hyperimmune globulin).<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> Tetanus immune globulin (TIG) contains tetanus antitoxin and is used to provide temporary passive immunity to tetanus.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> TIG commercially available in the US is prepared from plasma of donors immunized with tetanus toxoid.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> Other tetanus antitoxin preparations (e.g., equine tetanus antitoxin) may be available in other countries.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html>

Uses for Tetanus Immune Globulin

Postexposure Prophylaxis of Tetanus

Postexposure prophylaxis of tetanus in individuals with tetanus-prone wounds who have previously received <3 doses of a preparation containing tetanus toxoid adsorbed or whose tetanus vaccination status is uncertain.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

TIG provides temporary passive immunity against tetanus.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> It is not a substitute for active immunization with a preparation containing tetanus toxoid adsorbed and is not a substitute for adequate medical and surgical care of contaminated or potentially contaminated wounds.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> (See General under Dosage and Administration.)

Tetanus is a potentially fatal disease caused by a neurotoxic exotoxin produced by Clostridium tetani (tetanospasmin).<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> C. tetani spores are ubiquitous in the environment and are found in soil and in animal (e.g., horses, sheep, cattle, dogs, cats, rats, guinea pigs, chickens) and human intestinal tracts.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> The spores can contaminate open wounds, especially puncture wounds or those with devitalized tissue; anaerobic wound conditions allow the spores to germinate and produce exotoxins that disseminate through the blood and lymphatic system.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> Neonatal tetanus occurs in infants born under nonsterile conditions to women inadequately vaccinated against tetanus; infection usually involves a contaminated umbilical stump and occurs because infant does not have passively acquired maternal antibodies against tetanus.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Obstetric tetanus occurs within 6 weeks after delivery or termination of pregnancy because of contaminated wounds or abrasions or unclean deliveries or abortions.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Generalized tetanus is characterized by rigidity and convulsive muscle spasms that usually involve the jaw (lockjaw) and neck and then become generalized.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> Tetanus occurs worldwide, almost exclusively in individuals who are unvaccinated or inadequately vaccinated against the disease.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> An average of 31 cases reported each year in the US from 2000 through 2007 (case fatality rate 10%);<html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> 20 cases reported in 2003.<html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> Most cases of tetanus in the US occur following acute injuries or wounds (puncture wounds, lacerations, abrasions) and usually occur in adults ≥40 years of age; however, an increase in the disease has been reported in younger adults (e.g., heroin abusers).<html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html>

Tetanus-prone wounds include wounds contaminated with dirt, feces, soil, or saliva; puncture wounds; avulsions; and wounds resulting from crushing, burns, or frostbite.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> Tetanus also has been associated with apparently clean, superficial wounds, surgical procedures, insect bites, animal bites, dental infections, chronic sores and infections, and IV drug abuse.<html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> Tetanus is not transmitted person-to-person.<html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html>

TIG is not necessary for tetanus postexposure prophylaxis in patients with clean, minor wounds (regardless of their immunization status) or for patients with tetanus-prone wounds who have previously received ≥3 doses of a preparation containing tetanus toxoid adsorbed.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html>

In the event of injury and possible exposure to tetanus, the need for active immunization against tetanus with or without passive immunization with TIG depends on the individual’s vaccination status and the likelihood of contamination with tetanus bacilli (e.g., condition of wound, source of contamination).<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> See Table 1 for ACIP guidelines regarding tetanus postexposure prophylaxis.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Because TIG is prepared from pooled human plasma, it may carry a risk of transmitting infectious agents, including the causative agents of viral hepatitis and HIV infection, and theoretically may carry a risk of transmitting the causative agent of Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) or variant CJD (vCJD).<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

Improved donor screening, viral-inactivation procedures (e.g., solvent/detergent treatment), and/or filtration procedures have reduced, but not completely eliminated, risk of pathogen transmission with plasma-derived preparations.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

The manufacturing process for TIG includes certain chemical (solvent/detergent) treatment procedures and/or heat-treatment procedures to reduce viral infectious potential.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Because no purification method has been shown to be totally effective in removing the risk of viral infectivity from plasma-derived preparations and because new blood-borne viruses or other disease agents may emerge which may not be inactivated by the manufacturing process or the chemical (solvent/detergent) treatment procedures currently used, administer TIG only when a benefit is expected.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Any infection believed to have been transmitted by TIG should be reported to the manufacturer at 800-520-2807.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Sensitization to repeated injections of human immune globulin occurs rarely.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Angioedema, nephrotic syndrome, and anaphylactic shock reported rarely.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Use caution in individuals who have exhibited previous systemic allergic reactions to immune globulin.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Skin testing (i.e., intradermal injection of concentrated IgG solutions) is unreliable since localized areas of inflammation may occur as the result of localized tissue irritation and can be misinterpreted as a positive allergy reaction.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Epinephrine and other appropriate agents should be readily available in case anaphylaxis or other serious allergic reaction occurs.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Recommendations regarding use of TIG in individuals with altered immunocompetence are the same as those for individuals who are not immunocompromised.<html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html>

ACIP states that recommendations concerning use of TIG in patients with altered immunocompetence, including HIV-infected individuals or those severely immunocompromised because of congenital immunodeficiency, leukemia, lymphoma, aplastic anemia, generalized malignancy, or therapy with alkylating agents, antimetabolites, radiation, or corticosteroids, are the same as those for patients who are not immunocompromised.<html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html>

AAP states that TIG should be used in the management of tetanus prone wounds in all HIV-infected individuals, regardless of their tetanus immunization history.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html>

Do not administer TIG in the same syringe or at the same injection site as tetanus toxoid adsorbed.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs under Interactions.)

Avoid inadvertent IV administration of TIG; serious systemic reactions (e.g., precipitous decrease in BP, anaphylaxis-like reaction) have occurred following inadvertent IV administration of immune globulin intended for IM administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>

Because bleeding may occur following IM administration in individuals with thrombocytopenia or a bleeding disorder (e.g., hemophilia) or in those receiving anticoagulant therapy, use in such individuals only if benefits outweigh risks.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

ACIP states that IM injections can be used in individuals who have bleeding disorders or are receiving anticoagulant therapy if a clinician familiar with the patient's bleeding risk determines that the injection can be administered with reasonable safety.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> In these cases, use a fine needle (23 gauge) to administer the dose and apply firm pressure to the injection site (without rubbing) for ≥2 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> If patient is receiving antihemophilia therapy, administer the IM dose shortly after a scheduled dose of such therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

Advise individual and/or their family about the risk of hematoma from IM injections.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

Interactions for Tetanus Immune Globulin

Inactivated Vaccines and Toxoids

Immune globulins, including TIG, are not expected to have a clinically important effect on the immune response to inactivated vaccines or toxoids; inactivated vaccines, recombinant vaccines, polysaccharide vaccines, and toxoids may be administered simultaneously with (using different syringes and different injection sites) or at any interval before or after administration of TIG.<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

Live Vaccines

Antibodies present in immune globulins, including TIG, may interfere with the immune response to certain live virus vaccines (e.g., measles, mumps, and rubella virus vaccine live [MMR], rotavirus vaccine live oral, varicella virus vaccine live).<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs under Interactions.) There is no evidence that immune globulin preparations interfere with the immune response to yellow fever virus vaccine live, typhoid vaccine live oral, influenza virus vaccine live intranasal, or poliovirus vaccine live oral (OPV; no longer commercially available in the US).<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

Varicella vaccine

Immune globulin preparations, including TIG, may interfere with the immune response to varicella vaccine live<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

Varicella vaccine live should not be administered simultaneously with or within 3 months before or after TIG<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

Yellow fever vaccine

No evidence that immune globulin preparations interfere with the immune response to yellow fever virus vaccine live<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

Yellow fever vaccine may be given simultaneously with TIG (using different syringes and injection sites) or at any time before or after TIG<html><head></head><body><sup>106</sup></body></html>

  • TIG is a sterile, concentrated, nonpyrogenic solution of immunoglobulins prepared by cold alcohol fractionation from plasma of adults immunized with tetanus toxoid.<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html>
