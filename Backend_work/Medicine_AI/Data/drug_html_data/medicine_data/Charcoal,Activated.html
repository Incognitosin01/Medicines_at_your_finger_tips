Hemoperfusion

Hemoperfusion through columns of activated charcoal to remove endogenous or exogenous toxins in uremia, hepatic failure, or acute toxicity associated with overdose of certain drugs.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Wounds and Ulcers

Has been used in dressings for suppurating wounds or ulcers to decrease odor and promote healing.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Charcoal, Activated Dosage and Administration

General

  • Most effective when administered early, preferably within 30–60 minutes of poison ingestion.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

  • Multiple-dose regimens may be considered for drugs that undergo enterohepatic or enteroenteric circulation, those with a small volume of distribution, those that are not extensively protein bound, and those with a low endogenous clearance.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> Also may be considered for life-threatening ingestions of phenobarbital, carbamazepine, quinine, dapsone, theophylline, paraquat, or Amanita phalloides.<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>114</sup></body></html>

  • Tablets or granules of activated charcoal are less effective than powder and should not be used in the treatment of poisonings.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Administration

Administer activated charcoal powder orally<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> or via nasogastric or orogastric tube<html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>129</sup></body></html> as extemporaneously prepared slurry or suspension or commercially available suspension.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

Continuous nasogastric infusion or division of the total dose into smaller amounts given more frequently may improve tolerance of large doses.<html><head></head><body><sup>129</sup></body></html>

If an antiemetic is required to successfully administer high dosages, a serotonin type 3 ( 5-HT3) receptor antagonist (e.g., ondansetron) or metoclopramide may be preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>129</sup></body></html>

Sorbitol may be administered with single-dose activated charcoal or with first dose of multiple-dose regimen for palatability and laxative action; additional suspending and flavoring agents generally not recommended.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Do not use for ingestion of petroleum distillates (e.g., gasoline, kerosene);<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> limited efficacy, and toxicity other than aspiration is rare.<html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

Sorbitol, present in many commercial preparations, should be administered only with a single dose of activated charcoal or the first dose of multiple-dose activated charcoal; no more than 1 or 2 doses of sorbitol or another cathartic (if required) should be used in a 24-hour period because of potential for dehydration, hypotension, electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypernatremia) associated with excessive catharsis.<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>129</sup></body></html>

If sorbitol is used with an initial dose of activated charcoal, a second cathartic generally should not be administered.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

Use sorbitol with caution in children and geriatric patients; monitor hydration and electrolytes.<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

May cause vomiting,<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> constipation,<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> diarrhea,<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> and GI obstruction<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>114</sup></body></html> or fecal impaction<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> in dehydrated patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>108</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>114</sup></body></html>

Generally should not be used when decreased peristalsis present (reduced or absent bowel sounds); if risk of GI obstruction, perforation, or hemorrhage exists; if surgery has occurred recently; or if electrolyte imbalance or volume depletion exists.<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

Aspiration of activated charcoal may lead to more severe complications than aspiration of gastric contents alone.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> Aspiration from vomiting or misdirected nasogastric catheter has resulted in granulomatous reactions, bronchiolitis obliterans, tissue reaction to suspension agents (sorbitol, povidone), increased lung permeability, and rarely, death.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>111</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>114</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>130</sup></body></html>

Take measures to reduce the risk of aspiration (e.g., placement of a cuffed endotracheal tube in patients with impaired laryngeal reflexes).<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html>

When used in fixed combination with other agents, consider the cautions, precautions, and contraindications associated with the concomitant agents.

Actions

  • Nonspecific adsorbent; inhibits GI absorption of various drugs and chemicals.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Broader spectrum of adsorptive activity than other adsorbents (attapulgite, Arizona montmorillonite, evaporated milk).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Inadequate adsorption of alcohols (e.g., ethanol, methanol, ethylene glycol), iron salts, lithium, corrosive agents (e.g. strong acids and alkalis), boric acid, arsenic, malathion, or organic solvents for clinical use in GI decontamination.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>105</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of calling poison control center (800-222-1222), physician, or emergency department before administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

  • Shake liquid well, drink, then rinse container with water, shake again, and drink to get full dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

  • Administer only after vomiting has ceased.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of not mixing with milk, ice cream, or sherbet.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of taking oral drugs not used for poisoning at least 2 hours before or after administration of activated charcoal.<html><head></head><body><sup>107</sup></body></html>
