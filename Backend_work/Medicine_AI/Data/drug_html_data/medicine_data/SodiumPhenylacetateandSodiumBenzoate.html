Uses for Sodium Phenylacetate and Sodium Benzoate

Acute Hyperammonemia

Adjunctive therapy for the treatment of acute hyperammonemia and associated encephalopathy in patients with urea cycle disorders (e.g., deficiency in carbamyl phosphate synthetase [CPS], ornithine transcarbamylase [OTC], argininosuccinate synthetase [ASS], or argininosuccinate lyase [ASL]);<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> designated an orphan drug by FDA for this use.<html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Treat any episode of acute symptomatic hyperammonemia as a life-threatening emergency; prompt use of all necessary therapies (e.g., dialysis [preferably hemodialysis], caloric supplementation, protein restriction) to reduce ammonia concentrations is essential.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

Hemodialysis is the preferred therapy for acute neonatal hyperammonemic coma, moderate to severe episodes of hyperammonemic encephalopathy, and episodes of hyperammonemia that fail to respond to an initial course of sodium phenylacetate and sodium benzoate therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> In such patients, administration of sodium phenylacetate and sodium benzoate helps prevent reaccumulation of ammonia by increasing waste nitrogen excretion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

Concomitant therapy with IV arginine hydrochloride is required in patients with CPS, OTC, ASS, or ASL deficiency; however, IV arginine hydrochloride is contraindicated in patients with arginase deficiency.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> Pending specific diagnosis, also give IV arginine hydrochloride to hyperammonemic infants with suspected urea cycle disorders.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

If a urea cycle disorder is suspected based on family history, document hyperammonemia before administering sodium phenylacetate and sodium benzoate.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Risk of rapid brain damage or death if acute symptomatic hyperammonemia is left uncontrolled; treat as a life-threatening emergency.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> Prompt use of all therapies to reduce serum ammonia concentrations (e.g., dialysis [preferably hemodialysis]) is essential.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> (See Acute Hyperammonemia under Uses.)

Manage hyperammonemia due to inborn errors of metabolism in coordination with medical personnel familiar with such conditions; usually requires health-care facilities able to provide multidisciplinary treatment (e.g., hemodialysis, nutritional management, medical support).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html>

Closely monitor plasma ammonia concentrations, neurologic status, laboratory tests, and clinical response during treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor serum electrolyte concentrations; maintain within the normal range.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Urinary loss of potassium is enhanced by excretion of nonabsorbable anions phenylacetylglutamine and hippurate; carefully monitor plasma potassium concentrations and provide replacement therapy, when necessary.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Perform blood chemistry profiles and frequent blood pH and blood gases (e.g., pCO2) evaluations to check for salicylate-like toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Salicylate-like Toxicity under Cautions.)

The Urea Cycle Disorders Conference Group and some experts recommend monitoring plasma concentrations of ammonia scavenging drugs (e.g., sodium phenylacetate and sodium benzoate) to avoid toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> Weigh the risk of overdosage against potential benefits of repeating a loading dose in the absence of facilities for drug concentration monitoring.<html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> (See Repeat Loading Doses under Dosage and Administration and also see Repeat Loading Doses under Cautions.)

Experts recommend double-checking the accuracy of prescription orders to avoid possible overdosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

Consider sodium content and use with caution, if at all, in patients with CHF, severe renal impairment, or sodium retention with edema.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If adverse effects associated with increased sodium concentrations occur, discontinue the drug, promptly evaluate the patient, and take appropriate measures.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Adverse neurotoxic effects (e.g., somnolence, fatigue, lightheadedness, headache, dysgeusia, hypoacusis, disorientation, impaired memory, exacerbation of existing neuropathy) reported in cancer patients receiving IV phenylacetate.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Acute onset (mainly mild) occurred upon initiation of therapy; reversible upon discontinuance of the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

The manufacturer states that, because plasma concentrations of phenylacetate are prolonged, do not repeat loading doses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> However, some experts state that a repeat loading dose within 24 hours should be considered only in neonates with severe disorders and/or those receiving dialysis; space loading doses at least 6 hours apart.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> (See Repeat Loading Doses under Dosage and Administration and see Laboratory Monitoring under Cautions.)

Sodium Phenylacetate and Sodium Benzoate Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Mean ammonia concentrations decrease significantly within 4 hours in patients responding to therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Not known whether sodium phenylacetate or sodium benzoate or their conjugated metabolites are distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Sodium phenylacetate is metabolized in the liver and kidneys to phenylacetylglutamine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Sodium benzoate is metabolized in the liver and kidneys to hippuric acid.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Phenylacetylglutamine and hippuric acid are principally excreted in urine via glomerular filtration and tubular secretion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Sodium phenylacetate and sodium benzoate are metabolized in the liver; limited information available in impaired hepatic function.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Sodium phenylacetate and sodium benzoate are metabolized and excreted by the kidneys; renal clearance of drug metabolites and ammonia is required.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

25°C (may be exposed to 15–30°C).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Diluted solutions are stable for up to 24 hours at room temperature and room lighting conditions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Sodium phenylacetate and sodium benzoate decrease ammonia concentrations by serving as alternatives to urea for nitrogen waste excretion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

  • Nitrogen content of phenylacetylglutamine is identical to urea (i.e., 2 moles of nitrogen).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Two moles of nitrogen are removed per mole of phenylacetate conjugated with glutamine; one mole of nitrogen is removed per mole of benzoate conjugated with glycine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

  • Glutamine and glycine used in these reactions are replaced by synthesis, reducing the nitrogen pool and attenuating the risk of ammonia and glutamine-induced neurotoxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>
