Uses for Imiquimod

Actinic Keratosis

Topical treatment of clinically typical, nonhyperkeratotic, nonhypertrophic actinic keratosis on the face or scalp in immunocompetent adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>34</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>35</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>36</sup></body></html>

Basal Cell Carcinoma

Topical treatment of biopsy-confirmed, primary superficial basal cell carcinoma in immunocompetent adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Safety and efficacy not established for other types of basal cell carcinoma, including nodular and morpheaform (fibrosing or sclerosing) types.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use only when superficial basal cell carcinoma tumor diameter is ≤2 cm and is located on the trunk (excluding anogenital skin), neck, or extremities (excluding hands and feet).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use only when surgical methods are medically less appropriate and patient follow-up can be reasonably assured.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Human Papillomavirus (HPV) Infections

Treatment of external genital and perianal exophytic warts (condylomata acuminata) caused by HPV in adults and adolescents ≥12 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>23</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>25</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>26</sup></body></html>

CDC and others recommend that external HPV warts be treated with a self-administered topical therapy (imiquimod, podofilox), a topical therapy administered by a health-care provider (podophyllum resin, trichloroacetic acid [TCA], bichloroacetic acid [BCA]), or a surgical technique (cryotherapy, electrosurgery, surgical excision).<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> Alternative therapies include intralesional interferon alfa or laser surgery.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

A preferred treatment regimen for external genital HPV warts in HIV-infected† adults and adolescents;<html><head></head><body><sup>39</sup></body></html> data are limited regarding the response rate in HIV-infected patients compared with those without HIV infection.<html><head></head><body><sup>39</sup></body></html> An alternative treatment regimen for external HPV warts in HIV-infected children†; topical therapies often are ineffective in such children and a surgical technique (cryotherapy, electrosurgery) usually is preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>40</sup></body></html>

Primary goal is destruction or clearance of visible, symptomatic warts.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> No regimen has been shown to eradicate HPV or affect natural history of HPV infection;<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> effect on transmission of HPV unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Should not be used to treat subclinical genital HPV infection (without exophytic warts).<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy for treatment of urethral, intravaginal, cervical, rectal, or anal HPV warts not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> Some clinicians suggest use of topical imiquimod for treatment of distal meatal HPV warts†, but data are limited.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Molluscum Contagiosum

Has been effective in a limited number of children, adolescents, and adults for the treatment of molluscum contagiosum†,<html><head></head><body><sup>31</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>32</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>33</sup></body></html> but safety and efficacy not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Imiquimod

Contraindications

  • Manufacturer states there are no known contraindications to use of imiquimod.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Safety and efficacy for treatment of actinic keratosis in skin areas >25 cm2 not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Degree of systemic exposure may depend on surface area of application rather than amount of applied dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy of repeat treatment of actinic keratosis (multiple treatment courses applied to the same 25-cm2 skin area) not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Subclinical actinic keratosis lesions may become apparent in the treatment area and may subsequently resolve.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Confirm diagnosis of superficial basal cell carcinoma prior to initiating topical imiquimod therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Safety and efficacy for other types of basal cell carcinoma, including nodular and morpheaform (fibrosing or sclerosing) types not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy for treatment of basal cell carcinoma lesions on the face, head, or anogenital area not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy for treatment of basal cell nevus syndrome or xeroderma pigmentosum not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Patients treated for superficial basal cell carcinoma should receive regular follow-up of the treatment site.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

A medical evaluation is indicated if any suspicious lesion arises in the treatment area at any time after initial clearance of superficial basal cell carcinoma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy for treatment of urethral, intravaginal, cervical, rectal, or anal HPV warts not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Follow-up visits are not required for patients self-administering imiquimod, but may be useful several weeks after initiation of therapy to determine response to treatment, to monitor and treat complications of therapy, and to provide additional patient education and counseling.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Follow-up examinations not mandatory if visible genital and perianal warts have cleared after treatment, but may be beneficial 3 months after treatment is completed since identification of external genital warts may be difficult.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Examination of sexual partners is not necessary for the management of genital HPV warts because data do not indicate that reinfection plays a role in recurrences and, in the absence of curative therapy, treatment to reduce transmission is not realistic.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> However, sexual partners of patients with genital HPV warts may benefit from examination to assess the presence of HPV warts or other sexually transmitted diseases and also may benefit from counseling about the implications of having a partner who has HPV warts.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

HIV-infected individuals† may not respond as well and may have more frequent recurrences of genital HPV warts after treatment compared with immunocompetent individuals.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>39</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>40</sup></body></html> (See Immunocompromised Individuals under Cautions.)

Women with genital HPV warts should be advised to undergo regular Papanicolaou (Pap) tests as recommended for women without genital HPV warts.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Intense local inflammatory reactions (e.g., skin weeping, erosion) can occur after a few applications of imiquimod cream.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>23</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>24</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>25</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>26</sup></body></html> These local inflammatory reactions may be accompanied or preceded by a flu-like syndrome with systemic manifestations (e.g., malaise, fever, nausea, myalgias, rigors).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Imiquimod Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Absorbed systemically following topical application to skin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Systemic exposure appears to be related to surface area of application site rather than total applied dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Not known whether crosses placenta following topical application.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether distributed into milk following topical application.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Following topical application to the skin in adults with actinic keratosis (75-mg doses 3 times weekly for 16 weeks), 0.08–0.15% of topical dose is eliminated in urine as unchanged drug and metabolites.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Following topical application in patients with HPV warts, 0.11 or 2.41% of topical dose is eliminated in urine as unchanged drug and metabolites in men or women, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Apparent half-life following topical application is approximately 10 times greater than that reported following sub-Q administration (2 hours); this indicates prolonged retention in skin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

4–25°C; avoid freezing.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of reading manufacturer’s patient information.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of not using the drug for any disorder other than that for which it was prescribed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of applying to affected areas as directed and not administering orally, intravaginally, or intra-anally.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> Importance of avoiding contact with eyes, lips, and nostrils.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients to immediately rinse with water if the cream gets into the mouth or eyes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients to apply the cream at bedtime, leave the cream on the skin for the prescribed period of time, and not bathe or get treatment area wet until the following morning (6–10 hours after application).<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of not using occlusive dressings.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients that cotton gauze dressing can be used, if needed; cotton underwear can be worn if the genital and perianal area is being treated.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that if a dose is missed, the dose should be applied as soon as it is remembered and the regular treatment schedule continued.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that they may experience local skin reactions during treatment (e.g., edema, erosion/ulceration, erythema, flaking/scaling/dryness, scabbing/crusting, vesicles, weeping/exudate) or application site reactions (e.g., burning, pruritus) that are mild to severe in intensity and may extend beyond the application site onto surrounding skin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients to contact their clinician if they experience any sign or symptom at the application site that restricts or prohibits their daily activity or makes continued application of the cream difficult.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that local skin reactions may be severe enough to require temporary discontinuance of the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Treatment can be resumed as directed by the clinician after the skin reaction has subsided.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • If doses are missed or treatment is temporarily discontinued because of local skin reactions, the total duration of treatment should not be extended beyond the maximum recommended duration (i.e., 16 weeks for treatment of actinic keratosis, 6 weeks for treatment of superficial basal cell carcinoma, 16 weeks for treatment of genital and perianal HPV warts).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of using sunscreen and avoiding or minimizing exposure to natural or artificial sunlight (e.g., tanning bed) during imiquimod treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that localized hypopigmentation or hyperpigmentation has been reported and that these skin color changes may be permanent in some patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients being treated for superficial basal cell carcinoma to contact their clinician if any suspicious lesion arises in the treatment area at any time after initial clinical clearance of superficial basal cell carcinoma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients being treated for genital HPV warts that imiquimod is not a cure for HPV infection and that the drug’s effect on transmission of HPV is unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> New HPV warts may develop during or after treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html> Importance of patient watching for recurrences of HPV warts, particularly during the first 3 months.<html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients being treated for genital HPV warts that uncircumcised males should clean under the foreskin before treatment and once daily during treatment and that females should avoid intravaginal application and take special care when applying near vaginal opening since local reactions at this site may result in pain or swelling and difficult urination.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients being treated for genital HPV warts to avoid sexual (genital, anal, oral) contact while imiquimod cream is on the skin and that the cream may weaken condoms and vaginal diaphragms.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs, as well as any concomitant illnesses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>
