Uses for Carbachol

Open-Angle Glaucoma

Reduction of elevated intraocular pressure (IOP) in patients with primary, open-angle (chronic simple, noncongestive) glaucoma.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Used mainly in patients refractory or hypersensitive to pilocarpine.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

May use concomitantly with sympathomimetic agents, β-adrenergic blocking agents, or carbonic anhydrase inhibitors.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Ocular Surgery

Production of miosis during surgery on the anterior chamber of the eye (e.g., cataract extraction, keratoplasty, peripheral iridectomy, cyclodialysis).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Reduction of the intensity of IOP elevation for the first 24 hours after cataract surgery.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Intraocular acetylcholine generally preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Reduction of IOP in the emergency treatment of acute (congestive) angle-closure glaucoma† prior to surgery.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Pilocarpine generally preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Ophthalmologic Examinations

Production of miosis to counteract mydriatic effects of sympathomimetic agents (e.g., hydroxyamphetamine, phenylephrine) after ophthalmoscopic examinations† in glaucoma patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Pilocarpine generally preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Carbachol Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer ophthalmic topical solution to the conjunctival sac; do not inject.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Administer intraocular injection by injection into the anterior chamber.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Remove contact lenses before instilling solution.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

To minimize adverse effects, begin with a low concentration and increase gradually as needed.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Additionally, instill daily dose or one of the daily doses at bedtime.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Following topical instillation, apply finger pressure on the lacrimal sac for 1–2 minutes to minimize drainage into nose and throat and reduce risk of absorption and systemic reactions.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Remove excess solution around the eye with a tissue and rinse off any medication on hands immediately.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Instill into anterior chamber of the eye before or after securing sutures, following the manufacturer’s directions.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> Vials are for single-dose use only; discard unused portion.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Adjust concentration and frequency of solution instillation according to patient requirements and response, as determined by tonometric readings before and during therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

In patients with heavily pigmented irides, higher solution concentrations may be required.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

1–2 drops of 1.5–3% topical solution up to 3 times daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Inject ≤0.5 mL of a 0.01% solution.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Prescribing Limits

Maximum: 0.5 mL of a 0.01% injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Special Populations

No special population dosage recommendations at this time.

Warnings/Precautions

Systemic reactions (e.g., salivation, syncope, cardiac arrhythmia, epigastric distress, headache, vomiting, asthma, hypotension, diarrhea, urinary urgency, increased sweating) reported rarely after topical application or intraocular injection; usually occur only with very frequent administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Use cautiously in patients with acute cardiac failure, bronchial asthma, active peptic ulcer, hyperthyroidism, GI spasm, urinary tract obstruction, Parkinson’s disease, recent MI, or systemic hypertension or hypotension.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Use topical solution cautiously in presence of corneal abrasion to avoid excessive penetration and systemic toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

If systemic symptoms occur, discontinue drug, at least temporarily.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Retinal detachment reported rarely; use with extreme caution, if at all, in patients with a history or risk of retinal detachment, especially if young or aphakic.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Carefully examine retinal periphery at least annually to detect an impending detachment.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Intraocular injection: Corneal clouding, persistent bullous keratopathy, retinal detachment, and postoperative iritis reported following cataract extraction in some patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Allergic conjunctivitis, dermatitis, or keratitis reported occasionally with miotics; usually alleviated by changing to another miotic.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> In some instances, allergic reactions may be caused by preservatives in the preparations.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Discontinue the drug if sensitivity develops or if original irritation persists or increases.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Possible spasm of accommodation and poor vision in dim light, particularly in geriatric patients and patients with lens opacities.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> (See Advice to Patients.)

Adverse effects often subside after first few days of therapy or if drug is temporarily discontinued.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> (See Administration under Dosage and Administration.)

Possible transient increase in IOP even when the angle is open.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> In some patients with angle-closure glaucoma receiving miotics, may increase IOP and precipitate acute attacks.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Possible lens opacities and cataracts; lens opacities may regress if therapy discontinued early in development; however, cataracts are often progressive.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Regular slit-lamp examinations recommended; discontinue therapy, at least temporarily, if iris cysts, iritis, synechiae, or lens opacities occur. <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Reduced visual acuity in dim light is common in geriatric patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> (See Ocular Effects [Topical Solution] under Cautions.)

Common Adverse Effects

Topical solution: Ocular irritation (burning or discomfort), lacrimation, temporal or periorbital headache, painful ciliary or accommodative spasm, blurred vision or myopia, conjunctival vascular congestion, poor vision in dim light.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Carbachol Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Topical carbachol penetrates intact corneal epithelium very poorly; penetration greatly improved in combination with wetting agent (e.g., benzalkonium chloride 0.003%).<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

May be absorbed through intact skin.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Topical solution: Miosis occurs within 10–20 minutes; maximal reduction in IOP occurs within 4 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Intraocular injection: Maximal miosis occurs within 2–5 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Topical solution: Miosis persists for 4–8 hours; reduced IOP persists for approximately 8 hours (3% solution has a slightly longer duration of action than lower concentrations).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Intraocular injection: Miosis persists for about 24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

8–27°C.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

15–30°C; protect from freezing and excessive heat.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> Stable for 18 months after the date of manufacture.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Choline ester; structurally related to acetylcholine.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Directly stimulates cholinergic receptors, resulting in muscarinic and nicotinic effects.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Indirectly inhibits cholinesterase activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> Resistant to hydrolysis by cholinesterases.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

    May increase release of acetylcholine following nerve stimulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Contracts the iris sphincter and the ciliary muscle, which produces constriction of the pupil (miosis) and spasm of accommodation, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Reduces IOP in normal and glaucomatous eyes.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Exact mechanism for lowering IOP is not precisely known.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Facilitates aqueous humor outflow by contracting the ciliary muscle and widening the trabecular meshwork.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Decreases activity of extraocular muscles of convergence and causes vasodilation of blood vessels of the conjunctiva, iris, and ciliary body and increased permeability of the blood-aqueous barrier, which may lead to vascular congestion and ocular inflammation.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Systemically absorbed carbachol produces parasympathomimetic effects on various body systems.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> (See Systemic Toxicity under Cautions.)

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of removing contact lenses prior to administering topical solution.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of not touching dropper tip to any surface in order to avoid contamination.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patient to immediately contact clinician if signs or symptoms of an ocular infection occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of using caution in night driving or other hazardous tasks in poor light.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of instilling daily dose or one of daily doses at bedtime to minimize adverse ocular effects.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients of other important precautionary information. (See Cautions.)
