Introduction

Anticoagulant; a vitamin K-dependent serine protease precursor prepared from pooled human plasma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Uses for Protein C Concentrate

Congenital Protein C Deficiency

Replacement therapy in patients with severe congenital protein C deficiency for prevention and treatment of venous thrombosis and purpura fulminans<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> (designated an orphan drug by FDA for this use).<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html>

Protein C Concentrate Dosage and Administration

General

  • Dosage and duration of therapy is dependent on the severity of protein C deficiency, the plasma protein C concentration, and the patient’s age and clinical condition.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Individualize dosage based on measured protein C activity, expressed as a percentage of normal plasma protein C concentrations, and adjust according to the pharmacokinetic profile for each individual patient.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Determine plasma protein C concentrations before and during therapy using a chromogenic assay to measure protein C activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • During acute thrombotic episodes, measure protein C activity immediately before administration until the patient is stabilized and continue monitoring afterward.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> The magnitude of increase in plasma protein C activity may be substantially reduced during acute episodes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Monitor coagulation parameters; however, correlation between levels of protein C activity and coagulation parameters not yet established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Administration

Administer by IV injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Initiate therapy under supervision of a clinician experienced in replacement therapy with coagulation factors/inhibitors and where monitoring of protein C activity is feasible.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Protein C (human) may be self-administered if clinician determines that the patient and/or caregiver is competent to safely administer the drug after appropriate training.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Prior to reconstitution, allow lyophilized powder and manufacturer-supplied diluent to warm to room temperature.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Reconstitute lyophilized powder with diluent provided by manufacturer.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Use strict aseptic technique since drug contains no preservative.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Reconstitute appropriate number of vials based on the indicated dosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> Vials contain approximately 500 or 1000 IU protein C (human); actual potency is labeled on each vial.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Reconstitute single-use vials of lyophilized powder by adding 5 or 10 mL of sterile water for injection to vial containing approximately 500 or 1000 units of protein C (human), respectively, using transfer needle provided by manufacturer; resulting solution contains approximately 100 units of protein C (human) per mL.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> Swirl vial gently until powder is completely dissolved.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Withdraw reconstituted solution from vial(s) using filter needle provided by manufacturer.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Before administration, replace filter needle with a suitable needle or infusion set with winged adapter.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> Filter needle provided by manufacturer is intended to filter contents of a single vial.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Administer at room temperature within 3 hours of reconstitution.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Potency of protein C (human) is determined by chromogenic assay and is expressed in international units (IU, units) as tested against the activity of the WHO reference standard.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

One unit is approximately equivalent to the protein C activity (as measured by an amidolytic assay) present in 1 mL of plasma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> The number of units of protein C is indicated on the label of each vial.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Initially, 100–120 units/kg, followed by 60–80 units/kg every 6 hours for 3 doses and a subsequent maintenance dosage of 45–60 units/kg every 6 or 12 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Adjust dosage according to measured plasma protein C activity; initially maintain peak plasma protein C activity at 100% of normal.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Following resolution of the acute event, maintain trough plasma protein C activity at a level above 25% of normal for the duration of treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

In patients initiating oral anticoagulant therapy, continue protein C replacement therapy until adequate anticoagulation is achieved.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs under Interactions.)

Short-term prophylaxis: Initially, 100–120 units/kg, followed by 60–80 units/kg every 6 hours for 3 doses and a subsequent maintenance dosage of 45–60 units/kg every 6 or 12 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Long-term prophylaxis (maintenance dosage): 45–60 units/kg every 12 hours; adjust dosage to maintain trough plasma protein C activity above 25% of normal.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> An increase in peak plasma protein C activity may be appropriate during periods of increased thrombotic risk (e.g., infection, trauma, surgery).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Possible hypersensitivity reactions, including anaphylaxis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If hypersensitivity reaction occurs, discontinue immediately and institute appropriate therapy as indicated.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Possible allergic reactions to trace amounts of murine (mouse) protein and/or heparin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Inhibiting antibodies to protein C (human) not observed in clinical studies; however, the potential for developing antibodies cannot be ruled out.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Potential vehicle for transmission of human viruses (i.e., HIV, hepatitis A [HAV], hepatitis B [HBV], hepatitis C [HCV], parvovirus B19) and other infectious agents.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Improved donor screening, viral-inactivating procedures (e.g., solvent/detergent, heat treatment), and/or immunoaffinity chromatography procedures have reduced but not completely eliminated risk of pathogen transmission with plasma-derived protein C (human) preparations.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Some viruses (e.g., human parvovirus B19, HAV) are difficult to remove or inactivate.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Human parvovirus B19 infection is most serious in pregnant women (fetal infection) or immunocompromised patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Consider vaccination (against HAV and HBV infection) for patients who routinely or repeatedly receive protein C (human).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Theoretical possibility of transmitting causative agent of Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Report suspected infections to the manufacturer at 866-888-2472.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Severe bleeding reported;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> may be associated with concomitant use of anticoagulants.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs under Interactions.)

Possible heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) due to trace amounts of heparin contained in protein C (human) preparations.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If HIT occurs, immediately determine platelet count and consider discontinuance of protein C (human).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

The quantity of sodium in the maximum daily dosage of protein C (human) exceeds 200 mg.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Anticoagulants, oral

Risk of transient hypercoagulable state (before desired anticoagulant effect is achieved) with initiation of concomitant therapy because protein C (human) has a shorter half-life than most vitamin K-dependent proteins (i.e., factors II, IX, and X), which leads to more rapid suppression of activity of protein C (human) than that of the procoagulant factors<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Protein C Concentrate Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Peak plasma concentrations appear to increase dose-linearly.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

The magnitude of increase in plasma protein C concentrations may be reduced with acute thrombosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

9.8 hours (range: 4.9–14.7 hours) based on the noncompartmental method.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Half-life may be shortened in patients with acute thrombosis, purpura fulminans, or skin necrosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Systemic exposure may be reduced in very young children compared with older individuals because the protein may have a shorter half-life, a larger volume of distribution, and/or more rapid clearance; consider this fact when selecting a dosage regimen for children.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Dosage and Administration: Dosage.)

Stability

Storage

2–8°C in the original container; protect from light and avoid freezing.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Stable for 3 years from the date of manufacture when stored under recommended conditions; do not use beyond the expiration date on the container.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • An inactive precursor to a vitamin K-dependent anticoagulant serine protease, activated protein C (APC).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> Protein C is converted to APC by the thrombin/thrombomodulin complex on the endothelial cell surface.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

  • APC, in conjunction with its cofactor, protein S, generally inhibits coagulation by inactivating activated coagulation factors V (Va) and VIII (VIIIa), which results in inhibition of the conversion of prothrombin to thrombin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> In addition, APC facilitates fibrinolysis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • In individuals with severe protein C deficiency, disruption of the protein C anticoagulant pathway results in impaired regulation of thrombin generation and associated thrombophilia.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
