Uses for Abciximab

Acute Ischemic Complications of PCI

Adjunct to anticoagulant therapy (e.g., heparin [referring throughout this monograph to unfractionated heparin], low molecular weight heparin), aspirin, and a P2Y12 platelet adenosine diphosphate (ADP)-receptor antagonist (e.g., clopidogrel, prasugrel, ticagrelor) to prevent acute cardiac ischemic complications in patients undergoing PCI or in patients with non-ST-segment-elevation acute coronary syndromes (NSTE ACS; unstable angina or non-ST-segment-elevation MI [NSTEMI]) not responding to conventional medical therapy in whom PCI is planned within 24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>91</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>994</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>995</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1100</sup></body></html>

Adjunctive therapy with a GP IIb/IIIa-receptor inhibitor can reduce the incidence of cardiac ischemic events, including subsequent MI and death, in patients with NSTE ACS undergoing PCI and in patients without these conditions undergoing PCI.<html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>47</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>48</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>49</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>50</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>51</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy in patients not undergoing PCI not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1016</sup></body></html>

The American College of Cardiology Foundation (ACCF), AHA, the Society for Cardiovascular Angiography and Interventions (SCAI), and other experts currently do not recommend routine use of GP IIb/IIIa-receptor inhibitors in patients with ST-segment elevation MI (STEMI) undergoing PCI; however, selective use of these drugs as an adjunct to heparin may be reasonable in certain high-risk patients (e.g., those with large anterior MI and/or large thrombus).<html><head></head><body><sup>994</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1016</sup></body></html>

ACCF/AHA/SCAI state that administration of a GP IIb/IIIa-receptor inhibitor at the time of PCI as an adjunct to heparin may be particularly useful in patients with NSTE ACS who have high-risk features (e.g., elevated troponin) and are not receiving bivalirudin and are not adequately pretreated with clopidogrel.<html><head></head><body><sup>994</sup></body></html>

Regarding choice of GP IIb/IIIa-receptor inhibitor in patients undergoing PCI, IV abciximab, “double-bolus” IV eptifibatide, and high-dose tirofiban given by direct IV injection all produce a high degree of platelet inhibition and reduce ischemic complications.<html><head></head><body><sup>994</sup></body></html>

Non-ST-Segment-Elevation Acute Coronary Syndromes

Has been used in patients with unstable angina or NSTEMI (i.e., NSTE ACS) managed with conservative medical therapy only†; however, manufacturer and some clinicians state that safety and efficacy of abciximab not established in such patients who are not undergoing PCI.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>991</sup></body></html>

A GP IIb/IIIa-receptor inhibitor may be used in conjunction with aspirin prior to diagnostic angiography (“upstream”) in patients with NSTE ACS in whom an initial invasive management strategy is planned; however, IV eptifibatide or tirofiban is the preferred GP IIb/IIIa inhibitor for this use.<html><head></head><body><sup>991</sup></body></html> Abciximab is recommended in this situation only if there is no appreciable delay before angiography and PCI is likely to be performed.<html><head></head><body><sup>991</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Risk of major bleeding (e.g., intracranial hemorrhage, genitourinary or GI bleeding, bleeding at arterial access site) and minor bleeding (e.g., spontaneous gross hematuria, spontaneous hematemesis); may require blood or platelet transfusions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> (See Bleeding Precautions and see Laboratory Monitoring under Cautions.)

Increased risk of major bleeding observed in patients weighing ≤75 kg;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> during concomitant thrombolytic therapy;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> when PCI is performed within 12 hours of onset of symptoms of MI;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> following prolonged (>70 minutes) PCI;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> or following failed PCI.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html>

Possible anaphylaxis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If anaphylaxis occurs, discontinue abciximab immediately and initiate appropriate treatment; drugs for treatment of hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., epinephrine, dopamine, theophylline, antihistamines, corticosteroids) should be immediately available.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Abciximab Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Maximal inhibition of platelet aggregation occurs within 10 minutes following IV administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Bleeding time returns to ≤12 minutes within 12–24 hours following discontinuance of infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Platelet function generally recovers within 48 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Not known whether abciximab is distributed into breast milk or is absorbed systemically after ingestion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Initial half-life is <10 minutes; second phase half-life is about 30 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Abciximab remains in circulation for ≥15 days in a platelet-bound state.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
