Introduction

Antitoxin; heptavalent preparation of equine immunoglobulin F(ab′)2 and F(ab′)2-related fragments capable of neutralizing botulinum neurotoxins A, B, C, D, E, F, and G.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>13</sup></body></html> Other botulism antitoxin (equine) preparations containing antibody to fewer botulinum neurotoxins (e.g., bivalent botulinum antitoxin AB, monovalent botulinum antitoxin E) were previously available in the US and may be available elsewhere.<html><head></head><body><sup>13</sup></body></html>

Administration

Administer only by slow IV infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prior to administration, must be diluted in 0.9% sodium chloride.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Dilution under Dosage and Administration.) Administer diluted antitoxin solution using a constant infusion pump;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> use of an inline filter is optional.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor closely for signs and symptoms of acute sensitivity or infusion reactions during and immediately after completion of the IV infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Sensitivity Reactions and see Infusion Reactions under Cautions.)

Provided in single-use vials.<html><head></head><body><sup>13</sup></body></html> May arrive from CDC either frozen or thawed.<html><head></head><body><sup>13</sup></body></html> If frozen, thaw by placing vials in a refrigerator at 2–8°C for approximately 14 hours until contents are thawed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> For more rapid thawing, place vials at room temperature for 1 hour, followed by immersion in a water bath at 37°C until thawed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not thaw in a microwave oven.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not refreeze.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prior to dilution, thaw antitoxin (if necessary) and bring to room temperature.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Severe hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., anaphylaxis, anaphylactoid reactions) may occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Risk of severe reactions may be increased in individuals with history of sensitivity to horses or equine blood products, other allergies (e.g., hay fever), or asthma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consider skin sensitivity testing prior to administration of the antitoxin in such individuals to ascertain risk of allergic reactions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Intradermal Sensitivity Testing under Dosage and Administration.)

Administer in setting with appropriate equipment, medication (e.g., epinephrine), and personnel trained in management of hypersensitivity, anaphylaxis, and shock.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Closely monitor for signs and symptoms of acute allergic reactions (e.g., urticaria, pruritus, erythema, angioedema, bronchospasm with wheezing or cough, stridor, laryngeal edema, hypotension, tachycardia) during and after IV infusion of the antitoxin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If hypersensitivity reaction occurs, immediately discontinue antitoxin infusion and initiate emergency medical care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If used in patients at risk for hypersensitivity reactions, initiate the IV infusion using lowest rate achievable (i.e., <0.01 mL/minute) and closely monitor patient.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Delayed hypersensitivity or serum sickness reactions reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Usually manifest as fever, urticarial or maculopapular rash, myalgia, arthralgia, and lymphadenopathy and typically occur 10–21 days after IV infusion of the antitoxin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor for signs and symptoms of delayed allergic reactions or serum sickness.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If such reactions suspected, administer appropriate medical care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Infusion-related reactions (e.g., chills, fever, difficulty breathing, headache, nausea, vomiting, arthralgia, myalgia, fatigue) reported within 20–60 minutes after IV infusion of the antitoxin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Arthralgia, myalgia, fatigue, and vasovagal reactions also reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor patients during and immediately after IV infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If infusion-related reaction occurs, reduce infusion rate and administer symptomatic care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If reaction worsens, discontinue infusion and administer appropriate medical care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Parenteral preparations containing maltose, including the antitoxin, may cause falsely elevated results in blood glucose determinations that use glucose dehydrogenase pyrroloquinolinequinone (GDH-PQQ).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May lead to inappropriate administration of insulin resulting in life-threatening hypoglycemia or may mask true hypoglycemic states.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Botulism Antitoxin (Equine) Pharmacokinetics

Following IV administration, pharmacokinetics of each antitoxin contained in the hepatavalent preparation differ.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

After antibodies contained in the antitoxin neutralize circulating botulinum neurotoxins, the botulinum antibody/antigen complexes are cleared from circulation by organs involved in processing immune complexes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Following IV administration of the haptavalent preparation in healthy adults 19–52 years of age, antitoxin serotypes A, D, and E have shortest half-lives (approximately 9, 8, and 8 hours, respectively).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Antitoxin serotypes B, C, F, and G have half-lives of approximately 34, 30, 14, and 12 hours, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Botulism antitoxin (equine) heptavalent contains purified F(ab′)2 and F(ab′)2-related immune globulin fragments prepared from equine plasma and has antitoxin activity against botulinum neurotoxins A, B, C, D, E, F, and G.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Provides passive immunity to botulinum neurotoxins A, B, C, D, E, F, and G.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Following IV administration, polyclonal antibodies, primarily F(ab′)2 and Fab, contained in the antitoxin combine with and neutralize circulating (unbound) neurotoxins produced by C. botulinum, preventing neurotoxin from interacting with ganglioside anchorage sites and protein receptors on cholinergic nerve endings and preventing neurotoxin from internalizing in cells.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Does not neutralize neurotoxin that is already fixed to tissues.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Exact amount of antitoxin needed to treat botulism intoxication unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Advise patients that botulism antitoxin (equine) heptavalent is prepared from horse plasma and that individuals with allergy to horses or equine blood products, other allergies (e.g., hay fever), or asthma may be at increased risk of hypersensitivity reactions and should receive the antitoxin only if benefits outweigh risks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of informing clinician of such allergies.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that the antitoxin may cause immediate sensitivity reactions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of immediately contacting clinician or seeking emergency treatment if manifestations of serious allergic reactions (e.g., urticaria, pruritus, erythema, angioedema, bronchospasm, stridor, laryngeal edema, hypotension, tachycardia) develop.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Immediate Hypersensitivity or Anaphylaxis under Cautions.)

  • Advise patients that the antitoxin may cause delayed allergic reactions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of immediately contacting clinician if manifestations of delayed allergic reactions or serum sickness (e.g., rash, fever, pruritus, myalgia, arthralgia, fever, lymphadenopathy) develop within 10–21 days after administration of the antitoxin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Delayed Hypersensitivity or Serum Sickness Reactions under Cautions.)

  • Advise patients that the antitoxin is a potential vehicle for transmission of infectious agents, including viruses, since it is prepared using horse serum.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Although screening and viral-inactivating and purification procedures used in manufacture of plasma-derived preparations have reduced the risk of pathogen transmission, the possibility still exists that transmission of viruses and other infectious agents could occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that the antitoxin contains maltose and may cause falsely elevated glucose readings when blood glucose monitoring systems based on glucose dehydrogenase pyrroloquinolinequinone (GDH-PQQ) are used.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Falsely elevated glucose determinations may lead to inappropriate insulin administration resulting in life-threatening hypoglycemia or may mask true hypoglycemic states.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of using glucose-specific test methods not affected by maltose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs, as well as any concomitant illnesses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients of other important precautionary information.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Cautions.)
