Uses for Pentobarbital

Insomnia

Short-term treatment of insomnia (i.e., ≤2 weeks duration); decreased effectiveness for sleep induction and maintenance after 2 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Has been used for routine sedation.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> However, barbiturates used infrequently for this indication since there are few clinical situations in which oral barbiturates provide a safety or efficacy advantage over nonbarbiturate sedatives/hypnotics.<html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

Surgery and Preanesthesia

Preoperatively, to produce sedation and relieve anxiety.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Provide basal hypnosis for general, spinal, or regional anesthesia, or to facilitate intubation procedures.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Seizure Disorders

Alternate therapy to control status epilepticus or acute seizure episodes resulting from meningitis, poisons, eclampsia, alcohol withdrawal, tetanus, or chorea.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

IV diazepam generally considered drug of choice for termination of status epilepticus.<html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Drug Withdrawal

Withdrawal of barbiturate or nonbarbiturate hypnotics in physically dependent patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Agitated Behavior

Has been used to control acute episodes of agitated behavior in psychoses†; however, little value in long-term management of psychoses.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Coma Induction

Has been used in high doses to induce coma in the management of cerebral ischemia† and increased intracranial pressure† associated with head trauma, stroke, Reye’s syndrome, cardiac arrest, asphyxiation, or drowning. <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Has been used to ameliorate or prevent sequelae associated with cerebral ischemia during neurosurgical procedures† that require long periods of cerebral hypoxia.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Pentobarbital Dosage and Administration

General

  • Adjust dosage carefully and slowly according to individual requirements and response.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

  • Following chronic administration, withdraw pentobarbital slowly to avoid the possibility of precipitating withdrawal symptoms if the patient is physically dependent on the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

  • To prevent rebound in rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, withdrawal of a single therapeutic dose over 5 or 6 days (e.g., reducing dosage from 3 to 2 doses daily for 1 week) has been recommended when barbiturates are discontinued following prolonged use.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Potential for paradoxical excitement and/or euphoria, restlessness, or delirium in patients with severe pain. <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html> Barbiturates could mask important symptoms in patients with acute or chronic pain.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html> Use with caution in such patients. <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html> Should not be used in the presence of uncontrolled pain.<html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

Possible tolerance, psychological dependence, and physical dependence following prolonged administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Abrupt cessation after prolonged use in dependent individuals may result in withdrawal symptoms (e.g., delirium, convulsions) and potentially be fatal.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Drug must be withdrawn gradually in patients receiving excessive dosages over extended periods of time.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Performance of activities requiring mental alertness or physical coordination may be impaired.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

Concurrent use of other CNS depressants may potentiate CNS depression.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs under Interactions.)

Possible respiratory depression, apnea, laryngospasm, or vasodilation and hypotension, particularly if pentobarbital is administered IV too rapidly. <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html> Administer slowly; personnel and equipment should be readily available for administration of artificial respiration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

May cause fetal harm.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html> If used during pregnancy or if patient becomes pregnant, apprise of potential fetal hazard.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

Potential risk of complex sleep-related behaviors such as sleep-driving (i.e., driving while not fully awake after ingesting a sedative-hypnotic drug, with no memory of the event), making phone calls, or preparing and eating food, while asleep.<html><head></head><body><sup>h</sup></body></html>

Potential risk of anaphylaxis and angioedema; may occur as early as with the first dose of drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>h</sup></body></html>

Exfoliative dermatitis (e.g., Stevens-Johnson syndrome), sometimes fatal, reported rarely. <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html> Because skin eruptions can precede potentially fatal reactions, discontinue pentobarbital whenever dermatologic reactions occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

  • CNS effects appear to be related, at least partially, to the drug's ability to enhance activity of GABA, the principal inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS, by altering inhibitory synaptic transmissions that are mediated by GABAA receptors.<html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

  • Capable of producing all levels of CNS depression—from mild sedation to hypnosis to deep coma to death.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

  • Anticonvulsant effects of barbiturates are multiple and rather nonselective.<html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html> Principal mechanism of action appears to be reduction of monosynaptic and polysynaptic transmission resulting in decreased excitability of the entire nerve cell; barbiturates also increase the threshold for electrical stimulation of the motor cortex.<html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

  • Barbiturates lower serum bilirubin concentrations in neonates and patients with congenital nonhemolytic unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia, presumably by induction of glucuronyl transferase, the enzyme that conjugates bilirubin.<html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Potential for pentobarbital to impair mental alertness or physical coordination; do not drive or operate machinery until effects on individual are known.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html>

  • When procedures requiring general anesthetics or sedation drugs, including pentobarbital, are considered for young children or pregnant women, importance of discussing with the patient, parent, or caregiver the benefits, risks (including potential risk of adverse neurodevelopmental effects), and appropriate timing and duration of the procedure.<html><head></head><body><sup>750</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>753</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs and alcohol consumption.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Importance of avoiding alcohol while taking the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients of other important precautionary information.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> (See Cautions.)
