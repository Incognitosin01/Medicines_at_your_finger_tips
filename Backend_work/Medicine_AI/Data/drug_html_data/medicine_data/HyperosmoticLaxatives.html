Uses for Hyperosmotic Laxatives

Constipation

Glycerin (rectally),<html><head></head><body><sup>238</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>239</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>240</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>241</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>242</sup></body></html> sorbitol (rectally),<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> and polyethylene glycol (PEG) 3350 (MiraLAX; orally)<html><head></head><body><sup>244</sup></body></html> may be used for relief of occasional constipation.<html><head></head><body><sup>238</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>239</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>240</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>241</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>242</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>244</sup></body></html> However, rectal suppositories or enemas should not be used when oral laxatives are effective.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Glycerin and sorbitol may be used to treat constipation occurring during pregnancy or the puerperium; however, bulk-forming laxatives or stool softeners are usually preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

May avoid or relieve constipation with proper diet (high fiber content [e.g., bran]), adequate fluid intake, prompt response to the defecation reflex, and exercise.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Avoid use of laxatives in infants and children; treat childhood constipation by counseling parents regarding acceptable variations in the frequency of bowel movements.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Bowel Cleansing

PEG 3350 electrolyte solutions are used to empty the bowel prior to colonoscopy and barium enema radiologic examinations.<html><head></head><body><sup>245</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>246</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>247</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>248</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>252</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>253</sup></body></html> Glycerin and sorbitol also have been used prior to these procedures, but these laxatives do not always entirely empty the colon.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

The American Society of Colon and Rectal Surgeons (ASCRS), American Society for Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ASGE), and Society of American Gastrointestinal and Endoscopic Surgeons (SAGES) recommend the use of PEG 3350 electrolyte solutions in patients with electrolyte or fluid imbalances (e.g., those with renal or liver insufficiency, CHF, liver failure, advanced liver disease with ascites).<html><head></head><body><sup>251</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>259</sup></body></html> These experts also recommend use of PEG 3350 electrolyte solutions for colonic cleansing in infants and children.<html><head></head><body><sup>251</sup></body></html>

Sorbitol as an Adjunct to Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate

Sorbitol is used orally or rectally to facilitate the passage of sodium polystyrene sulfonate (a resin) through the intestinal tract, prevent constipation caused by the resin, aid in potassium removal, and improve the palatability of the resin.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>256</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Long-term use of laxatives has been associated with laxative dependence, chronic constipation, and loss of normal bowel function.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Chronic use or overdosage of laxatives may produce persistent diarrhea, hypokalemia, loss of essential nutritional factors, and dehydration.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Generalized tonic-clonic seizures associated with electrolyte abnormalities (e.g., hyponatremia, hypokalemia) have been reported following use of PEG 3350 electrolyte solutions for bowel cleansing in patients without a history of seizures.<html><head></head><body><sup>248</sup></body></html> Such neurologic effects resolved with correction of fluid and electrolyte abnormalities.<html><head></head><body><sup>248</sup></body></html>

Use PEG 3350 electrolyte solutions with caution in patients receiving drugs that increase risk of electrolyte abnormalities (e.g., diuretics, ACE inhibitors).<html><head></head><body><sup>248</sup></body></html> Consider performing baseline and postcolonoscopy electrolyte tests in these patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>248</sup></body></html>

Use PEG 3350 electrolyte solutions (Golytely, Colyte, MoviPrep) with caution in patients with severe ulcerative colitis.<html><head></head><body><sup>245</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>247</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>248</sup></body></html>

When used in combination with bisacodyl, consider the precautions, cautions, and contraindications associated with bisacodyl.<html><head></head><body><sup>252</sup></body></html>

If large volumes of liquid containing sugars (e.g., Gatorade) are added to PEG 3350 (with electrolytes) powder for solution, the sugars may reach the colon and be converted to methane or other flammable gases.<html><head></head><body><sup>259</sup></body></html> If electrocautery is used for polyp removal, possible risk that such gases could ignite and explode.<html><head></head><body><sup>259</sup></body></html>
