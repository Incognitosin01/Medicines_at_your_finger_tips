Introduction

Hematinic agent; a polynuclear iron (III)-hydroxide sucrose complex.

Uses for Iron Sucrose

Iron Deficiency Anemia in Patients with Chronic Kidney Disease

Treatment of iron deficiency anemia in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD), including those who are undergoing dialysis (hemodialysis or peritoneal) and those who do not require dialysis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

In patients with CKD on hemodialysis, IV iron superior to orally administered iron in increasing hemoglobin concentrations and/or minimizing dosage of an erythropoiesis-stimulating agent (ESA); the National Kidney Foundation Kidney Disease Outcomes Quality Initiative (NKF-KDOQI) guidelines state that the IV route of iron administration is preferred in such patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Available data suggest that iron sucrose and sodium ferric gluconate may be associated less frequently with serious adverse effects (e.g., hypersensitivity reactions) than iron dextran.<html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html>

Iron sucrose injection also used as iron maintenance therapy in children with dialysis-dependent (hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis) or non-dialysis-dependent CKD.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Pediatric Use under Cautions.)

Prescribing Limits

Do not exceed 100 mg per dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Special Populations

Select dosage with caution because of age-related decreases in hepatic, renal, and/or cardiac function and of concomitant disease and/or other drug therapies.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Iron Sucrose

Contraindications

  • Known hypersensitivity to iron sucrose or any ingredient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Known hypersensitivity to iron sucrose or any ingredient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Hypersensitivity reactions, including anaphylactic shock, loss of consciousness, collapse, hypotension, dyspnea, and seizures have been reported; sometimes fatal.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> Immediately discontinue drug and obtain medical attention if symptoms of hypersensitivity or drug intolerance occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Monitor patients for at least 30 minutes after completion of infusions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Dosage and Administration.)

Possible hypotension associated with IV administration; may be minimized by adhering to recommended total doses and rates of administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Dosage and Administration.)

Do not administer in patients with evidence suggesting iron overload because body iron excretion is limited and excessive iron in tissues can be hazardous.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Periodically monitor laboratory values indicative of iron storage in the body (e.g., transferrin saturation, serum ferritin concentrations, hemoglobin, hematocrit) to detect iron accumulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Common Adverse Effects

Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, headache, dizziness, hypotension, pruritus, extremity pain, arthralgia, back pain, muscle cramp, injection site reactions, chest pain, peripheral edema.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Interactions for Iron Sucrose

Oral Iron Preparations

No formal drug interaction studies to date.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Like other parenteral iron preparations, iron sucrose injection may be expected to reduce absorption of concomitantly administered oral iron.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Iron Sucrose Pharmacokinetics

Distribution

Distributed into liver, spleen, and bone marrow.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Following IV administration, dissociated into iron and sucrose by the reticuloendothelial system.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Sucrose component is eliminated mainly by urinary excretion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

6 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Serum clearance of iron expected to be more rapid in iron-deficient patients than in healthy individuals.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

20–25°C (may be exposed to 15–30°C); do not freeze.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

When diluted with 0.9% sodium chloride injection at concentrations of 2–10 mg of elemental iron per mL and stored in a plastic syringe, stable for 7 days at controlled room temperature (25 ± 2°C) or under refrigeration (4 ± 2°C).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

When stored undiluted in a plastic syringe at a concentration of 20 mg of elemental iron per mL, stable for 7 days at 25 ± 2°C or 4 ± 2°C.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

When added to IV infusion bags containing 0.9% sodium chloride injection at concentrations of 1–2 mg of elemental iron per mL, stable for 7 days at 25 ± 2°C.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
