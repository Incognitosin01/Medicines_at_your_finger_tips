Introduction

Antimigraine agent; recombinant humanized IgG4 monoclonal antibody that inhibits calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) function.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html>

Uses for Galcanezumab-gnlm

Preventive Treatment of Migraine

Preventive treatment of migraine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

Substantially reduces monthly migraine days in patients with episodic or chronic migraine and reduces acute antimigraine agent-use days in patients with episodic migraine compared with placebo.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

The American Headache Society (AHS) states that galcanezumab and other CGRP antagonists offer a number of advantages over some oral migraine preventive therapies, including no need for dosage escalation, rapid onset of therapeutic activity, demonstrated efficacy in patients after prior preventive treatments have failed as well as in those concurrently receiving oral preventive treatments, minimal risk of adverse drug interactions, and favorable overall tolerability profiles.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> However, because of the relatively high cost of CGRP antagonists, AHS recommends that CGRP antagonists be considered for use only in patients who are unable to tolerate oral preventive therapies and/or who have had an inadequate response to a 6-week trial of ≥2 oral preventive therapies (e.g., topiramate, valproate, β-adrenergic blocking agents, tricyclic antidepressants, SNRIs).<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Treatment of Episodic Cluster Headache

Treatment of episodic cluster headache.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

In the principal efficacy study, substantially reduced weekly cluster headache attack frequency in patients with episodic cluster headache compared with placebo.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>22</sup></body></html>

Galcanezumab-gnlm Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer by sub-Q injection only.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Commercially available in single-dose prefilled auto-injectors (i.e., injection pens), which contain 120 mg of the drug, and single-dose prefilled syringes, which contain either 100 or 120 mg of the drug, and are intended for patient self-administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Administer by sub-Q injection into the abdomen, anterior thigh, back of upper arm, or buttocks; avoid injections within 2 inches of the navel, knee, or groin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May use back of the upper arm or buttocks if someone other than the patient is administering the injection (e.g., caregiver, healthcare professional).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

May administer multiple injections of the drug (i.e., the initial loading dose for migraine consisting of 2 injections and the dose for treatment of episodic cluster headache consisting of 3 injections) at separate locations at the same body site.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Avoid injection into areas where the skin is tender, bruised, erythematous, or indurated.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prior to use, provide patients and/or caregivers with proper training on how to prepare and administer galcanezumab-gnlm sub-Q using the prefilled auto-injectors or syringes, including aseptic technique.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consult manufacturer's labeling for detailed instructions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prior to sub-Q administration, remove prefilled auto-injector or syringe from original carton and allow to sit at room temperature for 30 minutes; avoid exposure to direct sunlight.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not warm auto-injectors and syringes using a heat source (e.g., microwave, hot water).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not use if the solution is cloudy or discolored or contains particles.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prefilled auto-injectors and syringes are intended for single use only; discard after use.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Initiate therapy with a one-time loading dose of 240 mg (administered as 2 consecutive injections of 120 mg each) followed by monthly doses of 120 mg.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In clinical studies, a higher dosage (240 mg once monthly) did not demonstrate additional clinical benefit.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

If a dose is missed, administer the missed dose as soon as possible.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May then schedule subsequent doses once monthly from the date of the last administered dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

When initiating therapy with galcanezumab or another CGRP antagonist in patients already receiving a preventive treatment for migraine, AHS recommends adding the CGRP antagonist to the existing antimigraine regimen and avoiding making other changes until the clinical efficacy of the CGRP antagonist is determined.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Patients with migraine who respond to CGRP antagonist therapy usually respond following the first 3 doses.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> Therefore, AHS recommends assessing clinical efficacy of galcanezumab after 3 months of treatment; continue therapy only if treatment benefits have been observed by that time.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

300 mg (administered as 3 consecutive injections of 100 mg each) at the onset of the cluster period and then monthly until the end of the cluster period.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If a dose is missed, administer the missed dose as soon as possible.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May then schedule subsequent doses once monthly from the date of the last administered dose until the end of the cluster period.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Galcanezumab-gnlm

Contraindications

  • Serious hypersensitivity to galcanezumab or any excipient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Sensitivity Reactions under Cautions.)

Serious hypersensitivity to galcanezumab or any excipient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Sensitivity Reactions under Cautions.)

Warnings/Precautions

Hypersensitivity reactions, including rash, urticaria, and dyspnea, reported during clinical trials.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May occur days after drug administration and may be prolonged.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If a serious or severe hypersensitivity reaction occurs, discontinue the drug and initiate appropriate medical therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Contraindications under Cautions and also see Advice to Patients.)

Manufacturer states that prefilled auto-injectors and syringes of galcanezumab-gnlm are not made with natural rubber latex.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Potential for immunogenicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Antibodies to galcanezumab, including neutralizing antibodies to the drug, reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Available data do not demonstrate an effect of anti-galcanezumab antibody development on pharmacokinetics, efficacy, or safety of the drug; however, data are too limited to make definitive conclusions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No adequate data on the developmental risk associated with use of galcanezumab in pregnant women.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> No adverse effects on offspring observed when pregnant rats and rabbits received the drug sub-Q at dosages resulting in systemic exposures greater than those expected clinically.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Estimated rates of major birth defects and miscarriage among deliveries to women with migraine (2.2–2.9% and 17%, respectively) are similar to rates reported in women without migraine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Possible increased risk of preeclampsia in women with migraine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html>

Consider the long half-life of galcanezumab if used in pregnant women or those planning to become pregnant.<html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html>

Not known whether distributed into human milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Effects on the breast-fed infant or on milk production also unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consider developmental and health benefits of breast-feeding, mother's clinical need for galcanezumab, and potential adverse effects on the breast-fed infant from the drug or underlying maternal condition.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Manufacturer states that safety and efficacy not established in pediatric patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> A clinical study to evaluate the efficacy and safety of galcanezumab in the preventive treatment of episodic migraine in pediatric patients 6–17 years of age† is currently under way.<html><head></head><body><sup>21</sup></body></html>

Pending further clinical experience with CGRP antagonists in pediatric patients, the Pediatric and Adolescent Headache special interest group of the AHS recommends limiting use of CGRP antagonists (e.g., erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab) mainly to postpubertal adolescents† with relatively frequent migraines (i.e., ≥8 headache days per month) who have moderate to severe disability associated with migraine (e.g., PedMIDAS score ≥30) and in whom ≥2 preventive therapies have failed.<html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html> For younger pediatric patients† with severe chronic migraine that is refractory to multiple preventive therapies, consider CGRP antagonists only in carefully selected patients with close monitoring (e.g., pubertal status, bone health, linear growth, weight, body mass index [BMI], infectious complications).<html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html>

Insufficient experience in patients ≥65 years of age to determine whether geriatric patients respond differently than younger adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Hepatic impairment not expected to affect pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>19</sup></body></html> (See Special Populations under Pharmacokinetics.)

Renal impairment not expected to affect pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Special Populations under Pharmacokinetics.)

Galcanezumab-gnlm Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Peak plasma concentrations achieved within approximately 5 days following sub-Q administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Steady-state concentrations achieved following a 240-mg sub-Q loading dose (i.e., the first dose).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Steady-state concentrations achieved after the fourth dose with a 300-mg, once-monthly sub-Q dosage regimen.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Exhibits linear and dose-proportional pharmacokinetics with dosages of 1–600 mg.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Sub-Q injection site does not appear to substantially influence absorption.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Antimigraine effects usually evident following 1–3 monthly sub-Q injections in responding patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Not formally studied in renal or hepatic impairment; renal or hepatic impairment not expected to affect pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>19</sup></body></html> Population pharmacokinetic analysis indicates that Clcr does not affect pharmacokinetics in patients with mild or moderate renal impairment and that serum bilirubin concentration (an indirect marker of hepatic function) does not substantially affect clearance.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>19</sup></body></html> Not studied in patients with severe renal impairment (Clcr <30 mL/minute).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
