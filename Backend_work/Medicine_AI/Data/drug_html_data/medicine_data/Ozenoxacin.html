Uses for Ozenoxacin

Impetigo

Topical treatment of impetigo caused by Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus pyogenes (group A β-hemolytic streptococci; GAS).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>8</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html>

Although impetigo may be self-limiting, anti-infective treatment usually indicated to reduce duration of symptoms and prevent recurrence or transmission to others.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> Empiric treatment with an appropriate narrow-spectrum anti-infective generally used for initial treatment and considered reasonable for typical cases.<html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>43</sup></body></html> Some clinicians suggest in vitro testing (i.e., Gram stain and culture of pus or exudates from skin lesions) to identify causative organism and confirm in vitro susceptibility,<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>43</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>292</sup></body></html> especially if impetigo is extensive and/or failed to respond to initial empiric treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html>

Nonbullous and bullous impetigo have been treated with topical and/or systemic anti-infective therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>43</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> Although comparative efficacy of various regimens not established in well-controlled clinical trials,<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> topical anti-infectives generally used for less extensive disease and systemic anti-infectives generally recommended if impetigo is severe or involves numerous lesions<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>43</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>292</sup></body></html> or if an outbreak is affecting multiple individuals (e.g., family members, childcare groups, athletic teams).<html><head></head><body><sup>43</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>292</sup></body></html>

When empiric treatment used, select appropriate narrow-spectrum anti-infective based on local patterns of resistance reported for S. aureus and S. pyogenes.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>292</sup></body></html>

Ozenoxacin Dosage and Administration

Administration

Apply topically to the skin as a 1% cream.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Do not apply to eyes or mucous membranes;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> do not administer orally, intranasally, or intravaginally.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Apply thin layer of cream to affected area.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May be applied to a maximum total treatment area of 100 cm2 in adults and pediatric patients ≥12 years of age or a maximum of 2% of total body surface area (≤100 cm2) in pediatric patients 2 months through 11 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Treated area may be covered with sterile bandage or gauze dressing, if desired.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Wash hands after applying the cream, unless the hands are being treated.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Children ≥2 months of age: Apply thin layer of 1% cream to affected area twice daily for 5 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Apply thin layer of 1% cream to affected area twice daily for 5 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prescribing Limits

Pediatric patients 2 months through 11 years of age: Maximum treatment area is 2% of total body surface area (≤100 cm2).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Pediatric patients ≥12 years of age: Maximum treatment area is 100 cm2.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Maximum treatment area is 100 cm2.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Ozenoxacin

Contraindications

  • Manufacturer states no known contraindications.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Manufacturer states no known contraindications.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

For external use only.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use only for topical application to skin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Not intended for oral, intranasal, ophthalmic, or intravaginal use.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prolonged use may result in overgrowth of nonsusceptible bacteria and fungi.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If superinfection occurs, discontinue ozenoxacin and institute appropriate therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Data not available regarding use in pregnant women.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Systemic absorption is negligible following topical application to skin (see Absorption under Pharmacokinetics);<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> maternal use of ozenoxacin 1% cream not expected to result in fetal exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No animal reproduction studies using ozenoxacin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Toxicity studies using oral ozenoxacin in pregnant rats and rabbits have not revealed any significant adverse developmental effects at exposures >10,000 times the maximum human plasma concentrations reported following topical application to skin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether systemically absorbed ozenoxacin distributed into human milk, affects human milk production, or affects breast-fed infants.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Systemic absorption is negligible following topical application to skin (see Absorption under Pharmacokinetics);<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> maternal use of ozenoxacin 1% cream not expected to result in exposure in breast-fed child.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Consider benefits of breast-feeding and importance of topical ozenoxacin to the woman;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> also consider potential adverse effects on breast-fed child from the drug or underlying maternal condition.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy of ozenoxacin 1% cream not established in infants <2 months of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy for topical treatment of impetigo in pediatric patients ≥2 months of age is supported by evidence from adequate and well-controlled studies that included pediatric patients 2 months through 17 years of age;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> safety profile in this age group similar to that reported in adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Insufficient experience in patients ≥65 years of age to determine whether geriatric patients respond differently to ozenoxacin 1% cream than younger patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Other reported clinical experience has not identified differences in responses between geriatric and younger patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Ozenoxacin Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Following topical application of ozenoxacin to skin, only negligible amounts absorbed systemically.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Appears to remain in upper skin layers (stratum corneum and epidermidis);<html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html> does not easily penetrate to lower skin layers.<html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html>

Systemic absorption following topical application of ozenoxacin creams of varying strengths (up to 2%, twice the strength of commercially available cream) was evaluated in 86 healthy individuals and patients with impetigo.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Following single or repeated application of ≤1 g of ozenoxacin cream to intact or abraded skin (surface area ≤200 cm2), systemic absorption occurred at the level of detection (0.489 ng/mL) in 2 study participants;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> no evidence of systemic absorption in any other study participants.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Approximately 80–85%.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

In vitro studies indicate ozenoxacin not metabolized in presence of fresh human skin discs and minimally metabolized in hepatocytes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not investigated because systemic absorption following topical application to skin is negligible.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

20–25ºC (may be exposed to 15–30°C).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of applying ozenoxacin 1% cream to affected skin as directed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that ozenoxacin cream is for external use only and should not be swallowed or used in eyes or nose, on mouth or lips, or inside female genital tract.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Inform patients that treated areas may be covered with sterile bandage or gauze dressing, if desired.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients to wash hands after applying ozenoxacin cream, unless the hands are being treated.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of completing full course of treatment, even if symptoms improve.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients to notify a clinician if symptoms do not improve within 3 days after starting treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs, as well as any concomitant illnesses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
