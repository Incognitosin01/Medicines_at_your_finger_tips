Introduction

Biosynthetic (recombinant DNA origin) preparation of blood coagulation factor IX (rIX-FP) genetically fused to recombinant albumin via a short, cleavable peptide derived from the activation peptide of native factor IX.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html>

Uses for Factor IX (Recombinant), albumin fusion protein

Hemophilia B

On-demand control and prevention of bleeding episodes in patients with hemophilia B (congenital factor IX deficiency; Christmas Disease).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html>

Maintenance of hemostasis in patients with hemophilia B undergoing surgery (i.e., perioperative management).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html>

Designated an orphan drug by FDA for treatment of patients with hemophilia B.<html><head></head><body><sup>20</sup></body></html>

Routine prophylaxis (i.e., administration at regular intervals) to prevent or reduce frequency of hemorrhagic events.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Such prophylactic therapy considered the current standard of care for patients with hemophilia B.<html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>176</sup></body></html> Decreases frequency of spontaneous musculoskeletal hemorrhage, preserves joint function, and improves quality of life.<html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>185</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>186</sup></body></html>

Circulating half-life of factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein longer than that of unmodified recombinant or plasma-derived factor IX preparations; may allow for less frequent dosing and improved patient compliance with prophylactic regimens.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>19</sup></body></html>

Several factor IX concentrates are currently available in the US, including a variety of recombinant and plasma-derived preparations;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>18</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>156</sup></body></html> the Medical and Scientific Advisory Council (MASAC) of the National Hemophilia Foundation recommends preferential use of recombinant factor IX preparations because of their potentially superior safety profile with respect to pathogen transmission.<html><head></head><body><sup>155</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>156</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>176</sup></body></html> Other experts (e.g., World Federation of Hemophilia) state that choice of preparation should be determined by local criteria.<html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> When selecting an appropriate factor IX product, consider characteristics of each clotting factor concentrate, individual patient variables, patient/provider preference, and emerging data.<html><head></head><body><sup>155</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>156</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>187</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Dosage (potency) expressed in terms of international units (IU, units) of factor IX activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Potency is determined by an aPTT-based one-stage clotting assay calibrated against a WHO standard.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> Administration of 1 unit/kg factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein increases circulating levels of factor IX by 1.3 units/dL in patients ≥12 years of age and by 1 unit/dL in patients <12 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Estimate dose required to achieve a particular percentage increase in plasma factor IX using the following formula:<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dose (units) = body weight (in kg) x desired factor IX increase (in % of normal or units/dL) × reciprocal of recovery (in units/kg per units/dL) <html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Determine desired factor IX level by the clinical situation and severity of bleeding.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> For recommendations on target factor IX levels for a given clinical situation, see the specific dosage sections for various uses below. These calculations and suggested dosage regimens are only approximations and should not preclude appropriate clinical monitoring and individualization of dosage based on the hemostatic requirements of patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Measure factor IX activity after a dose is given to verify calculated dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html>

If calculated dose is ineffective in achieving appropriate factor IX levels, consider possibility that neutralizing antibodies (inhibitors) may have developed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> (See Development of Inhibitors to Factor IX under Cautions.)

Higher doses or more frequent dosing may be required in pediatric patients because of increased clearance, shorter half-life, and lower recovery of factor IX.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Factor IX (Recombinant), albumin fusion protein

Contraindications

  • Known history of life-threatening hypersensitivity to factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein or any ingredient in the formulation (including hamster proteins).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Known history of life-threatening hypersensitivity to factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein or any ingredient in the formulation (including hamster proteins).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Risk for development of inhibitors to factor IX following treatment with any factor IX preparation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> Reported in <5% of patients with hemophilia B receiving factor IX concentrates.<html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html>

Monitor patients regularly for development of inhibitors with appropriate clinical observation and laboratory tests.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> (See Laboratory Monitoring under Cautions.) Suspect presence of inhibitors if expected factor IX levels not achieved or bleeding not controlled with recommended dose, particularly in those who previously achieved a response.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html>

Because of an association between inhibitor development and allergic reactions, evaluate for presence of inhibitors in any patient experiencing hypersensitivity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Hypersensitivity under Cautions.) In patients with inhibitors, increased risk of anaphylaxis following re-exposure to factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Consultation with a hemophilia treatment center is strongly recommended for patients with inhibitors.<html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html>

Risk of thromboembolic complications (e.g., pulmonary embolism, venous thromboembolism, arterial thrombosis).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor for early manifestations of thromboembolism and consumptive coagulopathy in patients with hepatic disease, signs of fibrinolysis, or other risk factors for thromboembolism or disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Nephrotic syndrome reported following attempted immune tolerance induction in hemophilia B patients with factor IX inhibitors and history of allergic reactions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Safety and efficacy of factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein for immune tolerance induction not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor factor IX levels (using aPTT-based one-stage clotting assay) to guide dosing and assess therapeutic response.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> Results can vary based on type of aPTT reagent; a kaolin-based reagent is likely to result in an underestimation of factor IX activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html>

Monitor for development of inhibitors.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Perform appropriate laboratory test (i.e., Bethesda assay) to confirm presence of an inhibitor.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>171</sup></body></html> (See Development of Inhibitors to Factor IX under Cautions.)

Risk of hypersensitivity reactions, including anaphylaxis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Closely observe patient for signs and symptoms of hypersensitivity (e.g., angioedema, chest tightness, hypotension, generalized urticaria, wheezing, dyspnea).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If a hypersensitivity reaction occurs, immediately discontinue drug and initiate appropriate therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No adequate and well-controlled studies of factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein in pregnant women.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Animal reproductive and development studies lacking.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Use during pregnancy only when clearly needed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether distributed into human milk; use with caution.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

Safety, efficacy, and pharmacokinetics of factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein evaluated in 32 pediatric patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Prophylactic administration of factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein successful in preventing spontaneous bleeding episodes; no differences in safety profile in pediatric patients compared with adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Compared with adults, pediatric patients may have higher body weight-adjusted clearance, shorter half-life, and lower factor IX recovery; higher doses or more frequent dosing may be required.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> (See Dosage under Dosage and Administration.)

Insufficient experience in patients ≥65 years of age to determine whether geriatric patients respond differently than younger patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Factor IX (Recombinant), albumin fusion protein Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Exhibits dose-proportional pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Mean recovery of factor IX approximately 1.3 units/dL in patients ≥12 years of age and 1 unit/dL in patients <12 years of age for each unit/kg of factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein administered.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Compared with adults, pediatric patients appear to have lower incremental recovery of factor IX.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In clinical studies, incremental recovery was approximately 0.95, 1.06, or 1.11 units/dL per each unit/kg administered in patients 0–5 years, 6–11 years, or 12–17 years of age, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Not known whether factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein is distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Following administration of a single 50-unit/kg dose in adults with hemophilia B, half-life is approximately 104 hours; average time to decline to 1% factor IX activity is 23 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Compared with adults, pediatric patients appear to have higher body weight-adjusted clearance and shorter half-life of factor IX.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In clinical studies, half-life was approximately 90, 93, or 87 hours in patients 0–5 years, 6–11 years, or 12–17 years of age, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

In clinical trials, factor IX (recombinant), albumin fusion protein has demonstrated approximately a threefold to fivefold prolongation of the half-life of factor IX activity in circulation when compared with the half-lives of unmodified factor IX preparations.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>13</sup></body></html>
