Introduction

Antivenin (antivenom); equine IgG F(ab′)2 fragments capable of binding and neutralizing venom toxins of North American rattlesnakes (pit vipers).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Uses for Crotalidae Immune F(ab′)2 (Equine)

North American Rattlesnake Snakebite Envenomation

Treatment of envenomation following snakebites involving North American rattlesnakes (Crotalinae, crotalines, pit vipers; formerly known as Crotalidae or crotalids).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html> Designated an orphan drug by FDA for this use.<html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

Crotalinae subfamily of venomous snakes includes rattlesnakes, copperheads, and cottonmouths or water moccasins.<html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html>

Has been effective in management of envenomation involving various crotaline, including Crotalus, Sistrurus, and Agkistrodon.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html>

Consultation with experts experienced in treating snakebites (e.g., regional certified poison control center at 800-222-1222) recommended to guide treatment decisions regarding individual patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Crotalidae Immune F(ab′)2 (Equine) Dosage and Administration

General

  • Initiate treatment as soon as possible after rattlesnake bite in patients who develop clinically important signs of envenomation (e.g., local injury, coagulation abnormality, systemic signs of envenomation).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Prior to and during treatment, assess CBCs, serum chemistries, and coagulation parameters (e.g., PT, aPTT, serum fibrinogen concentration) to evaluate response to the antivenin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Monitor closely during and for at least 1 hour after IV infusion of the antivenin to assess for allergic reactions and confirm that local signs of envenomation are not progressing, systemic symptoms of envenomation have resolved, and coagulation abnormalities have normalized or are trending towards normalization.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Administration

Administer by IV infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Must be reconstituted and diluted prior to administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Reconstitute appropriate number of vials of lyophilized Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine) by adding 10 mL of 0.9% sodium chloride to each vial; mix using continuous gentle swirling (usually dissolves within 1 minute).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Reconstituted solution should be clear to yellow/green and opalescent;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> do not use if discolored or turbid.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Immediately after reconstitution, combine contents of appropriate number of reconstituted vials and dilute total dose (total combined reconstituted vials) to a total volume of 250 mL using 0.9% sodium chloride.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May need to adjust volume of dilution fluid for infants or very small children.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use reconstituted and diluted antivenin within 4 hours after reconstitution.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Stability.)

Discard partially used reconstituted vials or unused diluted antivenin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Administer by IV infusion over 60 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Start initial IV infusion using reduced rate of 25–50 mL/hour for first 10 minutes; observe patient closely for sensitivity reactions (including anaphylaxis).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If reduced rate well tolerated, give remaining initial infusion at rate of 250 mL/hour.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If sensitivity reaction occurs, immediately discontinue IV infusion and reassess risks and benefits before continuing treatment with the antivenin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Sensitivity Reactions under Cautions.)

Warnings/Precautions

Severe hypersensitivity reactions, including anaphylaxis, may occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Patients with history of sensitivity to equine protein are at increased risk for anaphylactic reactions to Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor closely for signs and symptoms of acute hypersensitivity (e.g., urticaria, rash, bronchospasm with wheezing or cough, hypotension) during IV infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If hypersensitivity reaction occurs, immediately discontinue IV infusion and initiate appropriate emergency medical care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Carefully weigh potential risks and benefits of restarting Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine) infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Delayed hypersensitivity or serum sickness reactions may occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html>

Usually manifest as fever, rash, urticaria, pruritus, myalgia, arthralgia, and lymphadenopathy;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> may occur 7–21 days after antivenin treatment.<html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Monitor for signs and symptoms of delayed allergic reactions or serum sickness.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If such reactions suspected, administer appropriate medical care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Coagulopathic effects, such as thrombocytopenia (platelet counts <150,000/mm3), hypofibrinogenemia (fibrinogen concentrations <150 mg/dL), and prolonged PT and PTT, are a complication in many snakebite victims (especially those with severe envenomation).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

Recurrent coagulopathy, characterized by thrombocytopenia, hypofibrinogenemia, and prolonged PT, can occur after successful initial control of envenomation.<html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> There is some evidence that recurrent coagulopathy may occur less frequently in patients treated with Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine) than in those treated with Crotalidae polyvalent immune Fab (ovine);<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> may be related in part to pharmacokinetics of Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine).<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html>

Monitor for signs and symptoms of recurrent coagulopathy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> Manufacturer recommends that, after initial control of envenomation achieved with Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine), monitor closely for reoccurrence of coagulation abnormalities for at least 18 hours in a healthcare setting.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Dosage under Dosage and Administration.) Some clinicians suggest that follow-up in patients treated with antivenin should include platelet counts, fibrinogen concentrations, hemoglobin, and PT at 2–3 days and 5–7 days after antivenin administration and as clinically indicated.<html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html>

Prepared from equine plasma;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> potentially may transmit infectious agents (e.g., viruses).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Several steps in manufacturing process (e.g., pepsin digestion, ammonium sulfate precipitation/heat treatment, nanofiltration) reduce risk of transmission of viruses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Contains trace amounts of cresol (<0.058 mg per vial) from manufacturing process.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Localized reactions and generalized myalgia reported when cresol used as an injectable excipient.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use during pregnancy only when clearly needed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine) can cause fetal harm if administered to a pregnant woman or affect fertility.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> No animal reproduction studies performed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use with caution in nursing women.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Efficacy and safety in pediatric patients comparable to that in adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In clinical trials, 24% of patients were children 2–16 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Age-related dosage adjustments not indicated since venom dose following snakebite is expected to be similar in children and adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> However, fluid volume used to dilute the antivenin may need to be adjusted in infants or small children.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Reconstitution and Dilution under Dosage and Administration.)

Efficacy in geriatric patients comparable to that in overall patient population.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In clinical trials, >9% of patients were >65 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine) is a polyvalent antivenin (antivenom) preparation of venom-specific F(ab′)2 fragments of equine IgG that bind and neutralize venom toxins of Crotalinae (rattlesnakes; genera Crotalus and Sistrurus) native to North America.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Following IV administration, the F(ab′)2 fragments in Crotalidae immune F(ab′)2 (equine) bind to venom components, facilitating redistribution of venom away from target tissues and elimination from the body.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Manufactured from plasma of horses immunized with venom of Bothrops asper and Crotalus durissus.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Antivenin obtained by pepsin digestion of equine plasma to remove the Fc portion of the equine IgG, followed by fractionation and purification to isolate the venom-specific F(ab′)2 fragments.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> The antivenin is stabilized with sodium chloride, sucrose, and glycine; may contain trace amounts of pepsin, cresol, borates, and sulfates from the manufacturing process.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Standardized by ability to neutralize B. asper and C. durissus venom in mice (mouse LD50 neutralizing units).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of immediately contacting a clinician if any unusual bruising or bleeding (e.g., nosebleeds, excessive bleeding after brushing teeth, blood in stools or urine, excessive menstrual bleeding, petechiae, excessive bruising or persistent oozing from superficial injuries) occurs after hospital discharge.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of immediately contacting clinician or seeking emergency treatment if manifestations of a hypersensitivity reaction or anaphylaxis (e.g., urticaria, rash, tightness of the chest, wheezing, hypotension) occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of immediately contacting clinician if manifestations of delayed allergic reactions or serum sickness (e.g., rash, pruritus, urticaria, myalgia, arthralgia, fever) occur after hospital discharge.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are pregnant or breast-feeding.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs, as well as any concomitant illnesses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
