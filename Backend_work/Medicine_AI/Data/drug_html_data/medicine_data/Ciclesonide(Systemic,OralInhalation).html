Uses for Ciclesonide (Systemic, Oral Inhalation)

Asthma

Long-term prevention of bronchospasm in patients with asthma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Administration

Exhale completely and place the mouthpiece of the inhaler well into the mouth with the lips closed firmly around it; keep the tongue below the mouthpiece.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Inhale slowly and deeply through the mouth while pressing the canister down with forefinger.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Hold the breath for about 10 seconds, or as long as comfortable, then withdraw the mouthpiece and exhale gently.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Hypersensitivity reactions, including angioedema with swelling of the lips, tongue, and pharynx, reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Localized candidal infections of the mouth and/or pharynx reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If infection occurs, initiate appropriate local or systemic antifungal treatment while still continuing with inhaled ciclesonide therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May require interruption of ciclesonide therapy in some patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use with caution, if at all, in patients with clinical or asymptomatic Mycobacterium tuberculosis infections of the respiratory tract; untreated systemic fungal, bacterial, parasitic, or viral infections; or ocular herpes simplex.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Treat acute asthma symptoms with a short-acting β2-agonist bronchodilator.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> If symptoms persist, promptly reevaluate asthma therapy and consider initiating systemic corticosteroids.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Increased susceptibility to infections in patients who are taking immunosuppressive drugs compared with healthy individuals.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> Certain infections (e.g., varicella [chickenpox], measles) can have a more serious or even fatal outcome in such patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Take particular care to avoid exposure in susceptible patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If exposure to varicella or measles occurs in susceptible patients, consider administering varicella zoster immune globulin (VZIG) or pooled immune globulin (IG), respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consider treatment with an antiviral agent if varicella develops.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Possible life-threatening adrenal insufficiency in patients being switched from systemic corticosteroids to orally inhaled ciclesonide.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Withdraw systemic corticosteroid therapy gradually<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> and monitor carefully for objective signs of adrenal insufficiency (e.g., fatigue, lassitude, weakness, nausea, vomiting, hypotension) during withdrawal of systemic therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Lung function (FEV1 or morning peak expiratory flow rate [PEFR]), adjunctive β2-adrenergic agonist use, and asthma symptoms also should be carefully monitored.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In most patients, several months are required for total recovery of HPA function following withdrawal of systemic corticosteroid therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Patients who have been maintained on ≥20 mg of prednisone (or its equivalent) daily may be most susceptible to such adverse events, particularly during the later part of the transfer.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Conversion to Orally Inhaled Therapy in Patients Receiving Systemic Corticosteroids under Dosage and Administration.)

Corticosteroid withdrawal symptoms (e.g., joint pain, muscular pain, lassitude, depression) may occur; carefully monitor during and for a number of months after withdrawal of systemic corticosteroids.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Acute adrenal insufficiency may occur during exposure to trauma, surgery, or infection (particularly gastroenteritis) or other conditions associated with severe electrolyte loss.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Possible unmasking of allergic conditions previously controlled by systemic corticosteroid therapy (e.g., rhinitis, conjunctivitis, eczema, arthritis, eosinophilic conditions).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Administration of higher than recommended dosages of inhaled ciclesonide over prolonged periods may result in manifestations of hypercorticism and suppression of HPA function.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> If such changes occur, reduce the dosage of ciclesonide slowly, consistent with accepted procedures for reducing systemic corticosteroid dosage and management of asthma symptoms.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Take particular care in monitoring patients postoperatively or during periods of stress for evidence of inadequate adrenal response.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Supplemental therapy with a systemic corticosteroid required during stress or severe asthma attacks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Long-term use of orally inhaled corticosteroids may affect normal bone metabolism, resulting in a loss of bone mineral density (BMD).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor patients with major risk factors for decreased BMD (e.g., family history of osteoporosis, prolonged immobilization, chronic use of drugs that can reduce bone mass [e.g., anticonvulsants, corticosteroids]) and treat with established standards of care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Glaucoma, increased IOP, and cataracts reported rarely in patients receiving orally inhaled corticosteroids.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Carefully monitor patients who have a change in vision and those with a history of increased IOP, glaucoma, and/or cataracts.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Bronchospasm and wheezing may occur with oral inhalation therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If bronchospasm occurs, treat immediately with a short-acting bronchodilator, and discontinue treatment with ciclesonide and institute alternate therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
