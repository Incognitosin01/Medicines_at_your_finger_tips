Warning

    Rhabdomyolysis with Hyperkalemia

  • Risk of acute rhabdomyolysis with hyperkalemia followed by ventricular arrhythmias, cardiac arrest, and death in apparently healthy children and adolescents who subsequently were found to have undiagnosed skeletal muscle myopathy (e.g., Duchenne’s muscular dystrophy).<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html>

  • Use in children and adolescents should be reserved for those undergoing emergency intubation, those in whom an airway should be secured immediately (e.g., patients with laryngospasm, difficult airway, or full stomach), or those in whom a suitable vein is not accessible and IM administration is needed.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> (See Pediatric Use under Cautions.)

Uses for Succinylcholine

Skeletal Muscle Relaxation

Production of skeletal muscle relaxation during procedures of short duration (e.g., endotracheal intubation) after general anesthesia has been induced;<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> neuromuscular blocking agent of choice for procedures lasting <3 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Facilitation of endotracheal intubation;<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> because of its rapid onset and short duration of action, generally considered neuromuscular blocking agent of choice in emergency situations when rapid intubation (e.g., rapid sequence intubation) is required.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>110</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>424</sup></body></html>

Also used to facilitate mechanical ventilation;<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> however, not used for prolonged neuromuscular blockade in the ICU.<html><head></head><body><sup>341</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>423</sup></body></html>

Because of serious adverse effects, restrict use in pediatric population.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> (See Boxed Warning.)

Administration

Administer IV or IM.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> IV administration is preferred; if necessary, may administer IM in infants or older patients in whom a suitable vein is not accessible.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> Some clinicians recommend that IM administration be reserved for life-threatening situations.<html><head></head><body><sup>424</sup></body></html>

For specific procedures and techniques of administration, consult specialized references.

For solution and drug compatibility information, see Compatibility under Stability.

For prolonged procedures, may administer by continuous IV infusion (preferably) or intermittent IV injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html>

Multiple fractional doses generally should not be used; repeated fractional doses and, to a lesser extent, continuous infusion, may lead to tachyphylaxis.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> (See Prolonged or Repeated Administration under Cautions.)

For continuous IV infusion, dilute succinylcholine chloride to the desired concentration (usually 1–2 mg/mL) in a compatible IV solution (e.g., 5% dextrose injection, 0.9% sodium chloride injection).<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> A concentration of 1 mg/mL usually is used for optimum dosage control; 2 mg/mL may be preferred if amount of fluid should be limited.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Potential for acute rhabdomyolysis with hyperkalemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> (See Boxed Warning and also see Pediatric Use under Cautions.)

Possible severe hyperkalemia resulting in serious cardiac arrhythmias or cardiac arrest in patients with upper motor neuron injury, multiple trauma, extensive or severe burns, or extensive denervation of skeletal muscle because of CNS disease or injury; use is contraindicated in these patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> Risk of hyperkalemia depends on extent and location of the injury, increases over time, usually peaks 7–10 days after the injury,<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> and can persist for >6 months after neural injury.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Use with extreme caution in patients with preexisting hyperkalemia or those at increased risk of hyperkalemia (e.g., those with paraplegia, chronic abdominal infection, tetanus, subarachnoid hemorrhage, degenerative or dystrophic neuromuscular disease, or conditions that may cause degeneration of central and peripheral nervous systems).<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Take appropriate precautions; emergency treatment for anaphylaxis should be immediately available.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html>

Because of the potential for severely compromised respiratory function and other complications, take special precautions during administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> (See Boxed Warning and also see General under Dosage and Administration.)

Possible fatal malignant hyperthermia; manifested by a rapid, profound elevation in body temperature and sometimes extreme muscular rigidity.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> Risk increases with concomitant administration of inhalation anesthetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs under Interactions.)

Succinylcholine Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Poorly absorbed from the GI tract.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Following IV administration, onset is rapid; complete muscle relaxation occurs within 0.5–1 minute following 10- to 30-mg dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Following IM administration, onset occurs in about 2–3 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Following IV administration, duration of action is short (about 2–3 minutes following 10- to 30-mg dose; effects gradually dissipate within 10 minutes).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> Duration of action may be prolonged when administered as continuous IV infusion or in fractional doses.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Following IM administration, duration of action ranges from 10–30 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Duration of action is prolonged in patients with low plasma pseudocholinesterase concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Crosses the placenta in small amounts.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Rapidly hydrolyzed by plasma pseudocholinesterase to succinylmonocholine and then more slowly to succinic acid and choline.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html>

Excreted in urine as active and inactive metabolites and small amounts of unchanged drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Severe hepatic impairment may decrease plasma pseudocholinesterase activity, resulting in increased duration of action due to reduced metabolism.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

In patients with renal impairment, possible decreased plasma pseudocholinesterase activity and possible accumulation of succinylmonocholine.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Produces skeletal muscle relaxation by causing a decreased response to acetylcholine (ACh) at the myoneural (neuromuscular) junction of skeletal muscle.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

  • Exhibits high affinity for ACh receptor sites and produces depolarization of motor end-plate at myoneural junction, resulting in transient twitching or fasciculation of skeletal muscles, followed by muscle paralysis (phase I block).<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

  • Prolonged or repeated administration results in gradual and variable transition to phase II block, which resembles nondepolarizing block.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html>

  • Phase I block is potentiated by cholinesterase inhibitors and can be reversed by nondepolarizing neuromuscular blocking agents; fully established phase II block can be reversed by cholinesterase inhibitors and potentiated by nondepolarizing agents.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

  • Stimulates cardiac vagus and subsequently sympathetic ganglia.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>
