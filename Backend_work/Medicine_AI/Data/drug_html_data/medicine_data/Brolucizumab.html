Introduction

Recombinant humanized monoclonal single-chain antibody fragment; a vascular endothelial growth factor A (VEGF-A) antagonist.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Uses for Brolucizumab

Neovascular Age-related Macular Degeneration

Treatment of neovascular (wet) age-related macular degeneration (AMD).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> When administered every 8 or 12 weeks (depending on clinician assessment of disease activity), brolucizumab-dbll was noninferior to aflibercept administered every 8 weeks in improving visual acuity and preventing further vision loss.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html>

Brolucizumab Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer by intravitreal injection into the affected eye(s).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Only qualified clinicians should administer the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Because of the risk of infection and endophthalmitis, always use proper aseptic technique when preparing and administering the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Endophthalmitis and Other Serious Ocular Effects under Cautions.)

Commercially available in single-use vials containing 6 mg of the drug for intravitreal injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Prior to administration, allow the vial to come to room temperature.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Solution should appear clear to slightly opalescent and colorless to slightly brownish-yellow; do not use if it contains particles or appears cloudy or discolored.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Withdraw entire vial contents through a sterile 5-µm, 18-gauge filter needle (provided by manufacturer) into a 1-mL syringe.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Next, replace filter needle with a sterile 30-gauge, ½-inch needle for intravitreal injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Expel air from the syringe and align plunger tip to the 0.05-mL mark on the syringe.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Administer immediately following preparation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Inject under aseptic conditions (including use of surgical hand disinfection, sterile gloves, sterile drape, sterile eyelid speculum [or equivalent], and availability of sterile paracentesis equipment [if required]) following adequate anesthesia and administration of a broad-spectrum topical microbicide to disinfect the periocular skin, eyelid, and ocular surface.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Each vial is intended for treatment of a single eye only.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If the contralateral eye requires treatment, use a new vial; change the sterile field, syringe, gloves, drape, eyelid speculum, and filter and injection needles before administering brolucizumab in the other eye.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Immediately following intravitreal injection, monitor for elevation in IOP via tonometry or by checking for perfusion of the optic nerve head.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Increased Intraocular Pressure under Cautions.)

Dosage

6 mg (0.05 mL of a solution containing 120 mg/mL) by intravitreal injection into the affected eye(s) once every month (approximately every 25–31 days) for the first 3 doses, followed by 6 mg once every 8–12 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

In pivotal clinical trials, 51–56% of patients received brolucizumab-dbll 6 mg every 12 weeks from the beginning of maintenance treatment through week 48, while 39–45% of patients received this dosing schedule through week 96.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> Selection of dosing interval (8 or 12 weeks) in these studies was based on clinician assessment of disease activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html>

Special Populations

No dosage adjustment required in patients with hepatic impairment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No dosage adjustment required in patients with renal impairment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No dosage adjustment required in patients ≥65 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Brolucizumab

Contraindications

  • Ocular or periocular infection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Active intraocular inflammation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Known hypersensitivity (e.g., rash, pruritus, urticaria, erythema, severe intraocular inflammation) to brolucizumab or any ingredient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Intravitreal injections, including those of brolucizumab, associated with endophthalmitis and retinal detachment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Always use proper aseptic injection technique when administering the drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Ophthalmic Administration under Dosage and Administration.)

Appropriately manage any symptoms suggestive of endophthalmitis or retinal detachment (e.g., eye pain, ocular redness, photophobia, blurred vision).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Advice to Patients.)

Acute increases in IOP observed within 30 minutes following intravitreal injections, including those of brolucizumab.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Sustained increases in IOP also reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Monitor IOP and perfusion of optic nerve head and manage appropriately.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Ophthalmic Administration under Dosage and Administration.)

Potential risk of arterial thromboembolic events (e.g., nonfatal stroke, nonfatal MI, vascular death [including deaths from unknown causes]) in patients receiving intravitreal VEGF antagonists.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In pivotal phase 3 studies, arterial thromboembolic events reported during 96 weeks of treatment in 4.5 or 4.7% of patients receiving brolucizumab-dbll or aflibercept, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Anti-brolucizumab antibodies detected in pretreatment serum samples in 36–52% of treatment-naive patients; after initiation of brolucizumab-dbll therapy, anti-brolucizumab antibodies detected in ≥1 serum sample in 53–67% of patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Intraocular inflammation observed in 6% of patients with anti-brolucizumab antibodies detected during therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Potential for anti-brolucizumab antibodies to affect efficacy or safety of the drug is unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No adequate and well-controlled studies in pregnant women.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Based on mechanism of action, may adversely affect embryofetal development or reproductive capacity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In animal studies, VEGF inhibition caused malformations, embryofetal resorption, and decreased fetal weight and also affected follicular development, corpus luteum function, and fertility.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use during pregnancy only if potential benefits justify potential risk to fetus.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Women of reproductive potential should use highly effective methods of contraception during therapy and for ≥1 month after last dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether brolucizumab distributes into human milk, affects the breast-fed infant, or affects milk production.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Breast-feeding is not recommended during brolucizumab therapy and for ≥1 month after last dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy not established in pediatric patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Approximately 90% of brolucizumab-dbll-treated patients in clinical trials were ≥65 years of age and 60% were ≥75 years of age; no substantial differences in efficacy or safety observed with increasing age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No dosage adjustment required in patients ≥65 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Effect of hepatic impairment on brolucizumab pharmacokinetics is unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No dosage adjustment required in patients with hepatic impairment since intravitreal injection of brolucizumab is not expected to result in clinically important systemic exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Mild to moderate renal impairment (GFR 30–70 mL/minute) does not alter systemic pharmacokinetics of brolucizumab-dbll.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Effect of severe renal impairment on the drug's pharmacokinetics is unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No dosage adjustment required in patients with renal impairment since intravitreal injection of brolucizumab is not expected to result in clinically important systemic exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Common Adverse Effects

Blurred vision, cataract, conjunctival hemorrhage, eye pain, vitreous floaters.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Interactions for Brolucizumab

No formal drug interaction studies to date.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Brolucizumab Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Mean peak serum concentrations of free (unbound to VEGF-A) brolucizumab attained 24 hours following a single 6-mg intravitreal dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Following repeated intravitreal doses, serum concentrations were near or below lower limit of assay quantitation at approximately 4 weeks postdose, with no accumulation observed in most patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Metabolic pathways not fully characterized; free brolucizumab is expected to undergo metabolism via proteolysis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination pathways not fully characterized; free brolucizumab is expected to undergo elimination via target-mediated disposition and/or passive renal excretion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Estimated mean systemic half-life following single intravitreal dose: 4.4 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Age (≥50 years) and sex do not alter systemic pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

2–8°C in the original carton; do not freeze; protect from light.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May store unopened vial at 20–25°C for up to 24 hours prior to use.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Binds to the 3 major isoforms of VEGF-A (i.e., VEGF110, VEGF121, VEGF165) and inhibits their biologic activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

  • VEGF-A induces neovascularization (angiogenesis) and increases vascular permeability, which appear to play a role in the pathogenesis and progression of neovascular (wet) age-related macular degeneration.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

  • Binding to VEGF-A prevents VEGF-A from binding to VEGF receptors (i.e., VEGFR-1, VEGFR-2), reducing endothelial cell proliferation, neovascularization, and vascular permeability.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html>

  • Reduces central retinal subfield thickness associated with age-related macular degeneration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html>

  • As the smallest functional antibody unit, single-chain antibody fragments (e.g., brolucizumab) offer the potential for delivering a larger molar dose compared with larger molecules and achieving greater ocular tissue penetration, which may provide a longer duration of effect and reduced systemic exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Necessity of advising patients about the risk of developing endophthalmitis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of patients informing their ophthalmologist immediately if a change in vision occurs or if the treated eye becomes red, sensitive to light, or painful.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients that they may experience temporary visual disturbances after an intravitreal injection of brolucizumab and the associated eye examination.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients not to drive or use machinery until visual function has recovered sufficiently.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise women of reproductive potential that they should use a highly effective method of contraception during brolucizumab therapy and for ≥1 month after the last dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise women that breast-feeding is not recommended during brolucizumab therapy and for ≥1 month after the last dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs, as well as any concomitant illnesses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients of other important precautionary information.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Cautions.)
