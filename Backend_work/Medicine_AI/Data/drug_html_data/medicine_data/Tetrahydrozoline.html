Introduction

Vasoconstrictor; an imidazoline-derivative sympathomimetic amine.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Uses for Tetrahydrozoline

Conjunctival Congestion

Temporary relief of conjunctival congestion, itching, and minor irritation.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Used alone or in fixed combination with astringents (i.e., zinc sulfate).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Controls hyperemia in patients with superficial corneal vascularity.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Ineffective in the treatment of delayed hypersensitivity reactions such as contact dermatoconjunctivitis.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Used for vasoconstrictor effect during some ocular diagnostic procedures; phenylephrine may be preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Tetrahydrozoline Dosage and Administration

Administration

Apply ophthalmic solution topically to the conjunctiva.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Remove contact lenses before administering ophthalmic solution;<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> wait ≥15 minutes to reinsert contact lenses.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Tetrahydrozoline

Contraindications

  • Known hypersensitivity to tetrahydrozoline or any ingredient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Known hypersensitivity to tetrahydrozoline or any ingredient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Patients with narrow-angle glaucoma or other serious eye disease should consult a clinician before using ophthalmic solution.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Possible irritation of conjunctiva and adverse systemic effects (particularly in children) with excessive dosage and/or prolonged or too frequent use.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Possible ocular hyperemia (redness); avoid prolonged use.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> (See Advice to Patients.)

Overdose may produce CNS depression with drowsiness, decreased body temperature, bradycardia, shock-like hypotension, apnea, and coma.

Accidental ingestion of imidazoline derivatives (i.e., tetrahydrozoline, naphazoline, oxymetazoline) in children has resulted in serious adverse events requiring hospitalization (e.g., coma, bradycardia, decreased respiration, sedation, somnolence).<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1001</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1002</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1003</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1004</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1005</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1006</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1007</sup></body></html> (See Pediatric Use under Cautions and also see Advice to Patients.)

High concentrations of ophthalmic solution may liberate pigment granules; more common in the elderly.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Possible headache, hypertension, weakness, sweating, cardiac irregularities (e.g., palpitations), tremors, drowsiness, lightheadedness, and insomnia.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Use with caution in patients with thyroid disease (e.g., hyperthyroidism), heart disease (e.g., coronary artery disease), hypertension, or diabetes mellitus.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

When used in fixed combination with astringents (i.e., zinc sulfate), consider the cautions, precautions, and contraindications associated with the concomitant drug.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Category C.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html>

Not known whether tetrahydrozoline is distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Caution if used in nursing women.<html><head></head><body><sup>101</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy of ophthalmic solution not established in children <6 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html>

Accidental ingestion of OTC ophthalmic solutions or nasal sprays containing imidazoline derivatives (i.e., tetrahydrozoline, naphazoline, oxymetazoline) in children has resulted in serious adverse events requiring hospitalization, including nausea, vomiting, lethargy, tachycardia, decreased respiration, bradycardia, hypotension, hypertension, sedation, somnolence, mydriasis, stupor, hypothermia, drooling, and coma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1001</sup></body></html> Respiratory depression, CNS depression, and/or lethargy reportedly occurred in infants following accidental ingestion of small amounts (≤6 mL) of 0.05% tetrahydrozoline ophthalmic solution.<html><head></head><body><sup>1002</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1007</sup></body></html> Keep out of reach of children.<html><head></head><body><sup>1001</sup></body></html> (See Advice to Patients.)

Common Adverse Effects

Ophthalmic: Blurred vision, irritation, mydriasis.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Tetrahydrozoline Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Absorption may occasionally be sufficient to produce systemic effects.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Following ocular administration, local vasoconstriction usually occurs within minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Local vasoconstriction may persist for 4–8 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

Tight containers<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> at 15–25°C.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Structurally and pharmacologically related to naphazoline, oxymetazoline, and xylometazoline.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Directly stimulates α-adrenergic receptors; exerts little or no effect on β-adrenergic receptors.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Conjunctival application constricts small arterioles and temporarily relieves conjunctival congestion.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • With ophthalmic use, importance of discontinuing drug and consulting a clinician if ocular pain or visual changes occur or if ocular redness or irritation worsens or persists for >72 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>
