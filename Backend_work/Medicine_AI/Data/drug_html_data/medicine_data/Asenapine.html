Warnings/Precautions

Increased risk of death with use of antipsychotic agents in geriatric patients with dementia-related psychosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>28</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>39</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>73</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>75</sup></body></html>

Analyses of 17 dementia-related psychosis placebo-controlled trials in geriatric patients mainly receiving atypical antipsychotic agents revealed an approximate 1.6- to 1.7-fold increase in mortality compared with that in patients receiving placebo.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>73</sup></body></html>

Most fatalities appeared to result from cardiovascular-related events (e.g., heart failure, sudden death) or infections (mostly pneumonia).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>73</sup></body></html>

Antipsychotic agents, including asenapine, are not approved for the treatment of dementia-related psychosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>39</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>73</sup></body></html> (See Increased Mortality in Geriatric Patients with Dementia-related Psychosis in Boxed Warning; Cerebrovascular Events in Geriatric Patients with Dementia-related Psychosis under Cautions; and also see Dysphagia under Cautions.)

Hypersensitivity reactions reported with asenapine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>96</sup></body></html> Symptoms included anaphylaxis, angioedema, hypotension, tachycardia, swollen tongue, dyspnea, wheezing, and rash.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>96</sup></body></html> Some cases reported occurrence of more than one hypersensitivity reaction, and several reported hypersensitivity reactions occurring after the first dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>96</sup></body></html> In some patients, symptoms resolved after asenapine discontinuance; others required hospitalization or emergency room visits and therapeutic interventions.<html><head></head><body><sup>96</sup></body></html> (See Contraindications under Cautions and also see Advice to Patients.)

Increased incidence of adverse cerebrovascular events (cerebrovascular accidents and TIAs), including fatalities, observed in geriatric patients with dementia-related psychosis treated with certain atypical antipsychotic agents (aripiprazole, olanzapine, risperidone) in placebo-controlled studies.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>28</sup></body></html> Asenapine is not approved for the treatment of patients with dementia-related psychosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Increased Mortality in Geriatric Patients with Dementia-related Psychosis in Boxed Warning.)

Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS), a potentially fatal symptom complex characterized by hyperpyrexia, muscle rigidity, delirium, and autonomic instability, reported with antipsychotic agents,<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> including asenapine.<html><head></head><body><sup>94</sup></body></html>

If NMS is suspected, immediately discontinue therapy and provide intensive symptomatic treatment and monitoring.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Tardive dyskinesia, a syndrome consisting of potentially irreversible, involuntary dyskinetic movements, reported with use of antipsychotic agents, including asenapine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>84</sup></body></html>

Reserve long-term antipsychotic treatment for patients with chronic illness known to respond to antipsychotic agents, and for whom alternative, effective, but potentially less harmful treatments are not available or appropriate.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> In patients requiring chronic treatment, use lowest dosage and shortest duration of treatment needed to achieve a satisfactory clinical response; periodically reassess need for continued therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

APA recommends assessing patients receiving atypical antipsychotic agents for abnormal involuntary movements every 12 months; for patients at increased risk for tardive dyskinesia, assess every 6 months.<html><head></head><body><sup>28</sup></body></html> Consider discontinuance of asenapine if signs and symptoms of tardive dyskinesia appear.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> However, some patients may require treatment despite the presence of the syndrome.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Atypical antipsychotic agents, including asenapine, have caused metabolic changes, including hyperglycemia and diabetes mellitus, dyslipidemia, and weight gain.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> While all atypical antipsychotics produce some metabolic changes, each drug has its own specific risk profile.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Hyperglycemia and Diabetes Mellitus, see Dyslipidemia, and also see Weight Gain under Cautions.)

Risk of orthostatic hypotension and syncope reported with atypical antipsychotics, particularly during initial dosage titration and when dosage is increased.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
