Preexposure Vaccination Against Varicella Infection in High-risk Groups

Health-care personnel should ensure that they are immune to varicella, especially those who have close contact with individuals at high risk for serious complications from varicella.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> ACIP and the Hospital Infection Control Practices Advisory Committee of the US Public Health Service (HICPAC) recommend vaccination against varicella in all susceptible health-care personnel.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> This protects the worker following varicella exposure in the workplace and also may help reduce nosocomial transmission of VZV.<html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html>

Travelers should be vaccinated against varicella.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> Varicella occurs worldwide.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html> Although vaccination against varicella is not a requirement for entry into any country (including the US), CDC states that individuals traveling or living abroad should ensure that they are immune.<html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html>

Postexposure Vaccination Against Varicella Infection and Outbreak Control

Postexposure vaccination in susceptible adults, adolescents, or children with recent exposure to varicella, unless contraindicated.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html>

Prevention and control of varicella outbreaks (e.g., in child-care facilities, schools, institutions).<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html> Varicella outbreaks can persist for up to 4–6 months.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html>

May prevent varicella or modify severity of the disease if given within 3 days, and possibly up to 5 days, after exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>112</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html>

If the exposure does not cause infection, postexposure vaccination should provide protection against subsequent exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html> If the exposure results in infection, vaccination during the presymptomatic or prodromal stage of varicella does not appear to increase risk for vaccine-associated adverse effects or cause more severe natural disease.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html>

During varicella outbreaks, ACIP recommends a second dose of varicella vaccine for those who previously received only a single dose, provided the age-appropriate time interval has elapsed since the first dose (i.e., 3 months for children 12 months through 12 years of age, at least 4 weeks for adults and adolescents ≥13 years of age).<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html>

In hospital settings, consider postexposure vaccination for unvaccinated health-care personnel who have no evidence of immunity at the time of varicella exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>113</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html> Preexposure vaccination is the preferred method for preventing varicella in health-care settings.<html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html>

When varicella vaccine cannot be used (e.g., pregnant women, neonates, immunocompromised individuals) and postexposure prophylaxis is considered necessary, passive immunization with varicella zoster immune globulin (VZIG) is recommended to prevent or reduce severity of varicella.<html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>138</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>143</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>144</sup></body></html> The only VZIG preparation currently available for use in the US (VariZIG; Cangene) must be obtained through an investigational new drug (IND) expanded access protocol from the distributor (FFF Enterprises at 800-843-7477).<html><head></head><body><sup>134</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>135</sup></body></html> If VZIG is not available for postexposure prophylaxis, immune globulin IV (IGIV) can be used.<html><head></head><body><sup>100</sup></body></html>
