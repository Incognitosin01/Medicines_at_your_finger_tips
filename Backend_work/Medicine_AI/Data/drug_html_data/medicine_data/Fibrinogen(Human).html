Introduction

A lyophilized concentrate of highly purified fibrinogen (blood coagulation factor I) prepared from pooled human plasma from suitable donors.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Uses for Fibrinogen (Human)

Acute Bleeding Episodes

Used to control acute bleeding episodes in patients with congenital fibrinogen deficiency (i.e., afibrinogenemia [absence or extremely low concentrations of plasma fibrinogen], hypofibrinogenemia [reduced concentrations of plasma fibrinogen]); designated an orphan drug by FDA for use in this condition.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

Not indicated in patients with dysfibrinogenemia (presence of abnormal or dysfunctional fibrinogen).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Fibrinogen (Human) Dosage and Administration

General

Monitor fibrinogen level.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Target level is 100 mg/dL; maintain this level until hemostasis is obtained.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Each single-use vial contains between 900–1300 mg of fibrinogen (human).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> The actual fibrinogen potency for each lot is indicated on the vial label.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Administration

Administer by slow IV injection using a dedicated line.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Reconstitute vial with 50 mL of sterile water for injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Gently swirl until the lyophilized powder is completely dissolved.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not shake the vial.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> The reconstituted solution should be colorless and clear to slightly opalescent and should be inspected visually for particulate matter; discard solution if cloudy or if particles are present.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Maximum rate is 5 mL/minute.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Individualize dosage based on extent of bleeding, laboratory test results, and clinical condition of the patient.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage is expressed in mg of fibrinogen and is based on weight.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Administration of 70 mg/kg of fibrinogen (human) increased plasma concentration of fibrinogen by approximately 120 mg/dL in a pharmacokinetic study in 14 patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

When the baseline fibrinogen level is known, use the following formula:

Target fibrinogen level (mg/dL) - measured fibrinogen level (mg/dL) / 1.7 (mg/dL per mg/kg body weight) = mg of fibrinogen/body weight (in kg)

When the baseline fibrinogen level is not known, recommended dose is 70 mg/kg.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Special Populations

No special population dosage recommendations at this time. <html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Fibrinogen (Human)

Contraindications

  • History of serious immediate hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., anaphylaxis) to fibrinogen (human) or any ingredient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

History of serious immediate hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., anaphylaxis) to fibrinogen (human) or any ingredient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Allergic and/or hypersensitivity reactions may occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Closely monitor for manifestations of hypersensitivity (e.g., hives, generalized urticaria, chest tightness, wheezing, hypotension, anaphylaxis).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Immediately discontinue drug and administer appropriate treatment if hypersensitivity occurs.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Thromboembolic events (e.g., MI, pulmonary embolism, DVT, arterial thrombosis) may occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Weigh benefits of the drug against risk of thrombosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Monitor for thrombosis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Potential vehicle for transmission of human viruses (e.g., HIV, hepatitis B virus [HBV], hepatitis C virus [HCV]) and other infectious agents.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Improved donor screening and viral eliminating/reducing procedures have reduced, but not completely eliminated risk of pathogen transmission with plasma-derived preparations.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Possibility still exists for disease transmission.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Report any suspected infections to the manufacturer at 866-915-6958.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Category C.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not studied in nursing women with congenital fibrinogen deficiency.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Evaluated in a limited number of children ≤16 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Experience in those ≥65 years of age insufficient to determine whether geriatric adults respond differently than younger adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Common Adverse Effects

Fever, headache.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Fibrinogen (Human) Pharmacokinetics

Elimination

Adults: Approximately 82.3 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Children <16 years of age: Approximately 69.9 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

2–25°C.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not freeze.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Protect from light.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Reconstituted vials may be stored at room temperature and used within 24 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Reconstituted solutions should not be frozen.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Fibrinogen (human) is a highly purified preparation of fibrinogen derived from pooled human plasma.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Fibrinogen is a substrate of thrombin, factor VIII, and plasmin.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> During the coagulation process, thrombin cleaves fibrinogen, catalyzing a series of steps that ultimately result in formation of a stable fibrin clot.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

    Plasma used in the preparation of fibrinogen are screened for human viruses (e.g., hepatitis B virus [HBV], hepatitis C virus [HCV], HIV).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Additional viral purification steps are employed to further reduce the risk of pathogen transmission.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Risk of hypersensitivity reactions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of informing clinician if hives, chest tightness, wheezing, hypotension, or anaphylaxis occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of blood clotting disorders; importance of contacting a clinician if any new or unusual symptoms of thrombosis occur (e.g., unexplained pleuritic, chest and/or leg pain or edema, hemoptysis, dyspnea, tachypnea, unexplained neurologic symptoms).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of transmission of certain viruses or CJD; careful screening and manufacturing process have reduced risk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Symptoms of viral infection include headache, fever, nausea, vomiting, weakness, malaise, diarrhea, and jaundice (hepatitis).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs, as well as any concomitant illnesses.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing patients of other important precautionary information.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Cautions.)
