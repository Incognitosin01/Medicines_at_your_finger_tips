Introduction

Antiarrhythmic agent; an amide-type local anesthetic, class Ib agent.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Uses for Lidocaine (Systemic)

Ventricular Arrhythmias

Alternative to other antiarrhythmic drugs (e.g., amiodarone, procainamide, sotalol) for the acute treatment of life-threatening ventricular arrhythmias such as those that occur following MI or during cardiac manipulative procedures such as cardiac surgery.<html><head></head><body><sup>400</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>401</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>500</sup></body></html>

Used during cardiac arrest for treatment of refractory (i.e., resistant to CPR, defibrillation, and a vasopressor [epinephrine]) VF or pulseless VT.<html><head></head><body><sup>400</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>401</sup></body></html> Considered an alternative to amiodarone for this use in current ACLS guidelines in adults; in pediatric patients, current evidence supports use of either amiodarone or lidocaine.<html><head></head><body><sup>401</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>402</sup></body></html>

Alternative to other antiarrhythmic agents or synchronized electrical cardioversion in the treatment of hemodynamically stable monomorphic VT; however, other agents (e.g., procainamide, sotalol, amiodarone) preferred.<html><head></head><body><sup>401</sup></body></html>

Status Epilepticus

Treatment of status epilepticus†, as a last resort.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Lidocaine (Systemic) Dosage and Administration

General

  • Solutions containing epinephrine must not be used to treat arrhythmias.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Adjust dosage carefully according to individual requirements and response;<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> constant ECG monitoring is recommended.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

  • Terminate infusion as soon as the basic cardiac rhythm appears to be stable or at the earliest sign of toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Immediately stop infusion if signs of excessive cardiac depression occur (e.g., prolongation of PR interval and QRS complex, appearance or aggravation of arrhythmias).<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

  • Infusions continued for >24 hours should be rarely necessary.<html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html>

Lidocaine (Systemic) Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Absorbed from the GI tract, but passes into hepatic circulation and only about 35% of an oral dose reaches the systemic circulation unchanged.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Toxic effects appear at oral doses that fail to produce therapeutic plasma concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Following IM (deltoid) injection, peak plasma concentrations are achieved in 10 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Absorbed in trachea; when administered endotracheally, plasma concentrations generally lower than those achieved with vascular administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>401</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>458</sup></body></html> Absorption is biphasic.<html><head></head><body><sup>458</sup></body></html> Endotracheal absorption may be increased by diluting the drug in sterile water instead of 0.9% sodium chloride injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>401</sup></body></html>

Following direct IV (bolus) administration of 50–100 mg, onset of action is 45–90 seconds.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Following direct IV (bolus) administration of 50–100 mg, duration is 10–20 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Plasma lidocaine concentrations of 1–5 mcg/mL are required to suppress ventricular arrhythmias; concentrations exceeding 5 mcg/mL associated with toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Approximately 90% of a parenteral dose is metabolized rapidly in the liver by de-ethylation to the active metabolites monoethylglycinexylidide (MEGX) and glycinexylidide (GX).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Rate of metabolism appears to be limited by hepatic blood flow.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Excreted in urine principally as metabolites.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Lidocaine has an initial half-life of 7–30 minutes and a terminal half-life of 1.5–2 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Elimination half-lives of MEGX and GX are 2 and 10 hours, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Membrane-stabilizing antiarrhythmic agent.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Combines with fast sodium channels in their inactive state and inhibits recovery after repolarization in a time- and voltage-dependent manner.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Exhibits rapid rates of attachment to and dissociation from transmembrane sodium channels.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Controls ventricular arrhythmias by suppressing automaticity in His-Purkinje system and by suppressing spontaneous depolarization of the ventricles during diastole.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>b</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>128</sup></body></html>

  • Little effect on AV node conduction and His-Purkinje conduction in normal heart at therapeutic concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Affects conducting tissues of ventricles more than atria.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Variable effect on the effective refractory period (ERP) of the AV node; ERP and action potential duration of the His-Purkinje system are shortened.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Cardiac actions appear to be similar to those of phenytoin.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>
