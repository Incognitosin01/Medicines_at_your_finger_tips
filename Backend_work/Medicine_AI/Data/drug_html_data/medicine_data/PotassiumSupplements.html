Uses for Potassium Supplements

Hypokalemia

Treatment or prevention of hypokalemia (potassium deficiency) in patients in whom dietary measures are inadequate.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Conditions that may indicate or result in potassium deficiency include vomiting, diarrhea, drainage of GI fluids, hyperadrenalism, malnutrition, debilitation, prolonged negative nitrogen balance, prolonged parenteral alimentation without addition of potassium, dialysis, metabolic alkalosis, metabolic or diabetic acidosis, GI tract abnormalities that result in poor absorption, certain renal diseases, and familial periodic paralysis characterized by hypokalemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium should be included in long-term electrolyte replacement regimens and has been recommended for routine prophylactic administration following surgery after adequate urine flow has been established.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium replacement may be indicated in patients receiving certain drugs that may sometimes cause potassium depletion (e.g., thiazide diuretics, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, loop diuretics, some corticosteroids, corticotropin, aminosalicylic acid, amphotericin B).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Although ingestion of potassium-rich foods and/or use of potassium-containing salt substitutes may prevent potassium depletion in patients receiving potassium-depleting drugs, judicious prophylactic administration of potassium may be advisable in selected patients during prolonged diuretic or corticosteroid therapy, especially if they are digitalized.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium chloride usually is the salt of choice in the treatment of potassium depletion, since the chloride ion is required to correct hypochloremia which frequently accompanies potassium deficiency and since the citrate, bicarbonate, gluconate, or another alkalinizing salt of potassium may cause hypochloremia, particularly when used in conjunction with chloride-restricted diets.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Alkalinizing potassium salts (acetate, bicarbonate, citrate, gluconate) should be used for treatment of hypokalemia in patients with metabolic acidosis (e.g., renal tubular acidosis).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Potassium also is available as the potassium phosphate salt; however, potassium phosphate usually is used to replace phosphate losses or to correct coexisting hypokalemia and hypophosphatemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>q</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>r</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>s</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>t</sup></body></html>

Hypertension

Inadequate dietary intake of potassium plays an important role in the development of hypertension,<html><head></head><body><sup>102</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> and high dietary intake of potassium (including use of potassium supplements) may protect against the development of high blood pressure and improve blood pressure control in patients with hypertension.<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1200</sup></body></html>

Most experts recommend enhanced intake of potassium<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1200</sup></body></html> (3.5–5 g daily)<html><head></head><body><sup>1200</sup></body></html> in hypertensive patients as part of lifestyle modifications unless contraindicated by chronic kidney disease (CKD) or use of drugs that reduce potassium excretion.<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1200</sup></body></html> (See Cautions: Precautions and Contraindications.) Increased potassium intake is recommended particularly in those unable to adequately reduce their sodium intake.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html>

Adequate intake of potassium should be considered as a means of preventing the development of hypertension.<html><head></head><body><sup>103</sup></body></html> Food sources high in potassium such as fruits and vegetables<html><head></head><body><sup>104</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>1200</sup></body></html> are preferred over potassium supplements.<html><head></head><body><sup>1200</sup></body></html>

Arrhythmias

Potassium salts may be used cautiously to abolish arrhythmias of cardiac glycoside toxicity precipitated by a loss of potassium.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Elevation of plasma potassium concentrations by 0.5–1.5 mEq/L or to the ULN may be useful in the management of tachyarrhythmias following cardiac surgery,<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> but this strategy should not be used in patients with atrioventricular block since potassium may further impair nodal conduction.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium Supplements Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer orally or by slow IV infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Potassium-containing injections (usually potassium chloride),<html><head></head><body><sup>k</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>m</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>n</sup></body></html> have been administered by hypodermoclysis† (into subcutaneous tissues).<html><head></head><body><sup>25</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>k</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>l</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>m</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>n</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>o</sup></body></html>

Potassium acetate, bicarbonate, chloride, citrate, and gluconate can be administered orally.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Potassium acetate and chloride can be administered IV.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Whenever possible, potassium supplements should be given orally since the relatively slow absorption from the GI tract prevents sudden, large increases in plasma potassium concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Replace IV potassium therapy with oral supplements and/or ingestion of potassium-rich foods as soon as possible.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Oral potassium supplements should preferably be administered with or after meals with a full glass of water or fruit juice to minimize the possibility of GI irritation and a saline cathartic effect.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Usually administered orally in 1–4 doses daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> Daily dosage >20 mEq should be divided into several doses and should not be given as a single dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Powders or tablets for oral solution should be dissolved and/or diluted and administered according to the manufacturers’ directions.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Extended-release potassium chloride preparations should be reserved for use in patients who cannot tolerate or refuse to take liquid or effervescent potassium preparations or for those in whom there is a problem of compliance with these latter dosage forms.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Close monitoring of ECG and plasma potassium concentrations is essential during IV administration of potassium, especially when the rate of administration is >20 mEq/hour.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> (See Hyperkalemia under Cautions.)

Potassium IV solutions should generally be administered only in patients with adequate urine flow (e.g., administer to postoperative patients only after adequate urine flow established).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

In dehydrated patients, 1 liter of potassium-free fluid should be administered prior to initiating potassium therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Local vascular intolerance may limit the ability to administer concentrated solutions; administer via large, high-flow vein (e.g., femoral vein) or administer less concentrated solutions in divided doses via 2 veins simultaneously.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Avoid administration of concentrated potassium solutions via subclavian, jugular, or right atrial catheter; local potassium concentrations achieved in the heart may be high and potentially cardiotoxic.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium chloride injection in plastic containers should not be used in series connections with other plastic containers, since such use could result in air embolism from residual air being drawn from the primary container before administration of fluid from the secondary container is complete.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Hyperkalemia has been reported when concentrated potassium chloride solutions were added to IV infusions from a hanging flexible plastic container, apparently as a result of pooling of the concentrated potassium solution at the base of the container and infusion of undiluted solution.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Squeezing the container does not facilitate mixing but tends to pump the concentrated solution into the infusion chamber.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Such solutions must be carefully mixed by inverting the plastic container during the addition of potassium solutions with subsequent agitation and/or kneading to prevent pooling.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

For solution and drug compatibility information, see Compatibility under Stability.

Potassium acetate and potassium chloride are available as concentrates that must be diluted prior to IV administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Generally, potassium concentrations in IV fluids should not exceed 40 mEq/L.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> However, higher potassium concentrations (e.g., 60–80 mEq/L) occasionally may be needed initially for management of severe hypokalemia and associated cardiac arrhythmias, diabetic ketoacidosis or diuretic phase of acute renal failure.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Must be administered by slow IV infusion.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Generally, rate of administration should not exceed 20 mEq/hour.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

More rapid administration occasionally may be necessary for management of severe hypokalemia and associated cardiac arrhythmias or diabetic ketoacidosis or diuretic phase of acute renal failure.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

If administered by hypodermoclysis†, potassium concentrations should not exceed 10 mEq/L to avoid local pain.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Dosage of potassium supplements usually expressed as mEq of potassium.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Normal adult daily potassium requirement and usual dietary intake of potassium is 40–80 mEq; infants may require 2–3 mEq/kg or 40 mEq/m2 daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Dosage must be carefully individualized according to the patient’s requirements and response.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

To avoid serious hyperkalemia, replacement of potassium deficits must be undertaken gradually, usually over a 3- to 7-day period depending on the severity of the deficit.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium replacement requirements can be estimated only by clinical condition and response, ECG monitoring, and/or plasma potassium determinations.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

40 mEq of potassium is provided by approximately:

3.9 g of potassium acetate

4.0 g of potassium bicarbonate

3.0 g of potassium chloride

4.3 g of potassium citrate

9.4 g of potassium gluconate

If used in pediatric patients†, do not exceed 3 mEq/kg daily in young children.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Average dosage approximately 20 mEq daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> Usually should not exceed 200 mEq daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>p</sup></body></html>

Usual dosage is 40–100 mEq or more daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> Usually should not exceed 200 mEq daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>p</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Hyperkalemia and cardiac arrest can occur following use of potassium supplements in patients with impaired mechanisms for excreting potassium.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Most common and serious adverse effect of potassium therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potentially fatal; can develop rapidly and patients may be asymptomatic.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> Occurs most frequently with IV potassium (especially if administered too rapidly),<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> but may occur with oral potassium.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Use IV solutions containing potassium with extreme caution, if at all, in patients with hyperkalemia, severe renal failure, or other conditions with potassium retention.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Evaluate renal function before therapy; monitor clinical status with periodic ECGs and/or determinations of plasma potassium concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Clinical signs and symptoms of hyperkalemia include paresthesia of the extremities, listlessness, mental confusion, weakness or heaviness of the legs, flaccid paralysis, cold skin, gray pallor, peripheral vascular collapse with fall in blood pressure, cardiac arrhythmias, and heart block.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

In patients who have both hypokalemia and metabolic acidosis, an alkalinizing potassium salt (acetate, bicarbonate, citrate, gluconate) should be used for treatment of hypokalemia.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Use of IV solutions containing potassium may cause fluid and/or solute overload, leading to decreased electrolyte concentrations, overhydration, congestion, and pulmonary edema.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Use IV solutions containing potassium with extreme caution, if at all, in patients with CHF, severe renal insufficiency, or other conditions with sodium retention and edema.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html>

Solid oral dosage forms of potassium have resulted in ulcerative and/or stenotic GI lesion; perforation has occurred.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html> Possibly more frequent with enteric-coated tablets (no longer commercially available in the US).<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Administer wax matrix and extended-release preparations with caution; discontinue immediately if abdominal pain, distention, severe vomiting, or GI bleeding occurs.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Reserve use of extended-release potassium chloride preparations for patients who cannot tolerate or refuse to take liquid or effervescent potassium preparations or for those in whom there is a problem of compliance with these latter dosage forms.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Some experts question the use of any solid potassium preparation, since use of dilute liquid preparations minimizes the risk of GI complications.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Pain and phlebitis may occur at IV administration site, especially with potassium solutions containing ≥30 mEq/L.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Monitor fluid balance, electrolyte concentrations, and acid-base balance periodically during therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> Regular serum potassium determinations are recommended, especially in patients with renal impairment or diabetic nephropathy.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Potassium Supplements Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Well absorbed following oral administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Following oral administration of extended-release formulations, potassium is released slowly; risk of high, localized concentrations is minimized.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Normal plasma potassium concentrations generally range from 3.5–5 mEq/L in healthy adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Plasma concentrations up to 7.7 mEq/L may be normal in neonates.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Plasma potassium concentrations are not necessarily indicative of cellular potassium concentrations; cellular deficits may occur without concomitant decreases in plasma potassium concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Hypokalemia may occur without substantial depletion of cellular potassium.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Extracellular fluid pH changes produce reciprocal effects on plasma potassium concentrations; 0.1 unit increase in plasma pH produces a decrease of 0.6 mEq/L in plasma potassium concentration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Enters extracellular fluid and actively transported into cells; intracellular concentration is up to 40 times extracellular concentration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Intracellular movement augmented by dextrose, insulin, and oxygen.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium concentrations in gastric and intestinal secretions are higher than plasma concentrations; diarrheal fluid may contain up to 60 mEq/L.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Excreted principally in urine; small amounts may be excreted via the skin and intestinal tract.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Filtered by the glomeruli, reabsorbed in the proximal tubule, and secreted in the distal tubule, the site of sodium-potassium exchange.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Tubular secretion influenced by chloride ion concentration, hydrogen ion exchange, acid-base equilibrium, and adrenal hormones.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Healthy adults on potassium-free diets usually excrete 40–50 mEq of potassium daily.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Potassium excretion decreased in patients with renal impairment.<html><head></head><body><sup>c</sup></body></html>

Surgery and/or tissue injury result in increased urinary excretion of potassium which may continue for several days.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Postoperative patients or patients under stress of disease with normal kidneys may excrete up to 80–90 mEq of potassium daily, even though they are not receiving any potassium.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients
