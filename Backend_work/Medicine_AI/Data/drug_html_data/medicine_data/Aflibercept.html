Aflibercept Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer by intravitreal injection only into the affected eye(s); must be administered only by a qualified physician.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prior to intravitreal administration, withdraw entire contents of aflibercept vial through a sterile 5-µm, 19-gauge filter needle (provided by manufacturer) into a 1-mL syringe (provided by manufacturer) using aseptic technique.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Prior to intravitreal injection, replace filter needle with a sterile 30-gauge, ½-inch needle (provided by manufacturer).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> To obtain appropriate dose (2 mg), expel contents in syringe until plunger tip is aligned with the line that marks 0.05 mL on the syringe.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Inject under controlled aseptic conditions (including surgical hand disinfection and use of sterile gloves, sterile drape, and sterile eyelid speculum [or equivalent]) following adequate anesthesia and administration of a topical broad-spectrum anti-infective agent.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Monitor patients for elevation of IOP immediately following intravitreal injection;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> monitoring may include evaluation of optic nerve head perfusion or tonometry.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> A sterile paracentesis needle should be available, if required.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Use each vial only for treatment of a single eye; discard any unused portion.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If contralateral eye requires treatment, use a new vial; change sterile field, syringe, gloves, drape, eyelid speculum, and filter and injection needles before administering to the other eye.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Intravitreal injection: 2 mg into the affected eye(s) once every 4 weeks for 12 weeks, then 2 mg once every 8 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Additional efficacy not demonstrated following administration every 4 weeks compared with every 8 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Intravitreal injection: 2 mg into the affected eye(s) once every 4 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Intravitreal injection: 2 mg into the affected eye(s) once every 4 weeks for the first 5 injections, followed by 2 mg once every 8 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Additional efficacy not demonstrated following administration every 4 weeks compared with every 8 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Intravitreal injection: 2 mg into the affected eye(s) once every 4 weeks for the first 5 injections, followed by 2 mg once every 8 weeks.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Hypersensitivity reactions reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Contraindications under Cautions.)

Intravitreal injections, including with aflibercept, associated with endophthalmitis and retinal detachment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Always use proper aseptic injection technique.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If signs or symptoms of endophthalmitis or retinal detachment (e.g., redness, sensitivity to light, pain, changes in vision [including blurred vision]) occur, manage appropriately.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Acute increases in IOP observed within 60 minutes of intravitreal injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Sustained increases in IOP also observed following repeated intravitreal injections of VEGF antagonists.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Monitor IOP and perfusion of optic nerve head and manage appropriately.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Potential risk of arterial thromboembolic events following intravitreal injection of VEGF antagonists, including aflibercept.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Incidence of immunoreactivity to aflibercept was similar before and after 24–100 weeks of therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> No differences in efficacy or safety between patients with or without immunoreactivity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Aflibercept Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Following intravitreal injection, mean peak plasma concentration attained in 1–3 days; peak plasma concentration estimated to be >100-fold lower than concentration required to half-maximally bind systemic VEGF.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Free aflibercept undetectable in plasma 2 weeks after intravitreal injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No accumulation observed following repeated intravitreal injections (i.e., once every 4 weeks).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Following intravitreal injection every 4 or 8 weeks, no differences in free plasma aflibercept concentrations observed between patients with mild, moderate, or severe renal impairment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Not known whether aflibercept is distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Expected to undergo elimination through both target-mediated disposition via binding to free endogenous VEGF and via proteolysis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Approximately 5–6 days following IV administration of doses of 2–4 mg/kg.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Stability

Storage

2–8°C.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not freeze; protect from light.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Store in original carton until use.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Acts as a soluble decoy receptor that binds to VEGF-A and PlGF and inhibits their biologic activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

  • VEGF-A induces neovascularization (angiogenesis) and increases vascular permeability,<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> which appear to play a role in the pathogenesis and progression of neovascular (wet) age-related macular degeneration, macular edema following retinal vein occlusion, diabetic macular edema, and diabetic retinopathy.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>8</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>25</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>27</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>28</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>29</sup></body></html>

  • Binding to VEGF-A and PlGF prevents these factors from binding to endogenous VEGF receptors (i.e., VEGFR-1, VEGFR-2), reducing endothelial cell proliferation, angiogenesis, and vascular permeability.

  • Binding affinity of aflibercept for VEGF-A isoforms is higher than that of endogenous receptors; aflibercept blocks VEGF binding and activation of VEGFR-1 and VEGFR-2 even at low concentrations.<html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Possible temporary visual disturbances after intravitreal injection and associated eye examinations; importance of avoiding driving a vehicle or operating machinery until visual function has recovered sufficiently.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Risk of endophthalmitis or retinal detachment; importance of immediately seeking care from an ophthalmologist if change in vision occurs or if the treated eye becomes red, sensitive to light, or painful.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
