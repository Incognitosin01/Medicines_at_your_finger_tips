Introduction

Antimigraine agent; recombinant fully human IgG2 monoclonal antibody that inhibits calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) receptor function.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>8</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html>

Uses for Erenumab-aooe

Preventive Treatment of Migraine

Preventive treatment of migraine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html>

Substantially reduces monthly migraine days and acute antimigraine agent-use days in patients with episodic or chronic migraine (with or without aura) when compared with placebo.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

The American Headache Society (AHS) states that erenumab and other CGRP antagonists offer a number of advantages over some oral migraine preventive therapies, including no need for dosage escalation, rapid onset of therapeutic activity, demonstrated efficacy in patients after prior preventive treatments have failed as well as in those concurrently receiving oral preventive treatments, minimal risk of adverse drug interactions, and favorable overall tolerability profiles.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> However, because of the relatively high cost of CGRP antagonists, AHS recommends that CGRP antagonists be considered for use only in patients who are unable to tolerate oral preventive therapies and/or who have an inadequate response to a 6-week trial of ≥2 oral preventive therapies (e.g., topiramate, valproate, β-adrenergic blocking agents, tricyclic antidepressants, selective serotonin- and norepinephrine-reuptake inhibitors).<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Erenumab-aooe Dosage and Administration

Administration

Administer by sub-Q injection only.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Commercially available in single-dose prefilled auto-injectors (i.e., injection pens) and single-dose prefilled syringes, which both contain 70 or 140 mg of the drug<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html> and are intended for patient self-administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Some packaging components contain dry natural rubber, which may cause allergic reactions in individuals sensitive to latex.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Latex Sensitivity under Cautions.)

Administer by sub-Q injection into the abdomen, thigh, or outer area of upper arm; avoid injections within 2 inches of the navel.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Use upper arm only if someone other than the patient (e.g., caregiver, healthcare professional) is administering the injection.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Avoid injection into areas where the skin is tender, bruised, erythematous, or indurated.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prior to use, provide patients and/or caregivers with proper training on how to prepare and administer erenumab-aooe sub-Q using the prefilled auto-injectors or syringes, including aseptic technique.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consult manufacturer's labeling for detailed instructions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prior to sub-Q administration, remove prefilled auto-injector or syringe from carton and allow to sit at room temperature for ≥30 minutes; avoid exposure to direct sunlight.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not warm auto-injectors and syringes using a heat source (e.g., microwave, hot water).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Do not use if the solution is cloudy or discolored or contains flakes or particles.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Prefilled auto-injectors and syringes are intended for single-use only; discard any unused portions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

70 mg once monthly.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May consider 140 mg once monthly in some patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Gradual dosage titration not necessary; may initiate therapy with either the 70- or 140-mg dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

If a dose is missed, administer the missed dose as soon as possible.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> May then schedule subsequent doses once monthly from the date of the last administered dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

When initiating therapy with erenumab or another CGRP antagonist in patients already receiving a preventive treatment for migraine, AHS recommends adding the CGRP antagonist to the existing antimigraine regimen and avoiding making other changes until the clinical efficacy of the CGRP antagonist is determined.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> The risk of adverse drug interactions with erenumab-aooe appears minimal (see Interactions).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Patients who respond to erenumab therapy usually respond following the first 3 monthly doses.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html> Therefore, AHS recommends assessing clinical efficacy after 3 months of treatment; continue therapy only if treatment benefits have been observed by that time.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Erenumab-aooe

Contraindications

  • Serious hypersensitivity to erenumab-aooe or any excipient in the formulation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Sensitivity Reactions under Cautions.)

Warnings/Precautions

Hypersensitivity reactions, including rash, angioedema, and anaphylaxis, reported during postmarketing experience.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Most hypersensitivity reactions were not serious and occurred within hours of administration; however, some reactions occurred >1 week following administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

If a serious or severe hypersensitivity reaction occurs, discontinue administration of the drug and initiate appropriate therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Contraindications under Cautions and see also Advice to Patients.)

Some packaging components (e.g., needle shield within cap of the prefilled auto-injector, needle cap of the prefilled syringe) of erenumab-aooe contain natural rubber (a latex derivative),<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> which may cause allergic reactions in individuals sensitive to latex.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html>

Some individuals may be hypersensitive to natural latex proteins;<html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>15</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html> rarely, hypersensitivity reactions to natural latex proteins have been fatal.<html><head></head><body><sup>14</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html>

Potential for immunogenicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Antibodies to erenumab, including neutralizing antibodies to the drug, reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Available data do not demonstrate an effect of anti-erenumab antibody development on efficacy or safety of the drug; however, data are too limited to make definitive conclusions.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

No adequate data on the developmental risk associated with use of erenumab in pregnant women.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> No adverse effects on offspring observed when pregnant monkeys received the drug sub-Q at dosages resulting in systemic exposures approximately 20 times the exposure from the maximum recommended human dosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Estimated rates of major birth defects and miscarriage among deliveries to women with migraine (2.2–2.9 and 17%, respectively) are similar to rates reported in women without migraine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Possible increased risk of preeclampsia during pregnancy in women with migraine.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether distributed into human milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Effects on the breast-fed infant or on milk production also unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consider developmental and health benefits of breast-feeding, mother's clinical need for erenumab, and potential adverse effects on the breast-fed infant from the drug or underlying maternal condition.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Manufacturer states that safety and efficacy not established in pediatric patients <18 years of age.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Pending further clinical experience with CGRP antagonists in pediatric patients, the Pediatric and Adolescent Headache special interest group of the AHS recommends limiting use of CGRP antagonists (e.g., erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab) mainly to postpubertal adolescents† with relatively frequent migraines (i.e., ≥8 headache days per month) who have moderate to severe disability associated with migraine (e.g., PedMIDAS score ≥30) and in whom ≥2 preventive therapies have failed.<html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html> For younger pediatric patients† with severe chronic migraine that is refractory to multiple preventive therapies, consider CGRP antagonists only in carefully selected patients with close monitoring (e.g., pubertal status, bone health, linear growth, weight, body mass index [BMI], infectious complications).<html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html>

Insufficient experience in patients ≥65 years of age to determine whether geriatric patients respond differently than younger adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Select dosage with caution, usually starting at the low end of the dosage range.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consider the greater frequency of decreased hepatic, renal, and/or cardiac function and of concomitant disease and drug therapy when selecting dosage in geriatric patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Hepatic impairment not expected to affect pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Special Populations under Pharmacokinetics.)

Renal impairment not expected to affect pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> (See Special Populations under Pharmacokinetics.)

Erenumab-aooe Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Following single-dose, sub-Q administration, estimated absolute bioavailability is 82%.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Peak concentrations achieved in approximately 6 days following a single sub-Q dose.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Steady-state trough concentrations approached in 3 months following once-monthly sub-Q administration; systemic accumulation is less than twofold.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Exhibits nonlinear pharmacokinetics due to binding to the CGRP receptor;<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html> however, at clinically relevant dosages, pharmacokinetics following once-monthly sub-Q administration are predominantly linear because of saturation of binding to the CGRP receptor.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html>

Antimigraine effects usually evident following 1–3 monthly sub-Q injections in responding patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>12</sup></body></html>

Not formally studied in renal or hepatic impairment; renal or hepatic impairment not expected to affect pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Population pharmacokinetic analysis indicates that pharmacokinetics not affected by mild or moderate renal impairment.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Not studied in patients with severe renal impairment (estimated GFR <30 mL/minute per 1.73 m2).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Age, sex, race, or subtype of migraine spectrum (e.g., episodic or chronic migraine) does not affect pharmacokinetics.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Due to large molecule size, unlikely to cross the blood-brain barrier.<html><head></head><body><sup>11</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>13</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>18</sup></body></html>

Elimination

At low concentrations, predominantly eliminated through saturable binding to the CGRP receptor; at higher concentrations, predominantly eliminated through a nonspecific, nonsaturable proteolytic pathway.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
