Uses for ceftazidime and avibactam

Ceftazidime and avibactam combination injection is used alone or together with other medicines (eg, metronidazole) to treat complicated infections in many different parts of the body (eg, stomach, kidneys). It is also used to treat hospital-acquired bacterial pneumonia and ventilator-associated bacterial pneumonia (HABP/VABP).

Ceftazidime and avibactam combination injection belongs to the class of medicines known as cephalosporin antibiotics. It works by killing bacteria or preventing their growth. However, ceftazidime and avibactam will not work for colds, flu, or other virus infections.

Ceftazidime and avibactam is to be given only by or under the direct supervision of your doctor.

Before using ceftazidime and avibactam

In deciding to use a medicine, the risks of taking the medicine must be weighed against the good it will do. This is a decision you and your doctor will make. For ceftazidime and avibactam, the following should be considered:

Allergies

Tell your doctor if you have ever had any unusual or allergic reaction to ceftazidime and avibactam or any other medicines. Also tell your health care professional if you have any other types of allergies, such as to foods, dyes, preservatives, or animals. For non-prescription products, read the label or package ingredients carefully.

Pediatric

Appropriate studies have not been performed on the relationship of age to the effects of ceftazidime and avibactam combination injection in children younger than 3 months of age to treat infections in the stomach or kidneys or in children to treat hospital-acquired bacterial pneumonia and ventilator-associated bacterial pneumonia. Safety and efficacy have not been established.

Geriatric

Appropriate studies performed to date have not demonstrated geriatric-specific problems that would limit the usefulness of ceftazidime and avibactam combination injection in the elderly. However, elderly patients are more likely to have age-related kidney problems, which may require caution and an adjustment in the dose for patients receiving ceftazidime and avibactam.

Breastfeeding

There are no adequate studies in women for determining infant risk when using this medication during breastfeeding. Weigh the potential benefits against the potential risks before taking this medication while breastfeeding.

In these cases, your doctor may want to change the dose, or other precautions may be necessary. Interactions with food/tobacco/alcohol

Certain medicines should not be used at or around the time of eating food or eating certain types of food since interactions may occur. Using alcohol or tobacco with certain medicines may also cause interactions to occur. Discuss with your healthcare professional the use of your medicine with food, alcohol, or tobacco.

Other medical problems

The presence of other medical problems may affect the use of ceftazidime and avibactam. Make sure you tell your doctor if you have any other medical problems, especially:

  • Allergy to penicillin, history of—May increase risk of an allergic reaction to occur again.
  • Brain disease (eg, encephalopathy) or
  • Diarrhea, severe, history of or
  • Myoclonus (muscle twitching or jerking) or
  • Seizures—Use with caution. May make these conditions worse.
  • Kidney disease—Use with caution. The effects may be increased because of slower removal of the medicine from the body.

Proper use of ceftazidime and avibactam

A nurse or other trained health professional will give you ceftazidime and avibactam in a hospital. It is given through a needle placed into one of your veins. Ceftazidime and avibactam must be given slowly, so the needle will have to stay in place for at least 2 hours.

Precautions while using ceftazidime and avibactam

Your doctor will check your or your child's progress closely while receiving ceftazidime and avibactam. This will allow your doctor to see if it is working properly and to decide if you should continue to receive it and to check for unwanted effects.

Ceftazidime and avibactam may cause serious allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, which can be life-threatening and require immediate medical attention. Tell your doctor right away if you or your child have a rash, itching, trouble breathing, trouble swallowing, or any swelling of your hands, face, or mouth while receiving ceftazidime and avibactam.

Serious skin reactions can occur with ceftazidime and avibactam. Check with your doctor right away if you or your child have blistering, peeling, or loosening of the skin, red skin lesions, severe acne or skin rash, sores or ulcers on the skin, or fever or chills while receiving ceftazidime and avibactam.

Ceftazidime and avibactam may cause diarrhea, and in some cases it can be severe. It may occur 2 months or more after you or your child stop receiving ceftazidime and avibactam. Do not take any medicine to treat diarrhea without first checking with your doctor. If you have any questions or if mild diarrhea continues or gets worse, check with your doctor.

Check with your doctor right away if you have any of the following symptoms while receiving ceftazidime and avibactam: confusion, loss of consciousness, jerking or twitching of the muscles, seizures, seeing, hearing, or feeling things that are not there, or severe sleepiness.

Before you have any medical tests, tell the medical doctor in charge that you are receiving ceftazidime and avibactam. The results of some tests may be affected by ceftazidime and avibactam.

Do not take other medicines unless they have been discussed with your doctor. This includes prescription or nonprescription (over-the-counter [OTC]) medicines and herbal or vitamin supplements.
