Introduction

Biosynthetic (recombinant DNA origin) preparation of blood coagulation factor VIII covalently linked to the Fc domain of human IgG1.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>7</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>9</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html>

Uses for Antihemophilic Factor (recombinant), Fc Fusion Protein

Hemophilia A

On-demand treatment and control of bleeding episodes in patients with hemophilia A (congenital factor VIII deficiency; classic hemophilia).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

Maintenance of hemostasis in patients with hemophilia A undergoing surgery (i.e., perioperative management of bleeding).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html>

Routine prophylaxis (i.e., administration at regular intervals) to reduce frequency of bleeding events.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> Such prophylaxis considered the current standard of care for patients with hemophilia A.<html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>218</sup></body></html> Decreases frequency of spontaneous musculoskeletal bleeding, preserves joint function, and improves quality of life.<html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>224</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>231</sup></body></html>

Designated an orphan drug by FDA for treatment of hemophilia A.<html><head></head><body><sup>3</sup></body></html>

Half-life of antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein is longer than that of conventional preparations of recombinant antihemophilic factor; may allow for less frequent dosing and possibly improve patient compliance with prophylactic regimens.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>4</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>8</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>10</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>16</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html>

Several antihemophilic factor concentrates are currently available in the US, including a variety of recombinant and plasma-derived preparations; the Medical and Scientific Advisory Council (MASAC) of the National Hemophilia Foundation recommends preferential use of recombinant antihemophilic factor preparations because of their potentially superior safety profile with respect to pathogen transmission.<html><head></head><body><sup>218</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>246</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>247</sup></body></html> Other experts (e.g., World Federation of Hemophilia) state that choice of preparation should be determined by local criteria.<html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> When selecting an appropriate antihemophilic factor preparation, consider characteristics of each clotting factor concentrate, individual patient variables, patient/provider preference, and emerging data.<html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>243</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>246</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>247</sup></body></html>

Not indicated for the treatment of von Willebrand disease.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Dosage

Dosage and potency expressed in terms of international units (IU, units) of antihemophilic factor activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Potency is determined by a chromogenic substrate assay; however, both chromogenic assays and one-stage clotting assays are routinely used in US clinical laboratories for measurement of plasma factor VIII activity.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> In general, administration of 1 unit/kg of antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein increases circulating factor VIII levels by approximately 2 units/dL (2%).<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Individualize dosage and duration of therapy based on severity of factor VIII deficiency, location and extent of bleeding, and patient's clinical and pharmacokinetic (e.g., in-vivo recovery, half-life) response.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> Estimate dose required to achieve a particular percentage increase in plasma factor VIII with the following formula:

Dose required (in units) = body weight (in kg) × 0.5 × desired factor VIII increase (in % of normal)

Determine desired factor VIII level by the clinical situation and severity of bleeding.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> For recommendations on target factor VIII levels for a given clinical situation, see the specific dosage sections for various types of uses below. Minor or moderate bleeding (e.g., joint, superficial muscle [except iliopsoas] without neurovascular compromise, deep laceration and renal, superficial soft tissue, mucous membranes): Initial dose of 20–30 units/kg to achieve a factor VIII level of at least 40–60% of normal; repeat dose every 12–24 hours for patients <6 years of age or every 24–48 hours for patients ≥6 years of age until bleeding resolves.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Cautions for Antihemophilic Factor (recombinant), Fc Fusion Protein

Contraindications

  • Life-threatening hypersensitivity reactions (e.g., anaphylaxis) to antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein or other components of the preparation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Risk for development of neutralizing antibodies (inhibitors) to factor VIII following treatment with any antihemophilic factor preparation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>255</sup></body></html> Reported to occur in approximately 20–30% of patients with severe hemophilia A and 5–10% of those with mild to moderate disease.<html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html>

Monitor patients for development of inhibitors using appropriate clinical observation and laboratory tests.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> (See Laboratory Monitoring under Cautions.) Suspect presence of inhibitors if expected factor VIII levels not achieved or bleeding not controlled with recommended dose, particularly in those who previously achieved a response.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html>

Monitor factor VIII levels using a validated test (e.g., one-stage clotting assay) to guide dosing and assess therapeutic response.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> Important to achieve and maintain adequate levels of factor VIII for effective hemostatic control during an acute bleeding episode or during surgery.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>6</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html>

Monitor for development of inhibitors.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Perform appropriate laboratory test (i.e., Bethesda assay) to confirm presence of inhibitors.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>215</sup></body></html> (See Neutralizing Antibodies to Factor VIII under Cautions.)

Potential risk of hypersensitivity reactions, including anaphylaxis.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> If a hypersensitivity reaction occurs, discontinue drug immediately and initiate appropriate therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether drug can cause fetal harm or affect reproductive capacity; use during pregnancy only when clearly needed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Not known whether distributed into human milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Consider known benefits of breast-feeding; mother's clinical need for antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein; and any potential adverse effects of the drug or disease on infant.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Safety and efficacy evaluated in previously treated adolescents 12–18 years of age in principal efficacy study.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

In a separate pediatric study evaluating patients <12 years of age, antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein half-life was shorter and body weight-adjusted clearance was substantially higher in children 1–5 years of age than in older pediatric patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>256</sup></body></html>

Insufficient experience in patients ≥65 years of age to determine whether geriatric patients respond differently than younger patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Antihemophilic Factor (recombinant), Fc Fusion Protein Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

In adults, mean recovery of factor VIII is approximately 2.26 units/dL for each 1 unit/kg of antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein administered.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Mean incremental recoveries in pediatric patients 1–5, 6–11, and 12–17 years of age are 1.92, 2.44, and 1.85 units/dL, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Compared with conventional preparations of recombinant antihemophilic factor, exhibits increased time after dosing to 1% factor VIII activity suggesting potentially longer therapeutic duration.<html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Not known whether antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein is distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Following administration of a single 50-unit/kg dose given as a 10-minute IV injection in adults, terminal half-life was approximately 19.7 hours and time to reach 1% factor VIII activity was 5.1 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Compared with adults, pediatric patients appear to have a shorter half-life; in one study, half-life was approximately 12.7, 14.9, or 16.4 hours in pediatric patients 1–5, 6–11, or 12–17 years of age, respectively.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

In pediatric patients 1–5 years of age, clearance is substantially increased compared with older pediatric patients and adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>256</sup></body></html>

Compared with conventional preparations of recombinant antihemophilic factor, half-life of antihemophilic factor (recombinant), Fc fusion protein is prolonged and clearance reduced.<html><head></head><body><sup>5</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>8</sup></body></html>
