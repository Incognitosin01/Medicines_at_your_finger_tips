Uses for Caffeine/Caffeine and Sodium Benzoate

CNS Stimulation

Used orally as an aid in staying awake and to restore mental alertness in fatigued patients.<html><head></head><body><sup>117</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>118</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Used in combination with antihistamines to overcome the sedative properties of antihistamines; however, efficacy and dosage required not adequately established.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Caffeine and sodium benzoate injection has been used in conjunction with supportive measures to treat respiratory depression associated with overdosage of CNS depressant drugs (e.g., opiate analgesics, alcohol) and with electric shock.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> However, most authorities believe caffeine and other analeptics should not be used in these conditions and recommend other supportive therapy because of caffeine’s questionable benefit and transient action.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Apnea of Prematurity

Short-term (10–12 days) treatment (as oral or IV caffeine citrate) of apnea of prematurity in neonates who are 28 to <33 weeks of gestational age (designated an orphan drug by FDA for this use).<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Use only after other causes of apnea (e.g., CNS disorders, primary lung disease, anemia, sepsis, metabolic disturbances, cardiovascular abnormalities, obstructive apnea) have been ruled out or treated appropriately.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Headache

Used in combination with ergotamine to prevent or abort vascular headaches (e.g., migraine and cluster headaches).<html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>e</sup></body></html> However, there is conflicting evidence regarding efficacy of this combination in the treatment of acute migraine attacks.<html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html>

Used orally alone and in combination with analgesics (e.g., acetaminophen, aspirin) for treatment of headache,<html><head></head><body><sup>121</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>d</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>h</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>l</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>o</sup></body></html> including migraine attacks.<html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>g</sup></body></html>

Some evidence that analgesic-caffeine combinations may produce slightly more analgesia than analgesic agents alone and may have beneficial effect on mood; however, these results have not always been reproducible in well-controlled studies.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Additional studies needed to determine the role, if any, of caffeine as an analgesic adjuvant.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Some experts state that the combination of acetaminophen, aspirin, and caffeine is a reasonable first-line therapy for mild to moderate migraine attacks or for severe migraine attacks that previously have responded to NSAIAs or nonopiate analgesics.<html><head></head><body><sup>116</sup></body></html>

Caffeine and sodium benzoate injection has been used for the symptomatic relief of headache following spinal puncture†.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Other Uses

Safety and efficacy of caffeine citrate in the prevention of sudden infant death syndrome† (SIDS) or prior to extubation in mechanically ventilated infants† not established.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Has been used orally alone and in combination with other drugs (e.g., analgesics, diuretics) to relieve tension, fatigue, and fluid retention associated with menstruation.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Usefulness is questionable because caffeine’s diuretic activity in patients with fluid retention is minimal.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Use caffeine citrate with caution in premature neonates with impaired renal or hepatic function, cardiovascular disease, or seizure disorders (see Cardiac Effects and also Nervous System Effects, under Cautions; also see Special Populations under Dosage and Administration).<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Consider possible need for monitoring serum caffeine concentrations (see General under Dosage and Administration).<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Monitor blood glucose concentrations periodically; hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia reported in neonates.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Long-term follow-up studies have not shown caffeine administration in premature neonates to adversely affect neurologic development or growth.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Pharmacokinetics of caffeine citrate not evaluated in premature neonates with hepatic impairment; use with caution.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> (See Hepatic Impairment under Dosage and Administration.)

Pharmacokinetics of caffeine citrate not evaluated in premature neonates with renal impairment; use with caution.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> (See Renal Impairment under Dosage and Administration.)

Caffeine/Caffeine and Sodium Benzoate Pharmacokinetics

Absorption

Caffeine and caffeine citrate are well absorbed following oral administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Absorption following oral administration may be more rapid than that following IM injection of caffeine and sodium benzoate.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Absolute bioavailability of orally administered caffeine in preterm neonates not fully determined.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Following oral administration of 100 mg of caffeine (as coffee), peak plasma concentrations reached after 50–75 minutes.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Following oral administration of 10 mg/kg of caffeine to preterm neonates, mean time to peak plasma concentration was 0.5–2 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Feeding formula does not affect time to peak plasma concentration in infants.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Serum caffeine concentrations >50 mcg/mL associated with serious toxicity.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Distribution

Rapidly distributed into body tissues; readily crosses the blood-brain barrier.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html>

Concentrations in the CSF of preterm neonates approximates plasma concentration.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Readily crosses the placenta and is distributed into milk.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Mean volume of distribution in infants is slightly larger than that in adults (0.6 L/kg).<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Approximately 17–36% in adults; data not available for neonates or infants.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html>

Elimination

Metabolized in the liver, principally via CYP1A2, to 1-methyluric acid, 1-methylxanthine, and 7-methylxanthine.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> May induce own metabolism; clinical importance of autoinduction is unknown.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Neonates: Limited hepatic metabolism due to immature hepatic enzyme systems.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Interconversion between caffeine and theophylline reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> (See Specific Drugs and Laboratory Tests under Interactions.)

Metabolism of caffeine by 9 months of age approximates that seen in adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Adults: Excreted principally in urine as metabolites (<1% recovered in urine as unchanged drug).<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Neonates: Excreted principally in urine as unchanged drug (approximately 86%).<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Mean fraction excreted unchanged in urine in infants is inversely related to gestational/postconceptional age;<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> by 9 months of age, fraction excreted as unchanged drug approximates that seen in adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Adults: 3–5 hours.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

Neonates: Approximately 3–4 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Elimination slower in young infants than in adults because of immature hepatic and/or renal function.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> Mean half-life in infants is inversely related to gestational/postconceptional age;<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> by 9 months of age, half-life approximates that seen in adults.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html>

Actions

  • Competitively inhibits phosphodiesterase, the enzyme that degrades cyclic 3′,5′-adenosine monophosphate (AMP), increasing levels of intracellular cyclic AMP.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Stimulates all levels of the CNS.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html> Stimulates the cerebral cortex and produces a more rapid and clearer thought flow, wakefulness or arousal in fatigued patients; also improves psychomotor coordination.<html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Stimulates medullary vagal, vasomotor, and respiratory centers in slightly larger doses, promoting bradycardia, vasoconstriction, and increased respiratory rate.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Produces a positive inotropic effect on the myocardium and a positive chronotropic effect at the SA node, causing transient increases in heart rate, force of contraction, cardiac output, and heart work.<html><head></head><body><sup>115</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Stimulates voluntary skeletal muscle, increasing the force of contraction and decreasing muscular fatigue.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Stimulates gastric acid secretion from parietal cells.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>

  • Increases renal blood flow and GFR; decreases proximal tubular reabsorption of sodium and water, resulting in mild diuresis.<html><head></head><body><sup>120</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>a</sup></body></html>
