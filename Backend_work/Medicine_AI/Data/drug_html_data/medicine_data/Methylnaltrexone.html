Uses for Methylnaltrexone

Opiate-induced Constipation

Management of opiate-induced constipation in patients with advanced illness or pain caused by active cancer who require opiate dosage escalation for palliative care.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>2</sup></body></html>

Management of opiate-induced constipation in patients with chronic non-cancer-related pain, including those with chronic pain related to prior cancer or its treatment who do not require frequent (e.g., weekly) increases in opiate dosage.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>17</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>18</sup></body></html>

Warnings/Precautions

Symptoms consistent with opiate withdrawal (e.g., hyperhidrosis, chills, diarrhea, abdominal pain, anxiety, yawning) reported.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Interactions for Methylnaltrexone

Methylnaltrexone does not substantially inhibit or induce CYP isoenzymes 1A2, 2A6, 2B6, 2C9, 2C19, or 3A4 in vitro; also does not induce CYP2E1 in vitro.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Major metabolites (methylnaltrexone sulfate, methyl-6α-naltrexol, methyl-6β-naltrexol) do not inhibit CYP isoenzymes 1A2, 2B6, 2C8, 2C9, 2C19, 2D6, or 3A4 in vitro; also do not induce CYP isoenzymes 1A2, 2B6, or 3A4 in vitro.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Methylnaltrexone and its major metabolites are not likely to cause clinically important interactions via inhibition of P-glycoprotein (P-gp), breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP), multidrug resistance protein 2 (MRP2), organic anion transport protein (OATP) 1B1 or 1B3, organic cation transporter (OCT) 1 or 2, organic anion transporter (OAT) 1 or 3, or multidrug and toxin extrusion transporter (MATE) 1 or 2K.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Methylnaltrexone, methyl-6α-naltrexol, and methyl-6β-naltrexol are substrates of OCT1, OCT2, MATE1, and MATE2K in vitro.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Methyl-6α-naltrexol also is a substrate of BCRP, and methylnaltrexone sulfate is a substrate of MATE2K and a potential substrate of BCRP.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Neither methylnaltrexone nor methyl-6β-naltrexol is a substrate of BCRP.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Methylnaltrexone sulfate is not a substrate of OCT1, OCT2, or MATE1.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Neither methylnaltrexone nor its 3 major metabolites are substrates of P-gp, MRP2, OATP1B1, OATP1B3, OAT1, or OAT3.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

Advice to Patients

  • Importance of patient and/or caregiver reading the manufacturer’s patient information.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of discontinuing methylnaltrexone therapy following cessation of opiate analgesic therapy.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise patients to inform their clinician if opiate therapy for chronic non-cancer-related pain is altered.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • For self-administration of methylnaltrexone injection, instruct patient and/or caregiver regarding proper dosage and administration, including the use of aseptic technique and proper disposal of needles and syringes.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients with advanced illness or their caregivers to inject one dose of methylnaltrexone every other day as needed and to never administer more than one dose in a 24-hour period.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients with chronic non-cancer-related pain to inject one dose of methylnaltrexone daily or to take methylnaltrexone tablets with water on an empty stomach at least 30 minutes before the first meal of the day.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients that close proximity to a toilet is advised after drug administration.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Advise patients with chronic non-cancer-related pain to discontinue all maintenance laxative therapy prior to initiation of methylnaltrexone; laxatives may be used as needed if response to methylnaltrexone is suboptimal after 3 days.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Possible risk of GI perforation.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Importance of discontinuing methylnaltrexone and promptly seeking medical attention if unusually severe, persistent, or worsening abdominal pain occurs.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Potential for symptoms consistent with opiate withdrawal (e.g., sweating, chills, diarrhea, abdominal pain, anxiety, yawning) to occur.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of discontinuing methylnaltrexone and informing clinician if severe or persistent diarrhea occurs.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of women informing clinicians if they are or plan to become pregnant or plan to breast-feed.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise women that methylnaltrexone use during pregnancy may precipitate fetal opiate withdrawal because the fetal blood-brain barrier is immature.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html> Advise women not to breast-feed while receiving the drug because of the potential for opiate withdrawal in nursing infants.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>

  • Importance of informing clinicians of existing or contemplated concomitant therapy, including prescription and OTC drugs.<html><head></head><body><sup>1</sup></body></html>
