Uses for Rabies Vaccine

Prevention of Rabies

Prevention of rabies in children, adolescents, and adults exposed to or at increased risk of exposure to rabies disease or virus.<html><head></head><body><sup>208</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>213</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>234</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html>

Rabies is a viral infection transmitted by saliva of infected mammals, most commonly wild, terrestrial carnivores (e.g., skunks, raccoons, foxes, coyotes) or bats.<html><head></head><body><sup>213</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> In the US, the greatest risk for naturally acquired rabies is from contact with and bites from insectivorous bats.<html><head></head><body><sup>222</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>242</sup></body></html> Following exposure and infection, rabies virus usually moves along a neural pathway and enters the CNS.<html><head></head><body><sup>234</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> After entrance into the CNS, the virus is unlikely to be affected by antirabies antibodies and encephalomyelitis usually develops and almost always is fatal.<html><head></head><body><sup>234</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> In the US, approximately 16,000–39,000 individuals receive rabies postexposure prophylaxis each year.<html><head></head><body><sup>234</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html> Although there were 27 rabies cases reported in the US during 2000–2008,<html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html> these individuals evidently did not receive rabies postexposure prophylaxis.<html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html> Rabies prevention and control strategies and elimination of canine rabies virus variants and enzootic transmission among dogs have lowered the number of rabies cases in the US to an average of 1–2 per year.<html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html> However, worldwide, rabies is much more common and at least 55,000 rabies-related deaths occur each year.<html><head></head><body><sup>249</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html>

USPHS Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) and AAP recommend preexposure vaccination with rabies vaccine (series of 3 doses with booster doses when indicated) in children, adolescents, and adults who are or will be at increased risk of exposure to the virus.<html><head></head><body><sup>213</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> (See Preexposure Vaccination Against Rabies in High-risk Groups under Uses.)

Postexposure prophylaxis with a regimen that includes local wound treatment, rabies vaccine (series of 4 or 5 doses), and a single dose of rabies immune globulin (RIG) is recommend for previously unvaccinated children, adolescents, and adults following potential rabies exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>213</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> Postexposure prophylaxis with a regimen that includes local wound treatment and a series of 2 booster doses of rabies vaccine (without RIG) is recommended for previously vaccinated children, adolescents, and adults following potential rabies exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>213</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> (See Postexposure Prophylaxis of Rabies under Uses.)

Preexposure Vaccination Against Rabies in High-risk Groups

Preexposure vaccination in children, adolescents, and adults who are or will be at risk of exposure to rabies virus.<html><head></head><body><sup>208</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>213</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>234</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html>

Preexposure vaccination does not eliminate the need for prompt postexposure prophylaxis if an exposure to rabies occurs.<html><head></head><body><sup>208</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>213</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>234</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>250</sup></body></html> (See Postexposure Prophylaxis of Rabies under Uses.)

Need for rabies preexposure vaccination depends on the nature of risk and associated level of potential exposure.<html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> Consider preexposure vaccination for individuals whose risk of rabies exposure is greater than that of the general population (e.g., veterinarians and their staff, animal-control and wildlife workers, field biologists, spelunkers, missionaries, rabies researchers, certain laboratory workers).<html><head></head><body><sup>212</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> Also consider preexposure vaccination for individuals whose activities bring them into frequent contact with rabies virus or potentially rabid bats, raccoons, skunks, cats, dogs, or other species at risk for having rabies.<html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> (For ACIP definitions of risk categories and recommendations regarding preexposure vaccination for each category, see Table 1.)

Travelers to areas where rabies is endemic may be at risk, especially if they are likely to come in contact with animals in areas where dog or other animal rabies is enzootic and immediate access to appropriate medical care (including rabies vaccine and RIG) is unlikely.<html><head></head><body><sup>212</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>236</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>237</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>249</sup></body></html> Canine rabies remains highly endemic in certain areas of the world (e.g., parts of Africa, Asia, Central and South America).<html><head></head><body><sup>212</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>237</sup></body></html> CDC recommends preexposure vaccination based on local incidence of rabies in the country to be visited, availability of appropriate agents for rabies postexposure prophylaxis in that country, and intended activity and duration of stay.<html><head></head><body><sup>212</sup></body></html>

Minimum acceptable antibody titer is complete virus neutralization at a 1:5 serum dilution by the rapid fluorescent focus inhibition test (RFFIT). Give booster dose of rabies vaccine if titer falls below this level.

  • Rabies vaccine is a sterile, freeze-dried vaccine containing inactivated rabies virus antigen (≥2.5 international units/mL).<html><head></head><body><sup>208</sup></body></html> <html><head></head><body><sup>234</sup></body></html>
